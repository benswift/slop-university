> An alarm that gets acknowledged and a fault that gets fixed turned out, once
> we actually timed both, to be two different promises.
>
> --- Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and Convenor, Capability Sprints, School
> of Continuous Improvement

A new Slop University paper has followed a rental-property portfolio's
self-testing smoke alarms for sixteen months, from the moment each weekly
fault first reaches a managing agent's dashboard through to the day a
technician actually attends. The School of Continuous Improvement finds the
work sharpens a distinction the School's broader evaluation-ecosystem agenda
keeps circling back to: that noticing a problem and doing something about it
are two separate achievements, and a system built to measure the first can
make the second look better than it is.

Across 214 properties and 386 logged faults, the paper's authors found
acknowledgment of a fault swift and almost identical across fault types,
while the wait for an actual technician stretched from a median of four days
for a full alarm failure to sixty-one days for the portfolio's most common
fault, a low-battery flag. A follow-up test of a repeated reminder message,
designed to close that gap, moved the outcome by less than two percentage
points.

"We built the reminder because it was the lever closest to hand, not because
we had reason to think it was the right one," said Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior
Research Fellow and Convenor of the Impact Pathway Atlas. "Knowing that now is
arguably the more useful result."

The portfolio's existing annual paper compliance certificate continues
alongside the new self-test log, unmodified by the deployment. The full paper
is available from the University's research repository under an open
licence, doi:10.5555/slop.vh3kpz.
