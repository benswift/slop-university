A flagged parking permit at Slop University isn't retired outright: staff
have a two-week grace window to reinstate it, and a mandatory log asking
them to record why. A new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities
finds that when staff use that window, they far more often write down that
the priority-tier score changed its own mind than that they overruled it
themselves --- and an independent recomputation of the score suggests the
reverse is usually closer to the truth.

> An officer who overrules an instrument on their own judgement and one
> who's simply following where it's already moved shouldn't produce the
> same paperwork. Ours mostly did.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Researchers reconstructed the priority-tier score's published formula
against each of 227 permits manually reinstated across four allocation
cycles, comparing the office's own logged reason against what the
arithmetic actually showed at the moment of reversal. Reversals logged as a
score revision showed no underlying change in 72% of cases; reversals
logged as a staff override coincided with a real score change in only
about 1 in 10. A parallel check, coded blind to the office's own log, found
the same pattern.

"We didn't set out to catch anyone doing anything wrong," said Dr Bram
Ntuli, Lecturer and Convenor of Improvement Grand Rounds. "We set out to
see whether a form with two boxes on it is actually capable of telling the
two apart. On this evidence, not reliably."

For the School of Emergent Priorities, the finding confirms a running
suspicion: an institution's own paperwork can be selectively honest about
who did what, down to a single officer at a single counter.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ovm5ni.
