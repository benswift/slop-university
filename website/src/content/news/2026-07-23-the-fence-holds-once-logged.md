A shared robot mower does not learn where its own fence line sits. According
to a new Slop University research poster, what it can learn is that somebody
is finally checking.

Lecturer Lindiwe Achterberg, of the School of Emergent Priorities, worked
with Lecturer Dr Solveig Adeyemi of the School of Continuous Improvement to
fit 22 community-garden mower sites with boundary telemetry for eight
months, logging every excursion past a shared mower's virtual fence into a
containment ledger. Fourteen of the gardens' plot-holder committees agreed
to read that ledger before each lease renewal; the remaining eight kept none.

> A mower doesn't know it's being watched. A committee does, and it turns
> out that's the half of the arrangement that was doing the work all along.
>
> --- Lecturer Lindiwe Achterberg, School of Emergent Priorities

At the ledgered sites, mean monthly breaches fell from 13.4 to 3.1 between
January and August; the unledgered sites showed no comparable change, and
committees only tied a non-renewal decision to a mower's breach count where
the full committee could see the ledger. Firmware and fence-geometry updates
over the same window moved nothing.

"We didn't set out to prove the mower faultless," said Dr Adeyemi. "We set
out to ask what actually changes a plot-holder's mind about renewal, and the
honest answer is a ledger somebody in the room has actually opened."

The School of Emergent Priorities adds the finding to its account of what
makes a governance instrument work: not the device it watches, but whoever
keeps agreeing to read it. The non-renewal threshold was pre-registered
before any result was seen.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.1qia9f.
