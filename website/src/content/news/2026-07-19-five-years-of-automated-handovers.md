The School of Continuous Improvement has spent five years asking a question
most organisations never formally get around to: once a system starts making
a call a person used to make, who is keeping score on whether anyone still
trusts it. A new impact report, covering 2021 to 2026, sets out what the
School found across five such handovers, from a transit authority's fare
gates to a cooperative research consortium's retired trust scores.

> Five years in, we are more confident about how to measure a handover than
> about what to do once we've measured it. That is not the conclusion we
> expected to publish, and it is the one the evidence gave us.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab

Compiled from partnerships with a metropolitan transit authority, a
big-four professional services firm, a regional university network, a
Nordic evaluation agency, and a cooperative research consortium, the report
tracks what happened once each partner let an automated system take over a
decision it used to make by hand --- an override at a fare gate, a flag on
a self-checkout screen, a call routed away from a person. In several of the
five areas, staff trust the report measured six months after a handover
still depended heavily on how much warning the system's arrival was given,
and how specific that warning had been.

Dr Solveig Adeyemi, who led the report's call-centre work, said the finding
"complicates the easy version of this story, where more warning is always
better. Warning about the wrong thing turned out to be almost as unhelpful
as no warning at all."

The report's method, in outline, pairs each partner's own measure of trust
or performance against an independently gathered figure the School
collected without reference to the partner's dashboard --- the same
discipline that has anchored the School's evaluation-ecosystem agenda since
its earliest work on the Living Dashboard and the Indicator Commons.

The full impact report is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.4fo82q.
