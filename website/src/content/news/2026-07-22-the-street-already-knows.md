The School of Emergent Priorities built its reputation on an instrument most
of the University associates with its own affairs --- a rolling account of
the priorities approaching before they've quite arrived. Its newest
publication asks what the same instinct finds once it's pointed somewhere
nobody thought to aim it: a wooden box on a corner, a pothole in the road, a
fridge door on a suburban street. _The Street Sees It Coming First_, a new
marketing brochure released today, gathers three studies already on the
University's public record and reads them together as one argument: a
neighbourhood keeps better notice of what's coming than anyone gives it
credit for.

> A street-library box is a very small archive, and archives don't close
> themselves. Someone has to notice a thread has gone quiet before it can be
> called finished.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The three studies span a season of neighbourhood correspondence, a wet
season of council road repairs, and eighteen weeks of a community fridge's
handwritten notes --- each one already carrying its own DOI, open for anyone
minded to check the brochure's gloss against the method underneath it.

"A ranking tells a crew where to start," said Dr Ronja Oyelaran, whose
interview work with road-maintenance crews features in the brochure's second
chapter. "It doesn't know what the crew already knows about a street that's
flooded twice this year, and eventually the crew stops pretending it does."

The School reads the brochure less as new research than as evidence of its
own instincts finding new material to work on, wherever they happen to be
pointed.

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.gxg4qq.
