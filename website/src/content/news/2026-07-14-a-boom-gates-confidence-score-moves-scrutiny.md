Two boom gates sit a few hundred metres apart on Slop University's western
campus, reading the same plates against the same automated confidence score.
Only one shows a security officer that score on a screen. A term-long audit
from the School of Emergent Priorities finds that difference alone moves how
closely officers check the vehicles the system rates most confident ---
without moving, on an independent count, how often those vehicles turn out
to actually belong there.

Researchers reconciled thirteen weeks of console activity across the two
gates against a desk audit of a sample of admitted vehicles, carried out
blind to what had happened at the boom, and against a staff-rotation check
to rule out any one team's habits. Overall intervention barely differed
between the gates. Within the system's highest confidence band, though,
console-linked checks all but stopped at the gate where staff could see the
score --- while the same band's vehicles were no more likely than any
other's to pass the independent audit.

> A programme can keep reporting the same falling number long after that
> number has started meaning something different.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Anouk Mensah, the paper's co-author, said the finding fits a pattern the
School keeps meeting elsewhere in its own research: "the language of an
automated system survives a lot of quiet re-scoping before anyone checks
whether the substance kept up with it."

The finding, the School notes, extends a running practice of auditing an
instrument's confidence against what it can actually confirm before anyone
leans on it further. The full paper is available from the University's
research repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.j1l7f6.
