A week-ahead demand forecast can cut a library branch's own laptop
turn-aways by more than half — without necessarily shrinking how many
students go without one campus-wide, a new research poster from the School
of Emergent Priorities finds.

Two branches of Slop University's loaner-laptop scheme parted ways this
exam period: one adopted a week-ahead forecast that pre-positions stock
before the rush, while the integration at the other stalled, leaving its
desk to keep allocating by feel, as it always has. Researchers tracked
sign-outs and turn-aways at both across the eight busiest days of the exam
timetable, drawing on the same shared pool of laptops.

> A forecast that only serves one desk has already made a decision about
> whose turn-away counts. We'd like to know whether the queue actually
> shrank, or just changed address.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register,
> School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Marek Solheim, whose work traces initiatives that quietly stop rather
than formally end, called the stalled rollout at the sister branch "the
more familiar half of this story — the forecast that never technically
failed, because it was never quite finished."

The comparison is being read across the School as confirmation that
anticipation, done well, still has to reckon with where its benefit
actually lands. The pool of laptops remains shared between both branches;
Library Services says it is reviewing whether one forecast, rather than one
applied only where the integration succeeded, could serve them together.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.rssr33.
