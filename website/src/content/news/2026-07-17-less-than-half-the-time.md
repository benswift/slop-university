Four floodlight towers over Slop University's main sports oval no longer
switch off the instant a booking's clock runs out: a motion-sensing
extension keeps them lit as long as something moves underneath, on the
assumption that whoever it is still needs to see. A new paper from the
School of Continuous Improvement asked how often that assumption holds.
Less than half the time.

Researchers paired eleven weeks of the floodlight system's own extension
log against a record it never consults: the on-duty grounds supervisor's
nightly closing entry, kept on paper, independent of the sensor loop. Of
132 times the lights stayed on past a booking's end, a person was
confirmed still on the ground in 61. The rest, checked against a
maintenance camera already mounted on the towers, traced to mowing and
line-marking, an irrigation cycle starting up, wildlife at the verge, and
insects circling the lamp housings.

> The sensor was never wrong about something moving. It was just
> answering a much bigger question than is a player still here, and
> nobody had told it otherwise.
>
> --- Dr Osei Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor, Review Cadence
> Observatory, School of Continuous Improvement

The team also tested a sensitivity adjustment the system's installer
recommended, meant to filter out smaller, non-human movement; it shifted
the result by two percentage points either way. "A finer setting doesn't
fix a sensor that was never asked the right question," said Dr Thandiwe
Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter.

Facilities has not changed the extension logic. The closing-log
comparison, the researchers note, cost nothing extra to run: the
paperwork already existed for other reasons.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.hed0q8.
