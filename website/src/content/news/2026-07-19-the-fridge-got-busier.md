The School of Emergent Priorities spends much of its year on how an
institution prepares for what it can already see coming. Its newest research
poster turns that lens on a smaller anticipatory instrument: the fridge-mounted
chart a parenting program hands out each school holidays, meant to ready a
household for the first week of term.

> A chart earns its keep by being finished, not by changing Monday morning. We
> didn't expect to find the two so cleanly separated, and we'd like to know
> how many of the University's own instruments share the same split.
>
> --- Postdoctoral Fellow Petra Umbile

Dr Iben Chikere and Dr Petra Umbile surveyed 214 households across three
primary-school parent networks, sorting each by whether its reset chart was
completed in full or abandoned within the first three days of the break, then
compared parent-reported bedtime and screen-time disputes across the first
fortnight of term against a subset's independently logged family-calendar
entries. Households that finished the chart reported almost exactly as many
disputes as households that didn't; what completion did predict, closely, was
how many stickers were left on the fridge door by the second week.

The result adds to the School's case that an instrument's completion rate and
the thing it was built to change are two separate measurements, worth taking
separately rather than assuming one stands in for the other.

"The garden study taught us that a form's completion and a form's content
aren't always the same measurement," said Dr Chikere, whose earlier work
traced a similar gap through a community garden's handover paperwork. "This
one confirms they usually aren't."

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.azf7r2.
