The School of Continuous Improvement's running account of what happens when a
group hands an informal arrangement to a formal system has a new entry: the
office gift exchange. A new Slop University paper compares twenty workplace
teams' annual Secret Santa draws --- eleven on folded paper, nine through an
anonymous matching app --- and asks whether the app keeps a secret any better
than the hat did.

Dr Renke Sabel, Dr Bram Ntuli and Dr Joost Nwosu followed each team from draw
to reveal, surveying every participant on whether they knew or strongly
suspected their assignment early, how the information reached them if so, and
whether the team acted once it noticed.

> Every instrument we deploy tells us something about what it replaces,
> whether or not it tells us what we asked it to. This one told us the same
> amount, in a different accent.
>
> --- Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Indicator Commons

Overall, app-matched teams leaked to about the same share of participants as
paper-slip teams did. What changed was the leak's shape: a paper slip mostly
gave itself away by being seen or overheard, while an app match mostly gave
itself away through a lock-screen notification nobody asked to see.
App-matched teams were also far quicker to call a re-draw once a leak
surfaced, something a folded slip rarely got the chance to do.

"Now that a team has a leak rate, the harder question is what happens to
it," said Dr Joost Nwosu, Postdoctoral Fellow. "Whether it quietly becomes
the number the next exchange gets judged against, or just sits in a file
nobody reopens."

The University counts the paper among its clearer recent illustrations of a
pattern it keeps rediscovering: formalising an arrangement changes its shape
well before it changes its outcome.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.6c4dv1.
