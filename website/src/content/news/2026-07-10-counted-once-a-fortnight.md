A student who needs the campus food pantry twice in a fortnight may visit it
once. The Living Dashboard, which reads those visits as a leading indicator of
financial hardship, records the one.

A new poster from the School of Continuous Improvement, presented this week by
the Office of Research Outputs, asks what that count is able to see. Dr Mirela
Hanke and Associate Professor Casimir Beng asked 64 pantry users to keep a
confidential fortnightly record of their own need, uncapped and held apart from
the ration, and matched each entry to the same person's official visit log
across eight fortnights. Eighteen sat for exit interviews on what a visit had
come to mean once their allocation was spent.

The diary index climbed 2.5× over the term. Recorded visits did not move,
holding within 0.84 to 0.98 of the single visit the pantry allows, and the two
series agreed only weakly (r = 0.34). Agreement was thinnest among the
participants who exhausted a fortnight's allocation in its first two days.

> Sixty-four people kept a private account of a difficult fortnight, every
> fortnight, for a term, and handed it to us. What the University owes them back
> is precision about what its own record was never built to hold.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Continuous
> Improvement

The University maintains that a measure standing between a student and the help
available to them should be asked, from time to time, what it is capable of
registering.

"A count that cannot rise above one is not counting need above one," said
Associate Professor Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "Whether the
Dashboard should carry a figure the ration has already settled is a question the
Lab now has to put to itself."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.y1dp98.
