A five-item scoresheet can make a book club host's private impression look
like data. Whether it behaves like data --- reliable, and also right --- is
the question a new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities and the
School of Continuous Improvement puts to 214 real meetings across 27
neighbourhood book clubs.

The instrument, a five-item Skip-Likelihood Index a host fills in as
discussion happens, agrees with itself well: its own items rise and fall
together across the study's eight months. Set against an anonymous show of
hands taken at the close of each meeting, though, the index's predictions
land only loosely, and the paper traces almost the entire relationship back
to a single item.

> An index that agrees with itself is doing something. It just isn't
> necessarily doing the thing you built it for.
>
> --- Runa Adegoke, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Strategic Drift Survey

Dr Adegoke and Dr Renke Sabel set the host-scored index against a same-night
confession count, collected independently and only after scoring was sealed,
across a regional network of library-affiliated and independent clubs. A
second host quietly repeated the scoring at a subset of meetings, to see
whether two people watching the same room would flag the same attendees.

"It isn't really about book clubs," said Dr Sabel. "It's about any scoring
job a person does quickly, in a room, without asking directly, which
describes a great deal of what this School spends its time watching."

The University counts the space between an instrument's internal agreement
and what it actually predicts among the more useful gaps its research keeps
finding, precisely because the gap is so easy to miss from inside the
instrument itself.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.3hjzqw.
