Ask a regular at any of the University's three working self-service
bike-repair stations how they found it, and the University's own published
directory is rarely part of the answer. A new paper from the School of
Continuous Improvement interviews eighteen campus bike users and the
contracted technician who maintains the network to find out what is.

Two of the network's five original stations were removed during a 2024
walkway project and never reinstated; the University's Sustainable
Mobility pages have listed all five, unchanged, ever since. Researchers
coded eighteen semi-structured interviews into a shared set of five
strategies bike users rely on instead of the page, and put the same
question to the technician who removes a station's hardware but has no
line to whoever maintains its listing.

> A directory is a promise an institution makes about its own accuracy, and
> this one had gone quietly unkept for two years before anyone needed to
> notice.
>
> --- Dr Osei Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor, Review Cadence
> Observatory, School of Continuous Improvement

"We study indicators that outlive their own usefulness for a living," said
Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the Indicator Commons. "A
station directory turned out to be exactly that kind of object, just
smaller than what we usually look at."

Most interviewees described locating a working station from memory or a
friend's tip rather than the page; only two had ever consulted it, and both
had been sent to a stretch of fencing. The School has added the directory
to its list of instruments worth checking, simply because nobody had
gotten around to it first.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xiq6w4.
