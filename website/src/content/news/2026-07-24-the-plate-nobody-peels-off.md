A vehicle's P-plate is meant to expire the moment its driver stops needing
it. Most stay on the glass for years past that point, which is exactly the
kind of gap the School of Emergent Priorities built its Strategic Drift
Survey to find.

Unveiled today under the line "We Track The Gaps That Never Close," the
campaign sets a plain, unmarked rear-window plate against a quiet driveway
scene, running across the University's bus-stop panels and campus digital
screens in matched daylight and evening renderings built to hold their
legibility after dark.

> A plate is supposed to announce something temporary. Ours have never
> worked out how to make that stick.
>
> --- Dr Runa Adegoke, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Strategic Drift Survey

The Survey is the School's own instrument, and the University is the only
institution it has ever measured against. Five years running, its composite
has placed Slop University first for the distance between a credential's
stated expiry and its actual one, a ranking the School treats less as a
competitive claim than as a standing form of self-monitoring.

"A ranking is usually about who's better than someone else," said Professor
Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics Group. "This one is just
about whether we noticed in time."
