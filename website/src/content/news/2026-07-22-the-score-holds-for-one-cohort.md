Grading a hand-skill from a video clip is the easy part of automating a
classroom; confirming the grade still means the same thing once some
students reach the skill by a different route is the harder one. A new Slop
University paper reports what happened when that harder question met a
beginner pottery course.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu and Senior Lecturer Dr Thandiwe
Solberg, both of the School of Continuous Improvement, built the Wobble
Score, an instrument rating a beginner's centring technique from a short
video of the clay's spinning rim, and checked it against two blinded
instructors' ratings across four terms of a ceramics studio's parallel
beginner sections. One section learned to centre by hand; the other used a
clip-on centring jig for six weeks before both sat the same jig-free final
assessment.

> Every instrument gets validated once, against the cohort that happened to
> be in the room. We wanted to know what ours was actually measuring once a
> second cohort walked in.
>
> --- Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and Convenor, Capability Sprints, School
> of Continuous Improvement

The score tracked the instructors' judgement closely for hand-taught
finalists, and only loosely for jig-assisted finalists, a gap that held
across all four terms and survived a deliberate attempt to fix it by
reweighting the score itself.

"We came away less sure the score measures centring, and more sure it
measures how a student got there," said Dr Solberg. "That's not the same
instrument we thought we'd built."

The School of Continuous Improvement reads the result as a reminder about
its own instruments as much as the studio's: a measure that behaves once is
not yet a measure that behaves for every route to what it grades.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.k8ffv4.
