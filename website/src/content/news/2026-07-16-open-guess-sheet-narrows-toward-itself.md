The Union Market's Jellybean Census has run for three consecutive terms on a
simple pairing: a jar, and either a sealed ballot box or a pinned sign-in
sheet where each guess sits in view of the next. A new research poster from
the School of Continuous Improvement, _Converging on Each Other, Not the
Jar_, is the first to check what the open sheet's famously tightening spread
of guesses has actually been converging on.

The finding lands as the School's evaluation-ecosystem agenda turns, this
term, toward the instruments campus events run on without a second look.
Researchers logged 428 guesses across eleven stalls over the full term,
split between the sealed and open conditions, matching each stall's spread
of guesses against the jar's own count once judging closed. The open
sheet's spread narrowed sharply as each stall's queue moved through it; the
sealed box's did not — and the open sheet's accuracy against the counted
jar did not improve to match.

> The sheet was never measuring the jar. It was measuring how quickly a
> queue agrees with whoever's already written something down.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor of the Impact Pathway
Atlas, said the result "raises the obvious next question for every board on
campus that shows its working as it fills up" — namely, which ones are
converging on an answer, and which are just converging.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.hh9kvt.
