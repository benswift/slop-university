A note taped to a community fridge door doesn't wait in a queue. It waits
in one of four, and a season-long audit from the School of Emergent
Priorities has found the four don't move at the same speed.

Dr Iben Chikere and Dr Petra Umbile photographed and logged every
handwritten note left on one suburban community fridge across an 18-week
season, coding all 214 into four categories --- thank-you, request,
warning, other --- and timing each against the next matching entry in the
volunteer restock sign-off sheet. A note flagged as a warning drew a
restock in a median 3.3 hours. A request waited a median 26.

> A note gets answered on its category, not on how badly whoever wrote it
> needed something. That's not a decision anyone made. It's just what four
> labels turned out to do once volunteers started reading for them.
>
> --- Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The coding scheme did not stay a research instrument for long. Partway
through the season, several regular contributors began pre-labelling their
own notes "WARNING," a habit the team can trace directly to which notes
drew the fastest volunteer visits. Co-author Dr Petra Umbile, whose own
work follows what becomes of contributions valued but never counted, called
the shift "the taxonomy teaching the fridge how to be read, rather than the
other way around."

The School of Emergent Priorities keeps finding the same pattern in
whatever it turns its instruments toward: a measure changes the thing it
was built only to describe. This time the measure was four handwritten
words, and the thing was who gets fed.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.dsr022.
