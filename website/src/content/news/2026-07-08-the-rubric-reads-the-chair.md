Every year, a rotating committee in Slop University's School of Continuous
Improvement redrafts the marking rubric for the Master of Applied Measurement's
capstone project, standard practice on a program built around the idea that what
an institution measures, it can improve. A new paper asks what happens when a
decade of capstones the rubric graded gets marked again, under every other
year's version of the same instrument.

Researchers regraded 140 capstones from ten MAM cohorts twice each, blind to the
original grade: once under their own submission year's rubric text, and once
under a different year's. When the alternate rubric shared the capstone's own
drafting chair, the distinction band changed for about one project in nine; when
it came from a different chair's tenure, it changed for more than half, a gap a
separate, rubric-blind quality ranking barely narrowed.

The School of Continuous Improvement treats the finding as a discomfort worth
sitting with rather than resolving quickly: the flagship program that trains the
University's own measurement specialists cannot, on this evidence, hold its
central assessment instrument steady across the people who take turns drafting
it.

"We built this design to find out whether the rubric was reading the work or the
work was reading the rubric committee," said Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer in
the School of Continuous Improvement. "It turned out to be the second one, and
that is not a comfortable thing to learn about your own flagship program."

Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Continuous Improvement,
recognised the pattern from her own work on the University's dashboards. "An
indicator that migrates from measuring something to deciding something usually
does it quietly enough that nobody notices the handover," she said. "Here you
can actually watch the distinction band cross over, cohort by cohort."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.jze8gt.
