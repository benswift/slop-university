When a fire alarm sounds in the building, the number that reaches Facilities
and Safety first isn't the one from the door: it's the headcount marshals
radio in from the lawn, the forecourt, and the car park below. A new paper
from the School of Continuous Improvement asked how closely that number
tracks what the building's own swipe-card system would say for the same
alarm, and found the two agree on some floors, and for different reasons on
the ones where they don't.

> A number's job doesn't end once it's accurate enough to act on. Ours told
> us that within the hour, and we only checked what it actually was
> afterwards.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Over one teaching term, Dr Hanke, Dr Renke Sabel, and Dr Osei Vandermeer
matched every fire-drill assembly headcount at a nine-storey administration
building against what the same building's swipe-card system recorded in the
minutes before each alarm, floor by floor, then tested whether an added
marshal briefing partway through the term closed any gap between the two.
It didn't: the lowest floors' tallies ran high, the highest floors' ran low,
and the pattern held before and after the briefing.

Dr Sabel called the finding "a fairly ordinary thing for an indicator to do
once nobody's asked it to prove itself in a while," and said the School is
weighing whether the roster formula that leans on the headcount deserves a
standing check of its own.

The School reads the result as evidence that even its most habitual
instruments are worth an occasional second look, particularly the ones
nobody remembers choosing over an available alternative.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.hq8wqq.
