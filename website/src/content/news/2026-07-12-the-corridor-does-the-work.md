Two in three of the "successful waterings" Slop University's office-plant
watering agent logs each fortnight turn out to follow a colleague topping up
the pot by hand, ahead of the soil-moisture threshold the agent was built to
act on. A new poster from the School of Continuous Improvement is the first
to check the agent's own record against what actually happened at the pot.

Dr Sten Okwuosa and Dr Thandiwe Solberg covertly tracked 58 potted plants
across eleven shared kitchenettes for 14 weeks, logging soil moisture and
leaf condition by hand alongside the agent's watering record. The agent's
claimed watering rate held between 88 and 93 per cent everywhere, regardless
of foot traffic; actual 14-week survival did not, ranging from 42 per cent in
the quietest kitchenettes to 89 per cent in the busiest.

> The instrument agrees with itself beautifully. It just isn't the thing
> keeping the ferns alive.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas

Dr Solberg, who convenes the School's Demo Quarter showcase, called the gap
"a clean instrument next to a curve it has stopped explaining," and read it
as one more instance of the School's evaluation-ecosystem work turning its
instincts on its own fixtures before anyone outside has to ask. Facilities'
monthly credit to the agent is unchanged for now; the School has asked for a
line in next quarter's report for whoever keeps the pots alive between
readings.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zi5oe0.
