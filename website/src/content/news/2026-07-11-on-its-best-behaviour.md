Slop University's fire-safety walk-through has recorded stairwell doors
closed on 97% of its scheduled passes for the past year. A new paper from the
School of Continuous Improvement sets that figure against a different one:
an anonymous survey of 438 staff and students across the same eleven
buildings finds three in five report propping a stairwell fire door open at
least once in the past month, most often with a folded document, a shoe, or
the corridor's own fire extinguisher.

> A walk-through announced a week ahead of itself is not really testing the
> door. It is testing whether the building can look its best when it knows
> someone is coming, and it turns out that it can.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor of Evaluation of Evaluation

The study is the kind of small, unglamorous audit the School of Continuous
Improvement was built to run, setting an official compliance record against
what a self-report instrument sees on the days nobody is carrying a
clipboard. The team split the survey into an anonymous condition and a
signed one to check the high propping-admission rate wasn't simply people
feeling safer confessing under cover; the two conditions returned almost the
same figure. Dr Bram Ntuli, the paper's second author, said the result "says
less about any one propped door than about what a compliance instrument can
and cannot see once its own inspection schedule becomes public knowledge."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.5uwpmh.
