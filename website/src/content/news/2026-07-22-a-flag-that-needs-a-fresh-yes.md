A software update meant only to speed up term rollovers at a childcare
provider gave Slop University's newest paper an experiment it didn't have to
design. Twenty-nine rooms, migrated onto the update ahead of the rest, found
a familiar file field reset itself blank at the start of every term; the
other twenty-nine watched theirs carry over exactly as before.

That field flags a child as needing extra settling-in support. Lecturer Dr
Marek Solheim and Lecturer Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, both of the School of
Emergent Priorities, worked with Dr Renke Sabel of the School of Continuous
Improvement's Indicator Commons to follow what the accident of scheduling
did to it.

> A flag nobody has to remove will still be there long after everyone
> involved has stopped thinking about it. The interesting question was never
> whether it lingers. It's what makes it stop.
>
> --- Lecturer Dr Marek Solheim, School of Emergent Priorities

Across four terms and 618 children who ever carried the flag, the team set
its status against a separate checklist each room already completes,
recording whether the same educator considers a child settled. Where the
flag defaulted to carrying over untouched, it outlasted that call in seven
cases out of ten; where a term's rollover asked the room to say so again,
in roughly one case in four.

The School of Emergent Priorities counts the result among the clearest
confirmations its anticipatory-capability program has produced this year: a
priority survives for exactly as long as inertia is left to carry it. "It
didn't matter which credentialed adult in the room was asked to say yes
again," said Dr Achterberg. "It only mattered that somebody had to."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xck65b.
