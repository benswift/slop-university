The School of Emergent Priorities keeps one instrument that has to answer
for the weather three days out: the Horizon Register, whose wet-weather
call decides how many loaner umbrellas Facilities puts out at each bin the
next morning. A new poster asks whether that call is worth the trouble of
making.

> Most of what the Register predicts, we never get to check twice inside
> one term. Umbrellas gave us six bins and fourteen weeks to find out
> exactly where the forecast is worth trusting.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

Six loaner-umbrella bins across two Schools were counted daily for a full
term, with each morning's restocking decision logged in advance under
either the Register's forecast rule or a simple repeat of the previous
day's tally, so neither rule could be adjusted after the fact. Restocking
shortfall under the two rules proved indistinguishable on dry or lightly
wet mornings. Once forecast rainfall passed 10 millimetres, the Register's
call cut the average shortfall from 4.6 to 3.1 umbrellas across the six
bins, a margin the School is filing as routine maintenance of its own
instrument rather than a mark against it.

Dr Ingrid Vasseur, who convenes the School's Futures in Committee seminar
series, said the result sits comfortably beside her own case that a
forecast's value is usually settled by committee long after the forecast
itself is made. "A model doesn't have to be right every day to earn its
keep," she said. "It has to be right on the day someone would otherwise
have guessed wrong."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.4kgmph.
