Regulators overseas have begun pressing platforms to slow autoplay and cap
infinite scroll. Slop University's lobby signage screen falls under none of
those orders, and slowed its own rotation anyway, on anticipated-compliance
grounds adopted before anyone asked. A new poster from the School of
Emergent Priorities tests what the change actually did.

Dr Lindiwe Achterberg and Dr Ingrid Vasseur set the screen's dwell-time
sensor --- the reading credited with justifying the slower rotation ---
against 214 unannounced recall quizzes run on passersby over nine weeks,
sorting each sensor read into a low, medium, or high tier before checking
what a passerby could actually remember moments later. Recall held at
roughly one of four rotating notices in every tier, unchanged once the
rotation itself slowed in week five.

> A sensor that lingers near a screen and a person who remembers what was
> on it turned out to be two different studies wearing one wire. We only
> meant to run the second.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The rotation cap stands; the poster does not ask that it be reversed. The
University reads the result less as a rebuke of the sensor than as a
reminder that acting early on a risk and checking whether the risk applied
are two different disciplines --- and on this evidence, only one of them
happened here.

"The change went to committee under a heading about keeping pace with
expectations that hadn't arrived here," said Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer
and Convenor of Futures in Committee. "Nobody on that agenda asked what the
sensor was measuring. Neither did we, until now."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.63i907.
