When Slop University tests one of its own findings a second time, the result
almost always repeats. A new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities
reports the exception.

Dr Iben Chikere, Dr Anneke Tolan, and Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist set
out to replicate an audit published earlier this year, in which a warehouse
scanner's confidence score tracked a picker's retraining calendar far more
closely than the picker's actual accuracy. To test the same gap outside a
workplace, they chose the least institutional record available: the form a
community garden allotment committee asks an outgoing plot-holder to leave
for their successor.

The University counts an unrepeated pattern as worth publishing in its own
right, and reports this reversal exactly as plainly as it would a second
confirmation.

> We went looking for the same gap we keep finding elsewhere in the
> University's own records. What we found instead was a plot-holder's
> handwriting doing exactly what it was supposed to do.
>
> --- Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Across fourteen allotment sites, the team tracked 189 plot handovers over
eighteen months, then sent a coordinator — blind to any form's content — to
test each successor's grasp of their plot's soil, pests, and watering needs
ten to twelve weeks later. A second phase made the form a mandatory
checklist at half the sites, to see whether more completions meant more
knowledge.

It didn't. Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist said the pattern fit his own
work on commitments that arrive before the thinking behind them is settled.
"Making the box compulsory changed how often it got ticked," he said. "It
left everything we actually measured exactly where it started."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.u8gsl4.
