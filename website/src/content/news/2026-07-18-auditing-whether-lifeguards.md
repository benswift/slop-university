An alarm that catches every drowning risk within four seconds looks, on
paper, like nothing at the poolside has changed. A new research poster from
the School of Continuous Improvement checked what a lifeguard was doing
with their own eyes while that compliance record stayed spotless.

Dr Sten Okwuosa and Dr Joost Nwosu drew on existing overhead footage across
nine public pools, coding how often each of 58 lifeguards scanned the water
unprompted as the months they'd worked under an installed alarm
accumulated. The venue's own alarm-response-time compliance was audited on
a separate track, against the vendor's own event log, with its four-second
threshold held constant throughout.

> An alarm that never misses a beat can still be quietly doing all the
> watching.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas, School of Continuous Improvement

Co-author Dr Joost Nwosu, whose own work follows a performance target once
it's been met, called the compliance figure "the cleanest case I've come
across of a target that stopped needing anyone to notice it was still being
hit." For a school built around measurement, the pairing wears a familiar
shape in a new setting: the audited number held still, and the behaviour
underneath it did not.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.g1d4nv.
