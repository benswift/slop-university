> The clipboard was supposed to capture what a volunteer thought a bag
> weighed. What it mostly captures, by the time someone's fourth or fifth in
> the queue, is what the scale already told them.
>
> --- Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor, Futures in Committee, School
> of Emergent Priorities

That reading sits behind a new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities,
_Weighed In Before It's Weighed_, which spent a growing season at the campus
community garden's toolshed comparing volunteers' eyeballed harvest-bag
guesses against the scale that settles the question moments later. The shed
has run the same setup since 2019: a digital platform scale by the door, and
a chalkboard beside it carrying a running tally of the morning's last few
readings, visible to whoever is still waiting.

Researchers logged 326 weigh events across fourteen working-bee sessions,
using each volunteer's queue position as a measure of how many prior
readings they'd seen chalked up before writing their own guess. Guess spread
narrowed by a similar amount whether that day's scale was later confirmed
accurate or --- in four sessions, traced to a bent load-cell bracket ---
found reading 0.35 to 0.45 kilograms high throughout. A laminated sign
reading "guess before you look," introduced partway through the term, left
the pattern statistically unchanged.

"People aren't guessing worse on the days the scale is wrong," said Dr Petra
Umbile, a co-author on the study. "They're guessing exactly as confidently,
at exactly the same number, for exactly the wrong reason."

The School frames the result as part of a wider look, this term, at the
small readouts campus routines quietly let stand in for the thing they were
meant to display. The full paper is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.kvkc0b.
