The School of Emergent Priorities built its case for heatmap-guided
invigilation on a simple premise: send attention to where the previous
sitting's integrity flags clustered, and the next sitting's flags should
thin out to match. A new paper tests that premise across fifteen sittings
and reports it did not hold.

> A heatmap earns its keep by moving someone's attention. Whether it also
> moves what an invigilator actually catches turned out to be a separate
> question, and across fifteen sittings this one kept answering no.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register,
> School of Emergent Priorities

The project tracked the Applied Foresight Practicum's return to in-person
sitting, pairing seating and patrol assignments each week to a live
"Sitting Heat Index" built from the sitting before it, then compared flag
rates, zone-level flag counts, and invigilator dwell time against a
reconstructed counterfactual in which seats and patrols had simply been
drawn at random.

Flag rates held level before and after the index's introduction, at 12.5
against 12.6 per sitting. Zone-level flags tracked invigilator dwell time
far more closely than the zone's own risk tier, and the randomised
counterfactual reached statistically the same flag total as the deployed
protocol.

Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, the paper's co-author, said invigilators "adjusted
to the index within a sitting or two and then mostly stopped noticing it,
the way any rostering tool turns into furniture" --- a description she
noted the paper's own title had already borrowed.

The School folds the null result into its evaluation-ecosystem agenda as
confirmation that the audit habit holds even when a favoured instrument
disappoints it, and is carrying the randomised counterfactual forward as a
standing check for any future instrument proposed on similar grounds.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.l19xml.
