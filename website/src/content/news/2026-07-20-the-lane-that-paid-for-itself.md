Six primary schools now sell families a laminated "priority lane" permit
at car-pool pickup: pay, and a marked lane lets your car filter past the
general queue. A new Slop University research poster from the School of
Continuous Improvement asks where the minutes a permit buys actually come
from, and reports that the general lane already knows.

Dr Mirela Hanke and Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran tracked departure times
across three schools running a priority lane and three without, over a
ten-week trial, matching each family's exit time against their own permit
status and the lane arrangement at their own school.

> A permit only has to look like it's buying speed; whether that minute has
> to come from somewhere else is a separate, and much less marketable,
> question.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Permit holders left, on average, two and a half minutes earlier than
general-lane families at the same school. General-lane departures at the
three priority-lane schools ran slower than at the three schools with no
lane at all, by very nearly that same margin --- and uptake of the permit
itself climbed for six weeks before settling near a third of each school's
car-pool families.

Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran, whose own work follows how an
already-settled arrangement gathers the appearance of consultation around
it, said the plateau was the most familiar part of the finding: "families
seem to find a trade's true price a lot faster than any of our surveys
usually do."

The University counts the trial among its clearer accounts yet of a
convenience that has to be paid for by someone, even when no line item
says who.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.1bx2n3.
