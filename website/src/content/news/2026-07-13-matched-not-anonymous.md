A staff member who files an anonymous integrity tip is told, correctly,
that the intake form asks for no name. A new paper from the School of
Continuous Improvement reports that the tip line's own ticketing system,
entirely by accident, often supplies one anyway.

Lecturer Dagny Okafor, Postdoctoral Fellow Mirela Hanke, and Senior
Lecturer Renke Sabel built a lightweight tool that scores an anonymous tip
against a pool of likely staff using only two fields the ticketing system
already keeps for ordinary operational reasons: when a tip arrived, and
what it said. Tested against a sample of closed tickets, the tool placed
the true submitter at the top of a short, team-scoped list more often than
not, with its reach narrowing as the candidate pool widens to a lab,
programme, or school.

> A field kept for scheduling and a field kept for record-keeping turn out,
> together, to do quite a lot of work a form's blank name field was
> supposed to make unnecessary.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation

The team paired the tool with an independent audit of existing triage case
notes, finding language consistent with the same kind of inference already
present at a broadly similar rate, without any tool involved. "The
capability isn't hypothetical," said Dr Mirela Hanke, a co-author. "In a
lot of these notes, something like it already seems to be happening."

The School situates the paper within its wider practice of auditing the
University's own assurance instruments before turning them on anything
else. A triage-confidence indicator drawing on the tool is now under
discussion for inclusion on the Living Dashboard.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.go3vki.
