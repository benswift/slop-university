Not every fix that works on campus travels well once it leaves it. The
School of Emergent Priorities has spent the past several months testing how
far one of them gets.

A new research poster from the School reports a 22-week attempt to
redeploy an already-published Slop University tool — a rule-based agent
that reclaims a booked-but-empty room once a grace window elapses without a
check-in — at three public library branches, matched against three that
kept booking as usual. The release is presented as part of the
University's wider interest in which of its findings hold their shape
outside the settings that produced them.

> A finding that only works in one building was never really general — it
> was a description of that building.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Runa Adegoke, Strategic Drift Survey

The team held every parameter from the original campus trial constant —
the same twelve-minute grace window, the same kiosk check-in signal — and
swapped only the setting, tracking weekly room use at three library
branches against three that changed nothing. Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja
Oyelaran, also of the School of Emergent Priorities, said the result
"raises exactly the kind of question the School exists to sit with a
while longer" — namely, how much of what travels between settings is the
intervention, and how much was always the room.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.wpf4rm.
