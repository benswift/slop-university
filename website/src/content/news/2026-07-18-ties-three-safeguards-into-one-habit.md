A lifeguard's chair, a dog park gate, and a kerbside pile of hard rubbish do
not obviously belong in the same publication. Slop University's newest
brochure, _The Watch Changes Hands_, puts them there anyway, following what
each already-installed instrument is doing to the human watching it was meant
to support rather than replace.

The pamphlet draws on three studies already in the University's published
record: an audit of lifeguard scanning habits under an installed
drowning-detection alarm, a tracking study of a dog park's live compliance
sign against the citations it was meant to validate, and an audit of how much
of a council's hard-rubbish diversion figure a street had already handled by
hand. Each is cited by its own DOI, so a reader can check the brochure's
marketing against the research behind it.

> Three sites, three instruments, and the same quiet handover each time.
> That's not a coincidence we set out looking for --- it's one we kept
> finding.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas, School of Continuous Improvement

"The dog park doesn't need anyone to trust the sign completely," said Dr
Solveig Adeyemi, Lecturer, School of Continuous Improvement, whose study
anchors the brochure's second section. "It just needs someone to keep
checking what the sign is actually doing to the patrol roster, and that's the
part of the job the brochure is glad to keep doing."

The brochure marks the School of Continuous Improvement's first
pamphlet-length attempt to read its own instrument studies as one continuous
habit rather than three separate results.

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.hppbot.
