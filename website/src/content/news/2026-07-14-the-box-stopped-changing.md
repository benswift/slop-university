A cover sheet's AI-use box gets filled in once, the University's newest
research poster finds, and then it simply repeats. Across a full teaching
semester, a student's first declared answer forecast every later one almost
perfectly --- even as an independent read on their actual reliance kept
moving underneath it.

> Once a box is signed, nobody comes back to ask if it still describes what's
> happening. We wanted to know how long that first answer keeps being
> trusted anyway.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

Associate Professor Lindqvist and Dr Anouk Mensah tracked 268 students
across six cover-sheet declarations each, pairing every tick against a
separate, independently derived estimate of the student's actual AI
reliance at the time. The declared answer barely moved: 94 per cent of
students were still ticking their Assignment One box by Assignment Six. The
independent estimate moved a great deal more, drifting by a full band or
further for well over half the cohort across the same stretch.

"A form doesn't know it's gone stale," said Dr Mensah. "It just sits there
being cited."

The finding extends an agenda the Trajectory Analytics Group already
carries --- how a stated commitment, once recorded, comes to stand in for
whatever it was originally meant to track --- applied here to a form most
people fill in without a second thought.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.4vwn04.
