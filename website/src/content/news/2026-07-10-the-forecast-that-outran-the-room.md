Nine lecture theatres, 214 timetabled sessions, and one number the
Timetabling Office sets before a single student enrols: a forecast of how
full the room will be. A new poster from the School of Emergent Priorities
asks how well that number holds up once the term is actually under way.

Dr Fenna Okoro and Dr Ingrid Vasseur set a term of ceiling-mounted clicker
counts against the Office's own pre-term capacity model, tracking nine
theatres from the first week of semester to the last. The forecast, once
issued, was left to run untouched --- the same policy the Office applies to
every subject it caps or clears to grow.

Attendance never caught up to what the forecast predicted. Across the nine
theatres, recorded occupancy tracked the model only weakly, and the shortfall
widened rather than closed as the weeks went on.

> A forecast is a bet placed once and rarely revisited. We would like ours
> revisited a little more often than a term.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the Horizon Register

The University holds that a forecast earns its keep only for as long as it
keeps describing the room it was built to predict.

Dr Vasseur, whose work follows how forecasting talk becomes committee
business, said the pattern was a familiar one: "the model outlives the
moment it was calibrated for, and nobody's job is to notice."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.qeq1w5.
