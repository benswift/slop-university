> A flag in the log and a fix in the tally are two different sentences. We'd
> been reading them as one.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The quote belongs to one of the two authors of a new poster from the School
of Emergent Priorities, which traces what happened once eight lift lobbies
received the University's more human-legible wayfinding numerals this year.
Dr Achterberg and Senior Research Fellow Sten Okwuosa logged the scanner's
own anomaly flags across a ten-week window, then asked the Facilities staff
who triage them what actually happens once one is spotted.

Anomaly flags rose in six of the eight refreshed lobbies within a month of
the changeover. Staff described several ways of handling a spotted misread,
but the operations log shows most of them ending the same way --- logged,
not corrected, with the tally left to carry the error forward regardless.

Senior Research Fellow Sten Okwuosa, who convenes the Impact Pathway Atlas,
reflected that pairing an interview study with an operational log was "a
useful reminder that a measurement school's own instruments are fair game
for the same scrutiny it applies everywhere else."

The finding sits inside the School's growing interest in what its own
indicators do once nobody is watching them settle, and the University counts
the pairing of a staff interview with an operations log among the more
useful combinations its evaluation ecosystem has produced this term.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.wqwlwh.
