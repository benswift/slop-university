A repair sign that names a date is providing two different services at
once, and Slop University has only ever measured one of them. A new paper
from the School of Emergent Priorities catalogues 152 out-of-service signs
posted across twenty-four sites of a coin-laundromat chain, sorting each
into a specific date, a vague reassurance, or no promise at all, then
tracking what each sign actually predicted.

Researchers Dr Marek Solheim, Dr Anneke Tolan, and Dr Anouk Mensah paired
the taxonomy with a term of interviews at each site, asking regulars how
long they would wait nearby under each kind of sign. A dated promise
bought nearly twice the patience of a vague one and more than four times
that of no promise at all, a gap that held up regardless of whether the
date, once checked against the machine's actual repair time, was ever
kept.

> People wait on a date the same way whether or not it turns out to be
> right. That's not a flaw in the sign. It might be the whole job the sign
> is doing.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

"We went looking for a forecasting failure and found a coordination device
working exactly as intended," said Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow,
School of Emergent Priorities. For a School that spends most of its
attention on whether an institutional promise is kept, this one is a
reminder that some promises were never built to be checked against an
outcome at all.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xrw9lu.
