A digital lane-rotation display now hangs on the wall of three
Slop-University-monitored public pools. Whether lap swimmers actually
consult it, rather than the lifeguard on deck, is the question a new Slop
University research poster set out to answer.

Dr Joost Nwosu and Dr Mirela Hanke, both of the School of Continuous
Improvement, surveyed 412 lap swimmers on deck across six months --- three
before the display went up, three after --- logging how often each patron
had asked a lifeguard about lane rotation that session. Self-reported
asking held at 68 per cent before installation and 66 per cent after, a
gap the pair's model treats as noise rather than signal.

> A board on the wall changes what information is available. It does not
> appear to change who a swimmer decides to trust.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Complaints about the display's glare and mounting angle rose through the
first fortnight after installation, then settled alongside every other
measure the study tracked. "The lifeguard was never really competing with
the sign," said Dr Nwosu. "We just hadn't checked."

The School of Continuous Improvement folds the finding into its wider case
files on measurement systems that don't quite reach the behaviour they're
built to shift.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.i2ulci.
