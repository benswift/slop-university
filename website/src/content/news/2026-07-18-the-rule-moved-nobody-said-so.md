Recite your household's phone rule to a stranger and you'll get a clean
answer: phones in the basket, no exceptions. Whether that's still what
happens at dinner is a different question, and until now, an unasked one.

Dr Anouk Mensah and Dr Ingrid Vasseur interviewed 34 households that named
a standing phones-in-the-basket rule unprompted, opening each conversation
by asking what they'd tell a visitor the rule was, then working back
through a fortnight of actual dinners for the exceptions that answer left
out. Coding 212 of those exceptions sorted them into four kinds, the
largest by far an exception nobody had bothered to name at all: the phone
just stayed, and neither parent nor teenager treated it as a departure from
anything. Set against what each household had opened by reciting, only
nine of the thirty-four still matched their own practice; the rest had
moved, almost always without a conversation either party could point to.

> A rule's wording is the last thing to notice it's out of date. Everything
> around it has already moved on.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

Co-author Dr Ingrid Vasseur, who usually watches this kind of drift happen
in slower motion around a standing committee table, said the dinner table
turned out to run on the same mechanism at a different speed: "an
accommodation gets made, nobody minutes it, and eventually the accommodation
is just what the rule means now." The School of Emergent Priorities treats
a rule's small print with the same seriousness it gives a strategic
priority's opening line, and the basket by the fridge, it turns out, earns
the comparison.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.8mdjk4.
