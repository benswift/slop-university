Every fortnight, a hard-rubbish collection round closes with a single
number: the share of what left a suburb's verges that the council counts as
diverted from landfill. A new research poster from the School of Continuous
Improvement spent nine months asking who actually did the diverting.

Dr Sten Okwuosa and Dr Renke Sabel photographed 214 verge-side piles across
eleven suburbs at set-out, at dawn, and again when the truck arrived,
matching each item's disappearance against the council's own round-level
diversion entry. The figure itself barely shifted, holding in the high
eighties across the nine-month window. The share of it attributable to
someone's overnight trailer rather than the contracted service climbed from
42% to 81% over the same period.

> The figure stopped needing anyone to actually pick anything up somewhere
> around month six. That's not usually how you'd want a number to behave.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas, School of Continuous Improvement

"Every indicator we chase starts out standing in for something and ends up
mistaken for the thing itself," said Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and
Convenor of the Indicator Commons. "This one just got there rather
quickly."

The School of Continuous Improvement reads the result as one more entry in
its long-running account of what an indicator quietly stops measuring once
something else starts doing the work behind it.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.y74xj1.
