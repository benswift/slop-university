The Living Dashboard's campus-mood tile updates every week from a single
number: an automated classifier's read of a photograph taken just before
Facilities wipes the courtyard's free-expression chalkboard clean. A new
research poster from the School of Continuous Improvement asked what that
number is actually responding to.

> The tile was built to tell us how the campus feels. What it's actually
> measuring is how much anyone bothered to write that week --- and we
> hadn't noticed the difference until we counted it ourselves.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Two coders, blind to the classifier's own weekly output, sorted forty
consecutive photographs --- one per week across a full academic year ---
into six content categories, from complaint to doodle to declaration, then
checked the coded mix against the Dashboard's score. The score fell as each
week's photograph filled with more marks, whatever those marks said; it
tracked the coded share of genuinely negative content only weakly.

Dr Bram Ntuli, Lecturer and Convenor of Improvement Grand Rounds and the
poster's co-author, said the coding scheme "isn't asking Facilities to read
every mark on the board," but that "a density-corrected score would at
least stop rewarding a quiet week over a happy one." The School reads the
finding as consistent with its wider commitment to instruments that measure
what they claim to.

The Dashboard's maintainers have not committed to retraining the classifier
before the tile's next review, though a density correction is reportedly
under discussion.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.jlxxxl.
