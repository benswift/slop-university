Ask a rideshare rider what they do with the price the app shows before they
book, and most describe a habit, not a decision: a screenshot taken from
routine, a number quietly rounded up, a glance that stopped happening months
ago. A new paper from the School of Continuous Improvement names each habit
and traces where it comes from.

Two researchers interviewed 32 riders, working back through each
participant's fare history for any trip where the final charge exceeded the
quoted number. Riders sorted into four habits: screenshotting every quote,
privately rounding the number up, checking only sometimes, or no longer
checking at all.

The habit tracked how often a rider had been caught out before.
Screenshotting was the default among riders recalling two or more fare
surprises, and the exception among riders recalling none. Partway through
each interview, researchers disclosed that the app's own terms label the
estimate "informational only, not a guaranteed price," then asked what
participants would do differently next time. Almost nobody changed their
answer.

> A rider who screenshots the quote isn't waiting for the number to be
> wrong. They've just stopped assuming it will be right.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, School of Continuous Improvement

Co-author Iben Chikere, a Lecturer in the School of Emergent Priorities,
said the finding fits a pattern the two schools keep meeting from different
directions: "an institution can relabel a number as many times as it likes;
the person relying on it has usually already worked out what to do about
it."

The paper extends the School's wider interest in what happens once a
measurement stops being trusted at face value.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.jugfj6.
