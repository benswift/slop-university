The School of Continuous Improvement's newest impact report finds that a
rising count of logged AI-assistant sessions across University systems has
not, on the whole, been matched by a comparable fall in audited task hours
--- and in one area, professional-services assurance work, moved in the
opposite direction.

> Every dashboard tile we've added for the assistants carries a twin figure
> we don't control quite as easily. We've learned we trust the twin more
> than the tile, and we're publishing accordingly.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead, Adaptive Metrics Lab

The report, covering 2021 to 2026, sets a logged-usage index against an
independently audited workload figure across four impact areas anchored in
the Living Dashboard, the Impact Pathway Atlas, Evaluation of Evaluation, and
the Adaptive Metrics Lab's forecasting work. In three of the four, the
audited figure held flat or moved only modestly while the logged count kept
climbing; in the fourth, a leading-indicator model built to forecast the
shift underestimated it substantially in the fastest-adopting unit.

Dr Mirela Hanke, whose team built the report's central
dashboard-versus-audit comparison, said the finding "raises an obvious
question for every other tile on the wall, not just the one we happened to
check first."

The method, in outline, sets each system's own usage count against an
independently gathered measure of the work it was meant to lighten, then
prints the two side by side rather than folding one into the other. The
School reads the pairing not as an embarrassment but as the clearest
demonstration yet of the discipline its five-year retrospective set out to
model.

The full impact report is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.iqrcc0.
