For a full ten-pin bowling league season, a homemade sensor rig assembled
from off-the-shelf parts sat beside a bowling alley's own proprietary
lane-scoring and foul-detection system, calling every frame the incumbent
called, so a new Slop University paper could measure not just how often the
two agreed, but what happened whenever they didn't.

The finding lands as the School of Continuous Improvement's latest test of
what legitimacy a measurement earns simply by being the system already
installed.

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Dr Osei Vandermeer and Dr Renke Sabel
logged the comparator rig's calls against the incumbent system's across a
full season of league play, then set both records against an independent
video review whenever a frame was formally disputed. Routine, clean frames
returned near-identical calls from both systems; disputed frames returned
only fair agreement, with the comparator and the incumbent about equally
likely to match the video review's own read.

What decided a disputed frame, in practice, was rarely the video review.
The league's rules committee upheld the incumbent system's original call in
91 per cent of formal disputes, regardless of which system the review
sided with.

> Whether a call should out-rank an existing system, once the two agree
> this often, is not a question our comparator rig can answer by itself ---
> but it is now one the committee has to sit with.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab

"A comparator only tells you two systems disagree," said Dr Osei
Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor of the Review Cadence Observatory.
"It doesn't tell a committee which one to believe, and ours evidently
didn't change that."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.m0pulh.
