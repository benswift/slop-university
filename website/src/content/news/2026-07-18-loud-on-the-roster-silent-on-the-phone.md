A twelve-item questionnaire can now put a number on something no committee
had thought to ask: how much a parent leans on the class group chat, rather
than their own memory, to know who's collecting their child today. Dr
Casimir Beng, Dr Mirela Hanke, and Dr Iben Chikere built the instrument from
parent interviews at the school gate, reproduced its structure on an
independent sample across ten primary schools, then spent most of their
effort checking it against two things it did not itself measure: a parent's
own phone-checking in the queue, and how often a household's pick-up roster
needed a quiet after-the-fact renegotiation.

> A scale that is right about a roster and silent about a phone hasn't
> failed. It's told us something about where reliance actually lives, which
> is more than a single number was ever going to manage.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab,
> School of Continuous Improvement

Parents scoring higher on "handover deferral" --- assuming somebody else has
pick-up covered until the chat says otherwise --- had their household's
roster renegotiated more often across the term. Parents scoring higher on
the scale overall showed no difference in phone-checking while waiting.
Co-author Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, thanked the parents who tolerated a
clipboard at the gate all term, saying the deferral result "is the closest
this project came to my own interest in what a handover keeps, and what it
quietly drops."

For the School of Continuous Improvement, an instrument earns its keep once
it's clear which questions carry the finding and which just fill out the
form. PURS has told the School exactly which third of itself to trust.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.efv97d.
