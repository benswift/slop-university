The School of Continuous Improvement's newest recruitment pitch for the
Master of Applied Measurement starts with something ordinary that never
quite is: a diner coffee refill, and the quiet arithmetic sitting underneath
it.

Running across the University's bus-stop panels and campus digital screens
under the line "The Refill Was Never Really Free," the campaign frames a
ceramic mug mid-refill --- steam still rising, the pour already thinner than
the first cup --- as the argument for the whole program. It runs in matched
daylight and evening renderings tuned for after-dark reading.

> Nobody writes down the rule that decides how generous a second cup gets.
> Somebody still follows it.
>
> --- Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Indicator Commons

Dr Sabel's research on indicators that outlive the review meant to retire
them supplied the pitch's premise, but the program itself is the School's
standing answer to it: a Masters built around noticing the unwritten rule
before it hardens into a habit nobody can explain.

"A generous refill and a durable indicator are the same finding wearing
different aprons," said Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the
Adaptive Metrics Lab. "Both keep working long after anyone remembers setting
the measure."

The Master of Applied Measurement is now taking applications for the current
teaching period. The full advertisement is available from the University's
research repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.lycnk4.
