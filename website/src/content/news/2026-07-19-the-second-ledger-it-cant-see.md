The earned-minutes balance on a household screen-time app is meant to work
like any ledger: spend what's credited, and stop when it reads zero. A new
research poster from the School of Continuous Improvement finds most
families don't stop --- they just stop asking the app.

> A ledger only holds if everyone agrees to read it the same way. By the
> second week, nobody in these households was reading it the same way
> twice.
>
> --- Postdoctoral Fellow Mirela Hanke, School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Mirela Hanke and Associate Professor Casimir Beng interviewed 58 parents
and teenagers across 34 households over the two-week school holidays,
coding each negotiation over spent screen time into one of four categories.
Two coders worked independently and checked their categories against each
other before either result counted.

Sibling swaps and chore-for-minutes trades made up most negotiated episodes
in the first week. By the second, next-day borrowing --- a household
spending tomorrow's balance today --- accounted for 61 percent of the 212
episodes logged, up from 24 percent at the fortnight's start. The app's own
borrow-ahead feature, the one sanctioned way to do exactly that, turned up
in under 4 percent of trades in either week.

The finding lands squarely inside the School's evaluation-ecosystem agenda,
which has spent the year cataloguing what an instrument's users do once its
terms stop suiting them.

"The app was built to be the only ledger in the house," said Associate
Professor Beng. "It still is, technically. Everyone's just started keeping
a second one it can't see."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.pj3krj.
