A neighbourhood tool library keeps track of every borrowed drill, saw, and
ladder in one place: a single paper ledger, filled in by whichever
volunteer happens to be on the desk that afternoon. For years, the
ledger's only record of a tool's condition --- the chip in a blade, the
frayed cord --- has depended on an outgoing volunteer remembering to
mention it to whoever takes the desk next. The School of Continuous
Improvement has been awarded an Indicator Stewardship Seed Fund grant to
stop relying on that memory.

The funded protocol pairs a photographed condition record for every tool
currently on loan with a witnessed sign-off between the outgoing and
incoming coordinator at each handover, and a standing rule that no ledger
entry closes until both signatures are on the page.

> A ledger that changes hands without a witness isn't a record any more.
> It's just whoever's turn it is to guess.
>
> --- Dr Osei Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor, Review Cadence
> Observatory, School of Continuous Improvement

Co-grantee Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, said the ledger had
quietly become exactly the kind of proxy her own research keeps finding:
a blank space next to a tool's name gets read as "fine" by whoever's next
on the desk, whether or not anyone actually checked.

The grant carries $71,238 and, the School hopes, an end to the ledger's
habit of inheriting a coordinator's confidence along with their
handwriting.
