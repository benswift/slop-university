Every pill a pharmacy's dispensing robot fills is already weighed against
what the prescription called for and logged automatically, before a human
ever touches the bottle. Staff still count the pills again by hand before
handover, on top of that log. The School of Emergent Priorities set out to
measure how much of the second count's value the first check hadn't already
banked.

Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist and Associate Professor Casimir Beng
matched robot-log and human-count records across 42 pharmacy sites over a
ten-month window, coding every flagged discrepancy by error type and by
which check caught it first. The human count adds relatively little on its
own --- a handful of percentage points beyond what the robot's log already
flags --- but nearly all of that thin margin sits in one place: pairs of
dosage strengths that look different on the shelf and weigh the same on a
scale.

> What surprised us wasn't that the two checks overlap almost completely.
> It's how neatly the overlap breaks down exactly where it doesn't.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

"A system built to catch everything gets treated as though it does, right
up until someone finally checks which parts of it are actually doing the
catching," said Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive
Metrics Lab.

The School of Emergent Priorities reads the result as part of a broader
pattern in its work this year: how rarely a uniform safeguard's value turns
out to be spread evenly across everywhere it's applied.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.1ruz61.
