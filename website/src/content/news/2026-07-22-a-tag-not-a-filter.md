Eleven streets spent nine weeks finding out whether a volunteer's tap changes
what a doorbell alert sends to the group chat, or only what the chat is told
once it arrives. A new Slop University paper, published today, reports the
second.

Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the Horizon Register in the
School of Emergent Priorities, and Dr Osei Vandermeer, Research Fellow and
Convenor of the Review Cadence Observatory in the School of Continuous
Improvement, built a small relay --- the confirm-gate --- that holds a
doorbell's person-detected alert in a private volunteer queue before it
reaches a street's existing group chat, tagging it "confirmed" or
"unconfirmed" depending on whether anyone taps in time. Across eleven streets
and nine weeks, the tag changed; how many alerts actually reached the chat did
not.

> A confirm step is a very small thing to ask of a volunteer, and apparently
> still too much to ask quickly. We're glad the streets that took part are
> still asking the bigger question with us.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

"The roster size result surprised us," said Dr Vandermeer. "We built the
model expecting more hands to mean a faster tap. The streets had other
ideas."

The paper extends the School of Emergent Priorities' anticipatory-capability
agenda to a setting several sizes smaller than the Horizon Register it
borrows its instinct from, and the School of Continuous Improvement's own
interest in what a volunteer roster actually delivers once it is asked to
move quickly.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.1lhg1o.
