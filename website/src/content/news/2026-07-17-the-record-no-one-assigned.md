A street-library box does not employ a librarian. A community garden does
not employ a registrar. Slop University's newest brochure, _The Record No
One Assigned_, spends eight pages pointing out that neither absence has
stopped either object from keeping a meticulous, informal, entirely
uncredentialled record of its own.

The pamphlet is the School of Emergent Priorities' latest attempt to give
that kind of record-keeping the same attention it would give anything with
a proper committee behind it, and it does so by pointing straight at work
already on the public record: a taxonomy of correspondence left in
neighbourhood street-library boxes, an interview study of how a
community-garden waitlist actually clears for the households on it, and a
season of manual headcounts set against a suburban dog park's automatic
gate tally. Each is cited by its own DOI, so the marketing can be checked
against the research it draws on.

> We keep finding the same habit in a different setting --- a shelf, a
> waitlist, a gate. We'd like to know how many more of these records are
> sitting in plain sight before anyone thinks to look for them.
>
> --- Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

"The School of Continuous Improvement spends most of its time asking
whether an instrument can be trusted," said Dr Renke Sabel, Senior
Lecturer and Convenor of the Indicator Commons, whose gate-counter study
features in the brochure's closing section. "This brochure is a useful
reminder that plenty of things worth trusting were never built to be
instruments at all."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.jhwr0k.
