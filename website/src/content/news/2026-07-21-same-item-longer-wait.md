> A charity shop's window used to change on a Tuesday afternoon. It still
> does --- the University's researchers just had to wait a little longer to
> find out.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran, School of Emergent Priorities

A regional charity retailer's "Find of the Week" window has, for as long as
anyone on staff can recall, been decided by whichever volunteer happened to
be sorting donations that day. Late last year the network's coordinating
office replaced that habit with a nomination form and a rotating
three-volunteer panel, framed as a step toward a more considered, more
representative choice. A new Slop University paper set out to measure what
the panel actually changed.

Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran, Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke, and
Postdoctoral Fellow Dr Anouk Mensah tracked nineteen op shops for
twenty-four weeks either side of the changeover, coding each week's winning
item, counting how many volunteers' opinions it drew on, and timing how
long the choice took to reach the glass. The item that won stayed the same
kind of item, in roughly the same proportion, before and after the panel
arrived. What moved was the clock: a same-day pick became a four-day wait,
and stayed there for the rest of the study.

"We went looking for a panel that had quietly changed what shoppers see in
the window, and came back with a panel that had quietly changed how long
they wait to see it," said Dr Anouk Mensah. "Both are worth knowing before
the next process gets added on top."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.vi3n0q.
