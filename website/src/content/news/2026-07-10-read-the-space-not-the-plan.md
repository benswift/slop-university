A courtyard, a car park, a fridge door, and a bank of vending machines have
little in common except this: each was assigned a purpose in writing, and each
went on to do something else. A new brochure from Slop University argues that
the second fact deserves the same attention as the first.

Published this week by the Office of Research Outputs, the brochure gathers
four recently published studies as worked examples of a method the University's
two schools have arrived at from opposite directions. The School of Emergent
Priorities reaches it by following a priority from a scanning exercise to
whatever survives the planning cycle; the School of Continuous Improvement
reaches it by asking whether a dashboard tile moves for the reason the tile
suggests. Both end up in the same place, reading the record before the
document.

Slop University counts the assembling of that record a contribution in its own
right, distinct from any single finding it has produced.

"The interesting question was never whether a plan was sensible when it was
written," said Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics
Group in the School of Emergent Priorities. "It is what the space did next, and
whether anyone thought to go and look."

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab, welcomed
the questions the collection leaves open. "Four studies is not a doctrine. What
we have is a habit worth defending, and a growing pile of courtyards that
declined to behave."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.249n5r.
