Guests who collect Commencement's do-not-photograph wristband turn up in the
ceremony's official photo set at roughly a third the rate of everyone else. A
new paper from the School of Continuous Improvement went looking for whether
that made anyone trust the system more, and found it didn't.

> A label can do its job and still not get any credit for it. We'd like to
> understand why before we hang another one on something more important than
> a wristband.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab

Associate Professor Beng, Dr Mirela Hanke, and Dr Dagny Okafor coded the
University's published photo gallery from six ceremonies against the
check-in log, then surveyed a stratified sample of guests on how confident
they were that their photography preference had been honoured. Of 812
attending guests, 398 collected a wristband; wristband-wearers surfaced in
the record at 11 per cent, against 34 per cent for everyone else, a gap the
paper traces almost entirely to the wristband itself rather than where a
guest sat or which ceremony they attended.

"We've learned to stop assuming a system that changes behaviour will
automatically earn belief in return," said Dr Dagny Okafor, who convenes the
School's Evaluation of Evaluation program. "The two turned out to need
separate proof."

A further subsample of wristband-wearers were told, before completing the
confidence survey, that they had already been checked and found absent from
every published photo. Their confidence scores landed no higher than anyone
else's.

The School is adding the finding to its running account of labels that move
one thing and not the other, alongside the marker-caddy result the new paper
set out to test again, where a name on a placard moved confidence and left
the caddy untouched.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.m1vrow.
