A pigeonhole marked "cleared" on Slop University's mailroom log, and a
pigeonhole its addressee has actually visited, turn out to be two different
events more often than the log currently admits. A new paper from the
School of Emergent Priorities reports the gap.

Dr Fenna Okoro, Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, and Dr Anouk Mensah set
an unannounced fortnight of direct observation against the mailroom's own
binary record, comparing who was actually seen at the wall of 118 staff
pigeonholes with what the daily sweep alone would have reported. Four
distinct clearance habits emerged, from same-day collection to pigeonholes
the mailroom itself eventually has to empty, and the log's single flag
turned out to track only two of them.

> A flag that has been right for years doesn't need to be checked again,
> until the day it quietly stops being right for everyone.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register

Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow and a co-author on the paper, whose own
work follows how a phrase survives a program's language long after its
meaning has moved on, said the flag "hasn't changed its name once in years;
what changed is everyone's idea of what the name means."

The School reads the mismatch as a minor but useful entry in its own
anticipatory-capability agenda: knowing in advance which of an
institution's numbers will hold up before someone leans on them. The
mailroom's sweep continues unchanged.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.2iwb4d.
