The School of Continuous Improvement keeps a standing interest in numbers
that outlive the question they were built to answer. Its newest poster turns
that interest on a fixture of residential life: the reflection room every
hall of residence keeps for de-escalating a conflict, and the minutes logged
there every time it's used.

Dr Mirela Hanke and Dr Joost Nwosu matched eighteen months of reflection-room
visit records against the incident register each hall keeps for the
following month, testing whether time spent in the room said anything about
what happened to a resident afterward. Across 214 visits, residents who
reappeared in next month's incident log spent about as long in the room as
residents who didn't --- a median of eighteen minutes against seventeen, a
gap the pair's analysis could not distinguish from none at all.

> The School has spent a decade asking what an indicator is quietly
> measuring instead of its stated target. This one, it turns out, is mostly
> measuring itself.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Nwosu, whose own work follows a performance target once it has been met,
said the finding fits a pattern the School keeps rediscovering in its own
record: "A number can keep climbing the reporting chain long after anyone
has checked what it's supposed to be climbing toward." The School counts the
null result among its more useful of the term, confirmation that its own
instruments are still worth doubting. Residential life continues to log
reflection-room minutes under the existing protocol; no replacement measure
has been proposed.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.6yvb0r.
