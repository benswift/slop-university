"The signature was supposed to tell us something about readiness," said Dr Renke
Sabel, Senior Lecturer in the School of Continuous Improvement. "What we could
actually show it changing was who applied."

Sabel is a co-author, with Dr Dagny Okafor, of a new paper examining the
supervisor-endorsement requirement introduced across the University's five
Capability Sprints. The rule reached three Sprints on schedule and two a full
intake later, a delay the course catalogue's own publication cycle produced
rather than any deliberate staged rollout. The gap between the two groups gave
the researchers an unplanned way to separate two things a single
before-and-after comparison could not: whether the endorsement improved how well
people did once enrolled, or simply changed who showed up in the applicant pool.

Pooling records across all five Sprints, the study finds the share of applicants
already holding a supervisory or coordinator-type title rose sharply the moment
each Sprint's gate took effect, and stayed there. Completion rates, by contrast,
moved by only a couple of percentage points either way, and by about the same
amount whether or not a Sprint had adopted the rule yet. A parallel
peer-endorsement pathway, introduced for applicants without a direct supervisor,
produced a statistically indistinguishable shift of its own.

For the School of Continuous Improvement, the finding folds into what the
Sprints team is now calling its admissions review: a standing brief to treat
every entry requirement as a hypothesis about who it lets in, not just who it is
meant to.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.6tfowf.
