Slop University's newest publication is not a study. It is a brochure that
spends eight pages pointing at three studies already on the public record,
all of them examining the same small gap: the distance between what a badge,
a score, or a flag reports and what has actually happened underneath it.

The pamphlet, _The Second Look_, follows the School of Continuous
Improvement into a distribution centre, where a warehouse scanner's
pick-confidence score turned out to track a staff retraining calendar more
closely than the errors it was built to flag, and the School of Emergent
Priorities into a share house and a first-year tutorial room, where a
parcel-locker's "held" badge and a diagnostic table-colour placement both
outlasted the thing they were meant to describe. Each case is cited by its
own DOI, so the marketing can be checked against the research it draws on.

> Every system we look at this year has been confident about something.
> Confident about the right thing turns out to be a much shorter list.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead, Adaptive Metrics Lab, School of
> Continuous Improvement

"Two schools keep arriving at the same instinct from opposite directions,"
said Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics Group.
"One of us asks how long a settled claim stays settled. The other asks what
an instrument was actually built to notice. This brochure is what happens
when we compare notes."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.bv5co0.
