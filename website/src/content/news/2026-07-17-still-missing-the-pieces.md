A tick on a laminated card and an actual count of a board game's pieces
turn out to be measuring two different things, a new research poster from
the School of Emergent Priorities finds. Every game on the Union
Building common room's shelf carries a card the last borrower initials on
return, certifying the box complete against its own piece list. Nobody
had checked whether the card and the box agreed.

Over a full teaching term, the team surveyed borrowers as they lifted a
game off the shelf, rating how much they trusted its card before taking
it. Once the term ended, all thirty-four boxes were counted twice, blind
to their certification history, against each publisher's own list — the
first time the shelf's paperwork and its actual contents have been
reconciled. Borrower trust tracked a game's certification closely;
the audit did not. Games carrying a certified-complete card were still
missing a median of two pieces, statistically indistinguishable from
games with no card at all.

> What we didn't expect was how confidently people trusted a tick they'd
> never verified themselves. That's the question we'd like the rest of
> the shelf to sit with.
>
> --- Dr Petra Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Anouk Mensah, the poster's co-author, said the finding fits a pattern
the School keeps noticing in its own paperwork: "a label survives long
after anyone has checked whether it still describes the thing
underneath."

The Office of Research Outputs is filing the result alongside a running
tally of the University's self-certifying systems, and says the shelf's
card will stay exactly as it is while a photographed lay-out check is
trialled at return instead.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.qeh57z.
