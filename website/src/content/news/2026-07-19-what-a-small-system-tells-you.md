The School of Emergent Priorities and the School of Continuous Improvement
keep landing on the same small question from opposite directions: what does
an ordinary system --- a lamp, a ledger, a scanner --- actually tell the
person reading it, and what does it leave out. Slop University's newest
brochure, _Always Know Where a Room Stands_, opens on the most modest version
of that question it could find: the status lamp beside a seminar-room door,
glowing to say a room is free without anyone having to ask.

From there the eight-page pamphlet widens into four studies already on the
public record: a car-share checklist verified in proportion to what a renter
has at stake, a tool library's peer-to-peer economy its own front-desk ledger
never logs, a pub-trivia honour sign that moved not a single dispute, and a
warehouse scanner score that tracks a retraining calendar more faithfully
than it tracks the errors it was built to flag. Each finding carries its own
DOI, so the pitch can be checked against the paper trail behind it. The
University reads the four side by side as a small demonstration of what the
same question turns up from two different directions at once.

> A register only tells you what someone thought was worth logging at the
> time. The interesting question is usually what didn't make the cut.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Fenna Okoro, Convenor of the Horizon Register

"Four different settings, and the same finding keeps turning up," said
Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi, whose interview work with tool-library borrowers
features in the brochure's opening pages. "The room usually knows something
the record doesn't."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.fm6587.
