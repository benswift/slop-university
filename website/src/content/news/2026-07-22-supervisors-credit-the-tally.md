> Ask a supervisor what told them a child needed a friend today, and the
> tally is almost never the answer they reach for first.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation, School
> of Continuous Improvement

That gap is now on the record. A Slop University research poster published
today interviewed 48 playground supervisors across nineteen primary schools
--- eleven keeping a formal buddy-bench uptake tally, eight without one ---
on what actually tips them off that a child needs company at recess, then
set their answers against what each school's end-of-term inclusion report
tends to cite.

Interviews were transcribed and thematically coded, then double-coded by a
second researcher for consistency; the coding scheme was fixed before the
first interview ran, with no category added afterwards. Supervisors at
tally-keeping schools named the tally itself as their trigger in under one
coded transcript in ten. Their own observation --- "just knew," "saw them
at the fence twice" --- led both groups almost identically, at 58 and 61
per cent.

"The tally was built to satisfy a report, not to explain an afternoon on
the asphalt," said Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and Convenor of
Capability Sprints. "Both jobs matter. The transcripts just don't let us
pretend they're the same job."

The School of Continuous Improvement says the next round of interviews
will turn to whoever actually drafts the end-of-term report, transcripts
and tally alike.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.nuuzxk.
