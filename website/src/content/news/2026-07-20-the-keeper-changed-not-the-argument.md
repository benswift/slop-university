> A ledger doesn't need to be wrong to be argued about; it only needs to
> change hands.
>
> --- Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Every babysitting co-op runs on the same quiet promise: put in an evening,
take one out, and let the ledger keep the score. Slop University's newest
research poster asks what survives that promise once the person keeping the
ledger hands it to somebody else. A field trial across 41 co-ops finds the
handover format barely moves the outcome — a written template draws about as
many disputes as a keeper explaining it over the fence.

The finding extends the School of Emergent Priorities' standing interest in
what a role actually carries forward once its occupant steps back, an
agenda Dr Chikere has built much of her research around.

Incoming ledger-keepers were randomly assigned a structured handover
template or the outgoing keeper's own freeform account, and disputes were
logged fortnightly across the following ten weeks at all 41 co-ops. Balance
disagreements, forgotten rate exceptions, and disputed session counts held
at comparable rates under both conditions.

"Whatever changes hands in a handover, it evidently isn't the thing that
starts the argument," said Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow, School
of Emergent Priorities, a co-author on the work.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xxp0r0.
