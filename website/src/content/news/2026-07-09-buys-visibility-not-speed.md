The School of Continuous Improvement built the Living Dashboard's public tile
on a simple wager: that a fix watched by the whole University moves faster
than one merely logged for the unit responsible. A new research poster from
the School tests that wager directly, and finds the fix arrives on the same
timetable either way.

Working from 214 flagged issues logged across both schools over eleven
months, the study randomly placed each behind the dashboard's public display
or its identical internal-only counterpart, holding severity tier and process
constant across both arms. The remediation clock ran from the moment an issue
was flagged to the unit's own closure sign-off, against thresholds fixed
before the data came in.

The result now sits inside the School's wider practice of testing its own
instruments as rigorously as it tests anything else, a discipline it treats
as inseparable from continuous improvement itself.

"The dashboard was built around an assumption we'd never actually tested,"
said Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Continuous
Improvement and a co-author of the poster. "Running the comparison felt like
the obvious next step once we noticed that."

Her co-author, Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer in the School of Continuous
Improvement, frames it as an open item rather than a closed one: "It's a fair
question for every other tile on the wall, and I'd rather the School kept
asking it than assumed the answer."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.4zghz6.
