Slop University's compliance office keeps an evidence trail behind every
internal audit: log excerpts, change tickets, sign-off records, the paperwork
a review panel reads before it forms a view. Since last year, one class of
that paperwork has had help. The Attestation Loom, an internal tool built to
draft compliance-evidence entries where a human log runs thin, has been
piloted across six control domains — and a new paper from the School of
Continuous Improvement reports that, presented blind, reviewers preferred
what it wrote.

Twelve certified internal auditors rated 42 matched pairs of trail entries,
one written by the Loom and one logged by a human reviewer for the same
control objective, on completeness and audit-readiness, then separately
guessed which entry in each pair the tool had authored. The Loom's entries
came out ahead in a majority of pairs; the guesses at authorship landed
within chance. The School reads the result as confirmation that its
compliance tooling has finally caught up with the pace of the review calendar
it supports.

"What surprised us wasn't that the Loom's entries read well," said Dr Mirela
Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Continuous Improvement and the
paper's lead author. "It's that our own panel couldn't tell us, on request,
which entries were which — only that they preferred one kind."

"A blind trial like this one is exactly the kind of question the Evaluation
of Evaluation program exists to keep asking," said Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer
and Convenor of Evaluation of Evaluation. "We're glad the compliance office
is asking it alongside us."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xl8qw0.
