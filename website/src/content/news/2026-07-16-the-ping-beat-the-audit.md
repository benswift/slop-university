Facilities' capital-planning process runs on numbers nobody outside the
unit sees from one term to the next; this term, for the first time, one of
them came from a University-built instrument only ten weeks old. A new
research poster from the School of Continuous Improvement, _Counted Before
It's Checked_, reports on a clip-on power-and-motion logger fitted to 22
shared lecture-hall AV carts, built to test whether the activity it counts
as a "ping" lines up with an actual booked class. Facilities began citing
the logger's raw totals in capital-planning minutes within three weeks,
well before the poster's own validation exercise had finished running.

The audit set ten weeks of logged pings against the room-booking system's
confirmed class list, tracking how closely the two moved once cart users
became aware the count mattered.

> We'd rather a number's shortcomings surface while it's still only
> informing a budget, not after one's been signed.
>
> --- Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Indicator Commons,
> School of Continuous Improvement

By the pilot's tenth week, only 38% of logged pings lined up with a
confirmed booking, down from 71% in its first — a gap the School traces
less to the logger itself than to what everyone around it learned to do
once they knew it was there. "A number moves fastest when it's still new
enough that nobody's asked where it came from," said Dr Sten Okwuosa,
Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway Atlas. "We'd like the
validated version to catch up before the business case does."

The release forms part of the School's continuing effort to test its own
instruments before anyone outside it has to.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zhqdoo.
