A queue outside the library's laptop-charging lockers used to mean one
thing: a flat battery before a lecture. Sustainability's newest reading of
that queue means something else --- an early figure in its energy-planning
pack for how much AI-tool computing has moved onto students' own laptops. A
new poster from the School of Continuous Improvement checks the figure
against the students actually queuing.

Dr Mirela Hanke and Dr Sten Okwuosa intercepted 238 students across five
library charging-locker banks over a fourteen-week term, asking each why
they were waiting, then matched the answers against the lockers' own
firmware battery-draw logs and the term's assessment calendar. The draw
index climbed thirty-eight per cent over the term, moving within a day of
every one of six assessment deadlines. What students said about AI tools
added almost nothing once the deadline calendar was already in the model.

> A locker only ever tells you it's plugged in. Everything else about why
> is a story we added afterward.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Okwuosa, whose Impact Pathway Atlas traces how a number outlives the
question it was built to answer, said the survey answers were the least
surprising part of the file. "Everyone queuing at a charging locker will
tell you the same thing if you stop and ask," he said. "They're there to
charge something."

Sustainability has not withdrawn the draw index from its current
energy-planning pack. For now, the pair say, a queue at a charging locker
is the only line item in that pack anyone has actually gone and asked about.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.uovoza.
