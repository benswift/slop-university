A disclosure sticker only proves itself once someone acts on it. A new Slop
University paper puts a number on how often that happens at a real-estate
shopfront, and the number is smaller than the sticker's own compliance box
would suggest.

Researchers from the School of Emergent Priorities and the School of
Continuous Improvement fitted a short printed code to 187 qualifying listing
cards across nine agency branches over fourteen weeks, tracking not just
whether a passer-by scanned it but what they did once the code's comparison
page loaded. Half the cards carried an enlarged version of the same code, a
low-cost check on whether size was ever the real obstacle.

> A notice can be entirely accurate and still not do the one thing everyone
> assumes it's doing, which is being read.
>
> --- Dr Runa Adegoke, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Strategic Drift Survey,
> School of Emergent Priorities

Scans arrived steadily across the fourteen weeks, and a larger code drew no
more of them than the standard one did. Only a small share of visitors went
on to use the comparison page's own side-by-side toggle, the one action that
put the original and the adjusted photograph in front of someone at once ---
a share that held almost identical whether the branch sat on a busy strip or
a quiet one. Dr Marit Osayande, Lecturer and Convenor of the Living
Dashboard, said the pattern felt familiar: "the instrument was never really
the hard part; getting anyone to look at it was."

The University reads the result as one more entry in its wider case that a
compliance instrument's presence and its function are rarely the same
question.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xzcpwq.
