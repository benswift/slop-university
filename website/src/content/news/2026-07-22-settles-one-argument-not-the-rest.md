Twenty-two taxi ranks, three ground-transportation precincts, and one very
specific argument: whose turn it is. A new Slop University paper finds that
installing a numbered-ticket dispenser ends that argument almost entirely,
and uncovers a second one waiting exactly where the dispenser doesn't reach.

Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran of the School of Emergent Priorities, with
Dr Fenna Okoro, Convenor of the School's Horizon Register, and Associate
Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab in the School of
Continuous Improvement, coded 397 disputes across 3,204 boarding cycles over
a 14-week season, sorting each into a seven-category taxonomy built to tell
an arrival-order dispute apart from the quieter disagreements over break
re-entry, group fares, and shift changes. Ranks with a dispenser saw
arrival-order disputes fall from 6.1 to 0.4 per 100 cycles. Total disputes
per 100 cycles, across every category combined, barely moved at all.

> We expected the dispenser to close the subject of boarding order entirely.
> Instead there's a second argument sitting right behind the first one,
> waiting for its own instrument.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran, School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Beng, who leads the Adaptive Metrics Lab, said the taxonomy "gives
Continuous Improvement a cleaner way to ask what a measurement instrument
actually fixed, instead of assuming a category removed is a problem solved."

Two field coders tracked each rank across the full season, logging every
audible or visible disagreement about who boards next and checking their
coding scheme against a stripped-down, three-category version of itself. The
scheme held up regardless, which the team takes as a sign the displacement
belongs to the ranks and not to how finely the researchers chose to slice
their categories. The finding joins the School of Emergent Priorities'
growing account of where a formal instrument earns its keep, and where it
mostly just decides which argument a rank is still allowed to have.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.236v83.
