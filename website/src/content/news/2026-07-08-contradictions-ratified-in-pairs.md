When two horizon-scan signals point in opposite directions, Slop University's
Horizon Register logs them the way it logs everything else: separately, one
entry at a time, and without remark. A new poster from the School of Emergent
Priorities sorts five years of those opposite-pointing pairs into a catalogue
and finds the register keeps its silence right through to ratification.

The study read 214 contradictory signal-pairs that arrived in the same scanning
cycle across 21 quarters, typed each against a four-part scheme by two
independent coders, and traced every pair through to a Futures in Committee
decision. Two in five were ratified as separate standing priorities within two
cycles of first appearing; directional contradictions, the sharpest kind,
crossed over at the highest rate of any type. In none of the ratified cases had
the register's own contradiction-flag field ever recorded that the two entries
pulled against each other.

The University puts the catalogue forward as the sort of housekeeping it would
rather do in the open than leave for someone else to notice, on a register it
keeps precisely because a scanning process accumulates more than any committee
reads at once.

"The Committee has always adjudicated a signal on its own merits, which is
exactly what it is asked to do," said Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor
of Futures in Committee in the School of Emergent Priorities. "What this
catalogue shows us is how much we were never in a position to see, because the
two entries were never once put on the table together."

Dr Petra Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Emergent Priorities,
placed the count within her own work on how a record's categories decide what it
can register. "You can only miss a contradiction the instrument has no field
for," she said. "Once we gave the pairs a category of their own, they stopped
being invisible and started being a number."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.pu0w2f.
