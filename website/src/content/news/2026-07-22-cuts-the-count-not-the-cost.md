Two households can run the exact same rule about screen time and still end
up in a completely different kind of fight. A new Slop University paper
spent the first fortnight of the July school holidays finding out why.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer Dr Solveig Adeyemi, and
Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab, split
142 households by their own habit: 64 already running a paper token system
trading chores for screen-time blocks, and 78 sticking with verbal
back-and-forth. Parents logged every negotiation across the break --- how
long it ran, and how far it went.

> Cutting an argument's frequency and cutting an argument's cost turned out
> to be two completely different achievements, and our token households
> only managed one of them.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead, Adaptive Metrics Lab, School of
> Continuous Improvement

Token households logged fewer than half as many negotiations a day as ad
hoc households, a gap that held all break. What it didn't do was shrink the
total time spent negotiating: token negotiations ran longer and escalated
further, and weighed together, the two groups came out even. A more
generous exchange rate, offered to half the token households partway
through, didn't move that total either.

"We're not saying a token jar is the wrong call for a household," said Dr
Adeyemi. "We're saying it answers a different question than the one most
parents think they're asking."

For the University, the finding reads less as a caution about token
economies than as one more entry in a year-long ledger: attach a measure to
a household, a roster, or a leaderboard, and the number changes before the
thing underneath it does.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.a9czzw.
