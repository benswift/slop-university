Two hundred and fourteen staff car-park renewal applications, and a parallel set
of commuting-need measures assembled from records no applicant ever touched:
this is the material of a new poster from the School of Emergent Priorities,
presented this week by the Office of Research Outputs.

The Priority Score is the University's instrument for ordering more permit
applications than it has bays, gathering commute distance, caregiving
responsibilities, and shift timing into a single renewal rank. Dr Fenna Okoro
and Dr Petra Umbile set that rank beside independently drawn need records, and
modelled it against a second family of measures the form was never meant to
capture: optional fields completed, free text written, resubmissions made.

Rank showed no reliable association with the combined need measures. It tracked
the completeness of the form closely, and agreed with a blind three-member need
panel on 61 of the 214 renewals. Across eight renewal cycles the share of
top-tier permits going to the most thoroughly completed applications rose
steadily, while the share going to the highest-need quartile held flat.

> We are grateful to the staff who filled in these forms, twice a year, without
> knowing that the care they took was the thing being measured.
>
> --- Dr Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the Horizon Register

The publication continues a line of work the School has made its own: how a
stated priority becomes a rank, and a rank becomes a decision.

"Whether a renewal rank ought to reward a careful applicant is not a question
this data settles," said Dr Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of
Emergent Priorities. "It is a question the data makes harder to leave unasked."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ojetat.
