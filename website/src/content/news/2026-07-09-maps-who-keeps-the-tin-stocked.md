When the tea room's biscuit tin runs dry between deliveries, staff do not wait
for the contracted supplier to catch up. A new paper from the School of Emergent
Priorities finds they have quietly built their own supply chain instead.

Dr Marek Solheim and Dr Ingrid Vasseur interviewed twenty-seven professional and
academic staff about what happens on their floor once the Authorised Restocking
Protocol's single contracted supplier falls behind the fortnightly visits it
promises. Thematic coding of the interviews, checked against weeks of
independent field observation, sorted what staff described into four
recognisable workarounds --- a lent key, a quietly cut duplicate, a defeated
lock, and a parallel stash kept outside the tin altogether --- distributed
differently across professional and academic roles depending on how close each
sits to the supplier relationship.

"A protocol is a commitment with a use-by date nobody wrote on it," said Dr
Solheim. "The network we found is what happens after that date passes and nobody
has said so out loud."

The School of Emergent Priorities reads the finding as consistent with its wider
interest in how a settlement outlives the conditions that justified it, without
needing to settle who, if anyone, should now revisit the Protocol itself.

"Staff did not describe themselves as getting around anything," said Dr Vasseur.
"They described a tin that needed to stay full, and a key that happened to be
the fastest way to keep it that way."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.krw1eq.
