A laminated index card taped beside the University Library's parcel-locker
wall has spent most of a term doing a job nobody assigned it: telling
students which compartment their delivery actually ended up in, after
Facilities' overnight reassignment system quietly moved it. A new paper
from the School of Continuous Improvement finds the card was doing more of
that job than the system itself.

The system in question, an overnight Compartment Rebalancer commissioned by
the School and Facilities together, redistributes held parcels each night
to even out wear across the wall's ninety-six compartments and keep queues
from clustering at popular banks. It runs without a human sign-off step,
and by design never repeats the pickup notification a student receives at
drop-off --- a choice made to protect the University's shared messaging
budget rather than a student's certainty about where their parcel is.

> A wear target met on schedule looks like success from every angle we
> measured. It took a taped index card to show us which angle we hadn't.
>
> --- Dr Joost Nwosu, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

The School regards the deployment as a model of what an unattended system
can achieve when its objective is narrow and its metrics are watched
closely --- though, as the authors are the first to note, watched by more
than the system itself.

"We've asked Facilities whether the list can be formally adopted rather
than removed on the next notice-board clearance," said Senior Lecturer
Thandiwe Solberg. "It's currently keeping something running that the
University paid to have running on its own."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.edlvio.
