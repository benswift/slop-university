A vet clinic's morning queue board is meant to settle an easy question
before anyone has to ask it: whoever's carrier went up first goes in first.
A new Slop University research poster spent a season checking how often
that's actually true.

Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran and Lecturer Dr Iben Chikere, both of the
School of Emergent Priorities, logged 346 queue events across six suburban
clinics over an eleven-week autumn season, timing each posted queue order
against who was actually called next. Where the two diverged, two
independent raters sorted the reason into one of four categories, then
checked each category against the gap between what the clinic quoted at
check-in and how long the wait actually ran.

> The board isn't lying to anyone. It's just not the thing deciding who
> goes next, and once you've coded a season of mornings you can see exactly
> what is.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran, School of Emergent Priorities

The taxonomy explained more than three times as much of the season's
quoting error as the raw override count did. One category, an animal
presenting visible distress, accounted for less than a quarter of the
divergences logged but close to half the minutes clinics under-quoted by.

"Every clinic already senses which mornings run long," said Dr Chikere.
"The taxonomy gives them a reason attached to the number, not just the
number."

The poster extends the School of Emergent Priorities' standing interest in
the gap between a posted arrangement and the judgement calls quietly
running underneath it.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.niwmql.
