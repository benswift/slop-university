A childcare centre's daily "healthy pack" sticker gets awarded at drop-off,
before anyone so much as lifts the lid. A new Slop University paper asks
what the sticker is actually rewarding, and how fast a family works out the
answer.

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab, led the
work with Dr Mirela Hanke and Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and Convenor
of Capability Sprints, all of the School of Continuous Improvement. The
team interviewed 34 parents and 11 room educators across six centres about
what earns the sticker, then ran a separate fourteen-week photographic
audit of lunchbox contents, scored against a nutrition rubric the centres
never saw.

> A sticker only has a few seconds to make its case. Ours turns out to
> spend every one of them on the fruit sitting on top, never on what's
> underneath it.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead, Adaptive Metrics Lab, School
> of Continuous Improvement

Parents and educators converged on the same handful of cues --- a whole
piece of fruit, a bright wrapper, an unpackaged snack, a note from home ---
and the sticker-award rate climbed from 38% to 81% of lunchboxes across the
audit window. The independent rubric score, checked against the same
lunchboxes, barely moved. A revision partway through, meant to make the
criteria harder to read at a glance, changed only how many days a family
took to notice.

"We'd like to know whether a slower check, one that doesn't have to decide
in the time it takes to walk past a desk, would ask anything different of a
family," said Dr Hanke.

The School of Continuous Improvement counts the four-cue consensus as the
kind of small, stable finding its evaluation-ecosystem agenda depends on: a
check learned by everyone involved, in a matter of weeks, well before
anyone thought to ask what it was actually for.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.fsaovv.
