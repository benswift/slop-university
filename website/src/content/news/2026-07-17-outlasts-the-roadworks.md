A strip of tape laid down to steer pedestrians around a footpath
resurfacing job is authorised for exactly one purpose: surviving the job. A
new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities finds it usually outlives
that purpose by months.

Professor Verity Marris, Dr Marek Solheim, and Dr Lindiwe Achterberg tracked
41 taped arrow-sets across nine shopping strips for eighteen months,
checking each set's continued presence against the works order that
authorised it. Where a removal deadline was specified at all, it made no
measurable difference to how long a set survived. Most of the removals that
did happen came about only when unrelated roadworks returned to the same
stretch of footpath, not because anyone had reviewed the marking itself.

> A works order gets a job done and stops there. Nobody wrote down whose job
> it was to notice the tape was still up.
>
> --- Professor Verity Marris, Director, Trajectory Analytics Group, School
> of Emergent Priorities

"The interesting question now is who a works order should name as
responsible once the crew has gone," said Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer,
School of Emergent Priorities. "At the moment, the answer is nobody, and
the tape just stays up."

The finding extends the School's long-running interest in the conditions
institutions attach to their own temporary measures, and in what happens
once those conditions are no longer anyone's to check.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ic6zdv.
