Presenting a failure at Improvement Grand Rounds does not appear to make it
happen again any less often. What the seminar reliably changes is how soon the
presenter's own team is asked back to the podium.

Dr Bram Ntuli, who convenes the seminar, and Dr Osei Vandermeer, Convenor of the
Review Cadence Observatory, paired four years of the School of Continuous
Improvement's incident register against the Observatory's own rostering log. Of
sixty-one logged failures eligible for the podium, thirty-four were selected;
recurrence ran no lower among the cases that were presented than among the cases
the rotation never reached. What moved instead was the interval before the
presenting unit's next invitation, which lengthened with each prior appearance
regardless of how full the unit's incident log stayed.

The University places the audit within its wider commitment to instrumenting its
own rituals as rigorously as it instruments everyone else's, on the view that a
seminar built to catch a failure is not exempt from being checked for one.

"Convening this seminar for as long as I have, you develop a private theory
about what it is actually doing," said Dr Ntuli. "This is the first time I have
checked mine against the roster instead of against my own impression of the
room."

"A scheduling gap is a strange thing to be the most reliable finding in a paper
about failure," said Dr Vandermeer. "We are not yet in a position to say why the
roster behaves this way, and we think that is the more interesting question to
leave open."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.qpc8x3.
