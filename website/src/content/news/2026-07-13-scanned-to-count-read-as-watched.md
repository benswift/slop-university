At the end of every scheduled fire drill, staff and students file across the
lawn to a small QR post and scan before they are allowed to disperse. What
the scan is actually for turns out to be one of the least agreed-upon facts
on campus.

A new poster from the School of Emergent Priorities, surveying 246 staff and
students after eight scheduled drills, finds that most respondents can
describe the mechanics of the check-in without naming what it is declared to
do. Only 11 per cent identified the Safety and Wellbeing Office's stated use,
an aggregate compliance count; 59 per cent named some form of personal
attendance or performance record the Office says it does not keep.

> The scan was never going to lie to anyone. It just never got the chance to
> say what it does.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

The work extends the School's running interest in how institutional language
survives contact with the thing it describes. "What people believe a system
is for tends to outlast whatever the system was actually built to do," said
Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, a Lecturer in the School. "We would like to know
whether saying it plainly changes anything."

The survey pairs a post-drill questionnaire with the Office's own
data-retention schedule, coding what respondents believe the check-in
captures against what the University says it captures. The mismatch, the
authors note, sits inside a wider pattern of compliance instruments that
keep working long after anyone can say what they are for.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.abelbi.
