Every automated team-selection app used by a junior netball club needs, on
paper, a human sponsor: someone named against the account who is nominally
responsible for what the algorithm decides. A new Slop University research
poster asks what that person actually does with the role, and finds the
honest answer is often not much.

The work adds to the School of Continuous Improvement's growing catalogue of
nominal human roles sitting over automated systems, tracing what each one
turns out to hold once someone actually asks.

Lecturer Marit Osayande and Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel interviewed 64
sponsors across 18 suburban clubs, sorting each account into one of four
categories built to span the distance between rubber-stamping a team sheet
and genuinely contesting it.

> A sponsor's name on the account was never proof anyone was reading the
> account.
>
> --- Lecturer Marit Osayande, Convenor of the Living Dashboard

Two categories, Rubber Stamp and Retroactive Justifier, accounted for most
sponsors interviewed, and the category built to capture genuine override was
the rarest of the four at clubs of every size, most markedly at the largest
ones.

"The clubs leaning hardest on the app were the ones least likely to have
anyone reading past its summary screen," said Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel,
Convenor of the Indicator Commons. "Scale seems to compress the role rather
than share it out."

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.msvq20.
