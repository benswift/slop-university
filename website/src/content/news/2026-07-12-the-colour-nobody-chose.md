A first-year tutorial table carries three things: chairs, a shared laptop
charger, and a single flat disc in gold, black, or grey that nobody at the
table chose. A new poster from the School of Emergent Priorities asks how
much weight that last object is still carrying by week nine.

The project set the week-one diagnostic score that fixed each student's
table colour against a blinded week-nine tutorial mark, across 214
first-year students and nine weeks of assessment in one teaching period.
Dr Lindiwe Achterberg and Dr Anouk Mensah found the colour's explanatory
power thin from two-fifths of the spread in early marks to under a tenth
by the end of term, while the reassignment flag meant to catch that drift
barely moved.

> Once a colour is assigned, undoing it turns out to need more evidence
> than assigning it ever did.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer

Dr Anouk Mensah, whose research traces how a priority's language outlives
the priority itself, said the table colours were "the kind of label that
keeps meaning something to the roster long after it has stopped meaning
anything to the room."

The University frames the finding as an invitation to check its own
smaller-scale sorting habits, table by table, rather than a verdict on any
one teaching team.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.jkkydn.
