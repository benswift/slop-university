The School of Emergent Priorities has spent years asking what a stated
attitude actually predicts once there's a log to check it against. Its
newest research poster picks a fittingly ordinary instrument: the trust
survey handed to supermarket shoppers about the self-checkout's
item-recognition prompt.

Dr Iben Chikere, Dr Mirela Hanke, and Associate Professor Casimir Beng
surveyed 238 shoppers across four store sites on how much they trusted the
kiosk's error prompt, then linked each respondent to twelve weeks of their
own till-side override-button use. Override rate held inside a five-point
band regardless of whether a shopper reported low, medium, or high trust in
the prompt, and shopper frequency made no difference either way.

> We built an instrument to measure something a shopper already does with
> their hand every week. It turns out the two were never talking to each
> other.
>
> --- Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

"The trust score passed every check we ran on the survey itself," said
Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "It
just doesn't reach the till."

The team is treating the null result as a finding in its own right rather
than a study to redo: a shopper's stated confidence and their override
hand, it turns out, answer to different things entirely, and the poster
reports the gap without proposing to close it.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.9pvjpl.
