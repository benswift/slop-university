A badge reader beside a repainted corridor door doesn't know why anyone
hesitates in front of it. Facilities' newest index believes it does. A new
poster from the School of Continuous Improvement checks that belief against
fourteen weeks of the corridor's own badge-reader logs.

The Link Building's east-west corridor lost its directional arrows to a
commissioned mural in week eight of the study period. Complaints to the
Service Desk spiked within days, and Facilities responded by publishing a
weekly confusion index, scored from complaint volume alone. Senior Lecturer
Renke Sabel and Dr Osei Vandermeer set that index against the corridor's
badge-reader transit times across the same fourteen weeks and found the two
tracking only weakly: transit time spiked the week of the repaint and
recovered within a fortnight; the index has not moved since.

> An indicator that stays high after the thing it was measuring goes back
> to normal isn't measuring that thing anymore. It's measuring itself.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel, School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Vandermeer, who convenes the School's Review Cadence Observatory, called
the corridor "a small, tidy case of a review cadence that only runs in one
direction" — one his own work keeps turning up elsewhere on campus, in
slower and less countable forms.

The University folds the finding into the same evaluation-ecosystem work
that produced the index in the first place, one instrument checking a
neighbour before either reaches a committee. Facilities has not yet said
whether the index will be retired or recalibrated.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.q7mkxv.
