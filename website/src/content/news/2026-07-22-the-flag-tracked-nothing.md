A flag went up over six mornings in ten this season at six suburban pools,
and by every measure the University checked, nothing else moved with it. A
new Slop University research poster reports one of the cleaner null results
of the year.

Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel, Convenor of the Indicator Commons, and
Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu spent a nine-week
smoke-affected season pairing each of 402 duty mornings' flag decisions
against same-day patron-reported symptoms, gate-tallied attendance, and the
government air-quality reading the flag is meant to translate for a
rostered volunteer.

Symptom-reporting held within a percentage point or two of baseline whether
the flag was up or down that morning, as did attendance. A continuous
re-scoring of the underlying air-quality reading performed no better than
the flag itself, and a pre-registered pass across weekday, weekend, and
school-holiday programs turned up nothing further.

> A flag is a small, cheap thing to raise. What it teaches us about the
> rest of the morning is turning out to be an even smaller one.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel, Convenor, Indicator Commons, School of
> Continuous Improvement

"A null result this clean doesn't end the question, it just clears the
ground for a better one," said Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and Convenor
of Capability Sprints. The finding joins a wider seasonal pattern the
School of Continuous Improvement has been assembling all year: instruments
trusted well past what they have been shown to track.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zllgyn.
