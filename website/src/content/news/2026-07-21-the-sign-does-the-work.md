A number posted next to a box of paperbacks turns out to answer a question
nobody living near it was actually asking. Little free libraries have begun
displaying a running take-versus-leave ratio, offered up as a kind of
health score for the neighbourhood's own generosity. A new Slop University
research poster went looking for what that score actually predicts, and
found it predicting almost nothing at all.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu and Dr Marit Osayande, Lecturer
and Convenor of the Living Dashboard, surveyed 214 registered users across
38 suburban boxes over fourteen weeks, matching each respondent's own
five-point rating of the box against its live posted ratio at the time,
while separately logging whether the placard itself could be read from the
footpath. Users rating a box "essential" or "rarely used" split almost
identically whether the posted ratio was generous or stingy. The one thing
that reliably separated respondents was whether they could recall the
number at all, which tracked the placard's legibility far more closely
than the ratio it displayed.

The School of Continuous Improvement counts the result among the year's
more useful non-findings — the kind that quietly closes off an explanation
everyone had been reaching for.

> We went in expecting the ratio to be doing more work than it turned out
> to be doing. That is usually where the more interesting finding is
> hiding.
>
> --- Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and Convenor, Capability Sprints,
> School of Continuous Improvement

"The ratio didn't need to be wrong to be beside the point," said Dr Marit
Osayande. "Keeping the glass clean and the placard at eye height turns out
to matter more than what the placard says."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.5l833a.
