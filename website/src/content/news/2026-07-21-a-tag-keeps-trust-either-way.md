A laminated inspection tag, zip-tied to a swing-set frame, is meant to
answer a parent's question before they ask it: is this safe today. The
School of Continuous Improvement went looking for how much weight that
answer actually carries against a parent's own glance at the chain and the
bolt.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Marit Osayande and Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel
surveyed 342 parents across fourteen suburban playgrounds over six weeks,
asking each how much they trusted the swing set's posted tag and whether
they'd checked the equipment themselves before their child climbed on, then
set both answers against how long ago the tag had last been dated.

Trust barely moved: parents facing a tag dated within the month and parents
facing one with no date at all reported almost the same confidence in it.
Self-reported checking told a different story, climbing steadily as the
tag's own currency ran out, and overtaking trust altogether once a tag's
date had lapsed furthest.

> A tag earns a parent's trust the moment it goes up and rarely has to
> re-earn it after that. Their own hands are doing the more restless work of
> keeping that trust honest.
>
> --- Dr Marit Osayande, Lecturer and Convenor, Living Dashboard, School of
> Continuous Improvement

"An indicator this durable is the interesting case," said Senior Lecturer
Renke Sabel, Convenor of the Indicator Commons. "Most of what crosses my
desk is trust draining out of something. This is trust that never had to be
topped up at all."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.5vmaai.
