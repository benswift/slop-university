> A name that turns up twice in the log tells duty staff something a
> capacity count never could, and by week twelve of the term, that was
> most of what the log was doing.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

The overflow desk near the Learning Commons' entrance has directed noisy
laptop typists and phone callers away from the shared reading floor for
longer than anyone currently staffing it can date. Its referral log was
introduced alongside a capacity-planning brief, meant to tell Library
Services when peak-period demand justified more desks like it. A new
research poster from the School of Emergent Priorities set out to check
whether that brief still holds.

It mostly doesn't. Coded against 187 log entries and interviews with nine
duty staff across a twelve-week term, the capacity-overflow reason fell
from 58% of entries in week one to 24% by week twelve, while an unlisted
"repeat name" flag rose from 8% to 52% over the same stretch, overtaking
every reason the intake form actually offers. Duty staff describe
consulting the log to see whether a name has come up before, a use nowhere
in its founding rationale.

Co-author Dr Lindiwe Achterberg placed the finding alongside the School's
wider interest in provisions that outlast the trial they were meant to
end --- pilots, she noted, rarely announce the moment they stop being
pilots. The University counts the finding among the more useful
corrections quietly emerging from its own record-keeping, and is treating
the log's next phase as an open question rather than a foregone
reinstatement of its original brief.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.th7iml.
