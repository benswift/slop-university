A computing cluster's exhaust has, on most campuses, exactly one job: to leave
the building as fast and as quietly as possible. Slop University has published
a case-for-support brochure making the argument that its own exhaust should
have two.

The brochure, titled _Nothing Goes to Waste_, sets out a line of insulated
pipe running from the Adaptive Metrics Lab's server room to a glasshouse
behind the same building, and the monthly reading Facilities now takes of what
grows there. The University treats the arrangement as one instance of a wider
habit: reading facilities and research infrastructure as a single connected
system, so that what one part of the campus generates, another part is made to
use.

> A pipe running somewhere useful isn't a finished argument on its own. It's
> an invitation to keep watching it, and to help us watch more of them.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead, Adaptive Metrics Lab

Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the Horizon Register, welcomed
the brochure's case for treating the cluster's growth as a standing planning
item, saying computing capacity "earns exactly the same entry any other
approaching priority gets."

The publication draws its argument from the University's own published
record: an energy audit that took a building's own dashboard at its word
before checking it, a look inside the Register the glasshouse now shares
space on, and a study of how two reasonable targets can drift apart once set
on separate clocks. Read together, the brochure argues, they make the case
that a research capability keeps paying its way in directions nobody planned
for at the outset --- provided someone keeps a reviewed figure on it.

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.78ftbn.
