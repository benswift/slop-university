Slop University's bike-rack permit register has risen in every quarter on
record. Not one quarter, across two and a half years, has seen it fall — even as
some of the people renewing that permit told researchers, when asked directly,
that they had stopped riding in months or years ago.

The finding comes from a new poster by the School of Continuous Improvement,
presented this week by the Office of Research Outputs. Dr Renke Sabel and Dr
Osei Vandermeer interviewed 38 active permit-holders, drawn from across the
University's buildings, about their current riding habits, then set what they
said against the register the sustainability office treats as its
cycling-participation index.

Riding frequency reported in interview showed only a weak relationship with
permit status. The register, meanwhile, tracked something else entirely: whether
a renewal had lapsed on paper, not whether a bicycle had been used. Cancelling a
permit currently requires a form, and nothing in the renewal cycle prompts
anyone to file one.

An indicator that only ever climbs is not obviously a success, and the School
would rather say so in its own newsroom than leave the finding for someone else
to make.

> An indicator survives by never being wrong in a way anyone has to act on. This
> one has never had to be wrong, because nobody ever checks what it's still
> counting.
>
> --- Dr Sabel, Senior Lecturer in the School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor of the Review Cadence Observatory,
welcomed the comparison. "We talk about closing the loop between what's sensed
and what's reported," he said. "This is a loop that only closes in one
direction: registrations flow in, nothing flows back out."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.qjaeiq.
