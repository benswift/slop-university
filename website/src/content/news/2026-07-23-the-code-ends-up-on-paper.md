Forty-two share houses agreed to test whether a phone that will not stop
nagging beats a notice taped to the fridge. A new paper from the School of
Continuous Improvement reports what happened when each house's shared door
code came up for its regular rotation.

Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Renke Sabel led the work with Lecturer Dr
Marit Osayande and Dr Joost Nwosu, all of the School of Continuous
Improvement. Half the houses received push notifications and text messages
counting down to each scheduled code change; the rest kept the letting
cooperative's existing paper notice, and a researcher photographed each
house's entry area every week for any sign the new code had made it onto
paper of its own.

> A phone can remind you as often as it likes. It cannot make the reminding
> feel like the household's own idea, and that turns out to matter more than
> the count.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Renke Sabel, Indicator Commons, School
> of Continuous Improvement

The reminders made no measurable difference. A rotated code turned up as a
sticky note, a whiteboard scrawl, or a taped index card within a similar
handful of days either way, and doubling how often the reminder arrived
moved the outcome by hours rather than days.

"We went into this expecting the nagging to at least buy a day or two," said
Dr Osayande, who convenes the School's Living Dashboard. "What we got
instead was a household that had already worked out its own way of passing
the code along, long before our notifications had anything useful to add."

The University says a null result earns exactly the same repository entry as
a positive one, and means to keep it that way.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.l3fxz8.
