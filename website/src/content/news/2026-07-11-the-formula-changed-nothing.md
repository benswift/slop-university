Slop University's Parking Office promised a fairer way to ration campus
parking permits this year. A new paper checks whether it changed who
actually gets one.

The Office's permit cap has held at the same figure for years, unmoved while
enrolment kept growing against it. Rather than revisit the cap, the Office
retired its old blanket waitlist for a priority-tier score weighing commute
distance, a documented mobility need, and years of continuous permit
holding, publishing the change as the scheme's central fairness improvement.

Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, Dr Ingrid Vasseur, and Dr Dagny Okafor
matched 890 applicants across the University's last blanket-waitlist cycle
and the two permit cycles run under its replacement, setting each
applicant's old queue position against their new score and the permit they
did, or didn't, receive under each scheme. A further check, run with the
mobility-need weighting quietly switched off, produced almost the same list
of recipients as the published score.

> We went looking for the moment the new score started sending permits
> somewhere the old queue never would have. We are still looking.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

The overlap traces to something the scheme's own paperwork doesn't mention:
applicants with a documented mobility need already skewed toward the
highest commute bands well before the new score existed, so weighting for
one turned out, in practice, to mean weighting for the other twice.

"A scoring formula and a waitlist are both just ways of deciding whose name
comes up next," said Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor of Evaluation of
Evaluation. "Ours happens to agree with itself before and after we changed
the method for deciding it."

Slop University's view is that redesigning a fairness rule is worth
reporting on regardless of whether the redesign moved anyone at all.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.6apmck.
