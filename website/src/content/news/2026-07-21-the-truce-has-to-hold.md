A fridge-mounted reset chart, a phone in a basket, a shared timeout timer:
three ways a household tries to make good on a promise it made itself, with
nobody but its own members left to keep it honest. Slop University's newest
brochure, _The Truce Has to Hold_, gathers what four of the University's
published studies found once they went looking at what actually happens to
an agreement like that once the first evening's resolve has to survive an
ordinary week.

The pamphlet moves from a primary-school reset chart, through a sibling
timeout timer and a phones-in-the-basket dinner rule, to a swapped safety
timer in a gym sauna --- reading each as evidence for the same throughline
the Schools of Emergent Priorities and Continuous Improvement keep arriving
at from opposite ends: an instrument can log a rule far more precisely than
it can make anyone keep it.

> A commitment doesn't fail because someone breaks it. It fails because the
> week it was made for stopped happening.
>
> --- Professor Verity Marris, Director, Trajectory Analytics Group

"We keep building better instruments to watch a rule," said Associate
Professor Casimir Beng, who leads the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "The rule was
never waiting on us."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ii56vw.
