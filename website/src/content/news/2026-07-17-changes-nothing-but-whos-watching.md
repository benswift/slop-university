When Campus Recreation swapped the gym sauna's honesty-based flip-timer card
for a swipe-logged app timer this term, following a member's report of
losing track of time inside, the Adaptive Metrics Lab saw an opening in the
changeover itself: fourteen weeks of occupancy-sensor data, split cleanly
either side of the new panel's install date.

The Lab's new research poster, _Swapped the Card, Not the Habit_, compares
2,214 logged sauna sessions across the term. Median session length holds at
fourteen minutes whichever timer is on the wall, and peak concurrent
occupancy stays at six throughout. A four-week window in which both systems
ran side by side shows the flip-card era's own sign-out numbers tracking the
swipe log closely.

> The card told the truth the whole time. We just didn't have a log that
> agreed with it until now.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas

The result is being read around the School of Continuous Improvement as a
small case study in its own instincts: assume the friction sits somewhere in
the record, and go and look for it. "It says something about how
comfortable we've become measuring things," said Dr Joost Nwosu, the
poster's other author, "that the interesting result here is a non-result."

The team compared session durations and concurrent headcounts drawn from
the Lab's occupancy sensor, sampled at one-minute intervals across the full
term, against the flip-card era's self-logged sign-out record.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.8qz26v.
