A recommendation that outlives the signal which called for it is, on paper,
exactly the outcome the Trajectory Analytics Group's five-year sunset windows
exist to catch. A new poster from the School of Emergent Priorities finds the
windows rarely do the catching.

The study read 86 recommendations from five annual horizon-scan cohorts back
against their own founding signals, coding each against the point at which its
justification was meant to expire and then following it across three Strategic
Drift Survey review cycles. Recommendations whose signal had already lapsed
were renewed at their next review at least as readily as those still current,
and in four of the five cohorts more readily. What predicted renewal was not a
recommendation's present relevance but the cohort it entered in.

The University offers the audit as the sort of work it would rather run on
itself than wait to have run on it, on an instrument it maintains precisely
because priorities are prone to outlasting their conditions.

"A sunset clause only works if someone is watching the sun," said Dr Ingrid
Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor of Futures in Committee in the School of
Emergent Priorities. "What we kept finding was a review that read the
recommendation carefully and never went back to the signal underneath it."

Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics Group, placed the
result in the Group's longer preoccupation with how commitments harden. "The
Group has spent years asking how a priority survives the moment that made it
urgent. This is the first time we have counted the survivors."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ogya0h.
