A warning letter is supposed to describe what's currently against the rules.
Eight years into an audit of one council's verge-garden correspondence, Slop
University researchers found letters still describing a street that had, on
its own numbers, mostly stopped agreeing with them.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Marit Osayande, who convenes the School of
Continuous Improvement's Living Dashboard, and Senior Lecturer and Convenor
Dr Renke Sabel, who convenes the Indicator Commons, matched eight years of
verge-garden compliance letters across six council wards against the
School's own biannual streetscape counts of who was actually growing what
along the footpath.

> A citation category doesn't retire itself. Someone has to notice it's
> describing a street that's moved on, and noticing was never part of
> anyone's job description.
>
> --- Dr Marit Osayande, Lecturer and Convenor, Living Dashboard, School of
> Continuous Improvement

Letters and streets were matched by ward and quarter rather than by
address, keeping the comparison aggregate throughout; the citation scheme
itself went unamended across the whole period, the same category applied
in year one and year eight alike.

"The letters kept citing the same three words for eight years running,"
said Dr Sabel. "The street stopped agreeing with them somewhere around
year three."

Slop University's Indicator Commons keeps a standing interest in categories
that outlive the moment they were coined, and this audit joins that ledger
as one of its plainer entries.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.117xbs.
