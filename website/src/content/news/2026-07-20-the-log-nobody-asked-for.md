The School of Emergent Priorities studies the gap between what an
institution says it does and what its daily operations actually enact; a
school canteen has just supplied about as literal a specimen of that gap as
the School has found all year.

A parent-run primary-school canteen retired its commercial ordering app for
a paper order slip and a peel-off sticker tag, matched by hand at a
volunteer-staffed till and logged with a time-stamp on a carbon duplicate. A
new Slop University paper reports how the eight-week pilot went, and a use
its paper trail picked up that nobody put there on purpose.

> A canteen till was never going to stay just a canteen till once it started
> keeping a log anyone could pick up and read; if there's a lesson in it for
> the wider University, it's that plenty of our own instruments started out
> exactly that literal.
>
> --- Dr Runa Adegoke, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Strategic Drift Survey,
> School of Emergent Priorities

Mismatches needing a volunteer's correction fell sharply as families and
student order-takers learned the format, at a modest, steady cost in till
time. Partway through the pilot, front-office staff began treating a late
order timestamp as an informal sign a student had arrived late, a use the
canteen built for lunch orders alone.

"The ordinary transactional log keeps turning out to be one of the more
honest things we study," said Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School
of Continuous Improvement, "precisely because nobody built it expecting to
be read that way."

The deployment stands as one of the School's clearer instances yet of
building an instrument rather than only auditing somebody else's.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.8qqhhm.
