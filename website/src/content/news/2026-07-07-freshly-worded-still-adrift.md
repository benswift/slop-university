  University's standing committees by how far their actual business has
  drifted from what a freshly rewritten charter says they do, and find the
  rewrite changed the prose but not the drift.

Every one of Slop University's 42 standing committees received a freshly
worded terms-of-reference document within the same fortnight, after the
University's committee-governance function rolled a shared AI drafting
template across the lot in one pass. The University's research programme on
measurement and process turned the rollout into a natural comparison: did any
committee's actual business move to match its tidier charter?

Researchers compared each committee's charter against six months of its own
logged meeting business, both before and after the rewrite, and sorted the
results into a four-part taxonomy describing how a charter and a committee's
practice can relate --- including one category where a committee's business
appears to match its charter only because the charter has become the
committee's own meeting agenda. The prose of every charter improved
measurably. The underlying pattern of drift did not.

The release extends the School of Continuous Improvement's
evaluation-ecosystem agenda from the systems the University measures to the
documents meant to describe them.

"A committee doesn't get better governed because its charter reads better,"
said Dr Osei Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor of the Review Cadence
Observatory in the School of Continuous Improvement. "It was worth finding
out properly, rather than assuming it either way."

"What surprised us wasn't the drift itself, it was how confidently a
committee can read as aligned simply by treating its own charter as the
day's agenda," said Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow at the School of
Emergent Priorities.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.glpjfv.
