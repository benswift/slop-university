The deposit a driver is charged for a Slop University motor-pool booking is
meant to come from a written risk rubric --- incident history, distance,
vehicle class. A new research poster from the School of Emergent Priorities
finds it mostly comes from how many people are standing at the key board
when the tier gets set.

Researchers reconstructed the rubric's predicted tier for 1,140
pool-vehicle bookings across a full teaching semester, then set it against
the tier the motor-pool desk actually charged. Eleven staff interviews,
coded thematically, supplied the second half of the picture: what the desk
says it's weighing once a queue starts to form.

> The rubric is there for the quiet moments. Nobody consults a checklist
> with four people watching the clock.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

"The desk isn't ignoring the rubric," said Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer,
School of Emergent Priorities. "It's applying it exactly as often as the
queue allows."

Charged-tier agreement with the rubric averaged 82% while the key board was
quiet, falling to 34% once three or more drivers were waiting, a pattern
the team found consistent across day, evening, and weekend shifts alike.

For the School of Emergent Priorities, the finding lands inside a wider
preoccupation with what a rubric is actually for once the counter gets
busy, and the desk says it is reviewing whether the tier could be computed
before a driver ever reaches the queue.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.jkn6nb.
