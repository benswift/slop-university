"A post that was never formally closed is a post the Classifier has no reason to
believe was ever open," said Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer in the School of
Emergent Priorities. "That is the whole of it. The roles we let lapse quietly
are the roles that come back as strangers."

Dr Solheim and Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Continuous
Improvement, put the University's Role-Continuity Classifier in front of a panel
of three archivists and gave both the same 96 personnel files: every post
reinstated across five schools in the past four years. The Classifier decides
whether a returning appointment carries its service history forward or starts a
fresh record. The panel, blind to its verdicts, read each file and decided the
same question by majority.

The two agreed moderately overall. Almost all of the disagreement sat in a
single role family. Of 31 invigilation and proctoring posts, the Classifier
filed 26 as new appointments; the panel read genuine continuity in 22 of those.
These are the posts the University's schools have been refilling in number since
supervised examinations returned to the timetable.

The University holds that a filing rule governing its own staff records is owed
the same scrutiny as any instrument it points outward, and publishes the audit
on that basis.

"The service record is not a side effect of the verdict. It is the verdict,"
said Dr Hanke. "What we cannot yet say is how much of the ledger has already
been rewritten this way."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.odkzun.
