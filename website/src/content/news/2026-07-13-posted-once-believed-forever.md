> The sign gets repainted every few weeks. What people remember doesn't.
>
> --- Professor Verity Marris, Director, Trajectory Analytics Group

That is the headline finding of a new poster from the School of Emergent
Priorities, which put a single question to 182 people passing a campus
renovation hoarding over eighteen weeks: when is this due to finish?
Facilities repainted the sign's posted completion date five times across
the same window. Almost none of it stuck.

Professor Verity Marris and Dr Ingrid Vasseur logged every repaint against
a weekly photograph of the hoarding, then matched each passer-by's answer
to whichever date it actually named. By the study's final weeks, fewer
than one answer in ten matched the currently posted date; more than seven
in ten still gave the date painted on the sign the day the hoarding first
went up.

"A committee revises a document and assumes the revision travels with
it," said Dr Vasseur, who convenes the School's Futures in Committee
seminar series. "This is what happens when the travelling part is a
stranger walking past a fence."

The School reads the result as a small, specific instance of a pattern its
anticipatory-capability agenda spends most of its time on: a commitment
that gets updated on paper long after the people it was made to inform
have stopped checking.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zm6il0.
