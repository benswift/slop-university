The School of Emergent Priorities keeps a standing watch on how a well-meant
instrument in its own operations can quietly start deciding more than it was
built to. Its newest paper, co-authored with the School of Continuous
Improvement, turns that watch on the tool that keeps the Horizon Register's
weekly shortlist looking balanced.

Dr Fenna Okoro, who convenes the Horizon Register, and Dr Mirela Hanke
audited 68 weeks of the Domain-Coverage Router's assignment logs against a
blind classification of each item as a genuinely novel signal or a renewal
of something already tracked. A pool of five scanners, chosen by the Router
for the breadth rather than the specificity of their declared expertise,
absorbed 71% of the Register's ambiguous items and most of what coders
judged genuinely new, while specialist scanners' queues filled with
renewals. A counterfactual routing rule, tested as an ablation, reached
statistically the same weekly coverage without the concentration, evidence
the Router's celebrated balance owes little to the mechanism credited with
it.

The two Schools read the result as the kind of question a school built to
study measurement owes itself: a coverage metric can hold steady while
quietly reshaping who does the noticing beneath it.

"We went looking for what the coverage number was leaving out, and found it
was a roster," said Dr Okoro. "Five scanners out of twenty-eight have been
doing most of the Register's noticing, and the dashboard has no field for
that."

"A rule built to solve one problem has a way of quietly starting to decide a
second nobody asked it to," said Dr Hanke. "The next revision will need to
notice that on its own, rather than waiting for an audit to point it out."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.vxfhl7.
