Dock staff at Slop University say they can read a loading bay faster than
the booking system can update it. A new research poster from the School of
Emergent Priorities puts a number on how often they're left to prove it.

Over nine weeks, researchers interviewed eighteen dock staff and sixteen
couriers across three loading bays, then coded what each said against the
roller door's own hand signals and the advance-booking system's published
order for the same 41 shifts. Sequencing calls matched the system's
forecast in roughly a third of cases, a rate a mid-study notice-board
upgrade — meant to make the forecast easier to read — left unchanged.

> This project has taught the School something about its own habits of
> noticing: how much of what actually coordinates a place never makes it
> onto a form.
>
> --- Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow, School of Emergent
> Priorities

Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics Group, said
the finding raises a question worth sitting with: "what a forecasting
system might do differently if it were built to notice the wave instead of
just the booking."

Dr Ingrid Vasseur, who convenes the School's Futures in Committee seminar
series, called the hand-signal exchange "a scheduling system the dock
built for itself, one wave at a time, without ever being asked to."

For the School of Emergent Priorities, the study extends a running
interest in what an institution's tools miss once someone is standing
close enough to see the gap themselves.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zi0r6x.
