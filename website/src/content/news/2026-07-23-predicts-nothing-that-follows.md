Ask a share house who taps "yes, this is home" on the family streaming
account, and you'll get a shrug: whoever's holding the remote. A new Slop
University paper asked whether that shrug matters to the account itself,
tracking 316 such households for eleven months to find out.

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab, worked
with Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu and Lecturer Dr Solveig
Adeyemi, all of the School of Continuous Improvement, to sort each household
by how fast it answered its account's "is this your home" check, then
watched for eleven months to see which were later flagged for further
verification. Households that answered within the hour and households that
never answered at all ended up flagged at close to the same rate.

> We didn't set out to prove a prompt useless. We set out to see what it
> actually measures, and the honest answer is: less than everyone assumed,
> including us.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead, Adaptive Metrics Lab, School
> of Continuous Improvement

"A null result doesn't feel like news," said Dr Nwachukwu, who convenes the
School's Capability Sprints program. "It just feels like the end of an
assumption nobody had actually tested until now."

The panel was recruited through the Adaptive Metrics Lab's household-research
registry and followed for eleven months, timing each household's response to
its home-confirmation check against whatever happened to the account
afterwards. Neither response speed nor a prior flag on a household's record
moved the odds of a new one.

The University counts a clean null as evidence its verification research asks
the right question about a household's account, not one it's dodging.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.osr64g.
