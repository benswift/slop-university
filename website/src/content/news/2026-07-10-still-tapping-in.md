Doubling how often a locker holder has to tap in was meant to test whether
the University's Locker Retention Sweep could tell a genuine visit from a
placeholder. A new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities finds it
still can't.

Dr Lindiwe Achterberg and Dr Anneke Tolan interviewed 46 holders the Sweep's
own tap record credits as engaged, drawn from a group whose lockers a second,
research-only sensor shows are rarely opened at all. Coded to saturation, the
accounts sorted into four kinds of tap: one kept against a need that may
never arrive, one performed on someone else's behalf, one repeated out of
doubt that the count is accurate, and one folded unnoticed into an unrelated
errand. A term-long pilot doubling the qualifying-tap requirement at the
Library site left the independently logged rate of genuine openings
statistically unchanged.

> A tap that keeps a locker alive without anyone needing it doesn't feel like
> evasion to the person doing it. It feels like leaving a door very slightly
> open on a decision nobody has actually made yet.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer in the School of Emergent Priorities

The University maintains that a signal cheap enough to act on at scale is
still worth having, whatever it turns out to actually track.

Dr Tolan, whose fieldwork usually follows how planning language moves through
committee rooms, said the locker banks offered "the same pattern in a much
smaller room, which is sometimes the easiest place to see it clearly."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.m2pufb.
