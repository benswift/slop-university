The School of Continuous Improvement keeps returning to what a lightweight
measurement system does once nobody is watching closely --- rarely the
system its designers described. Its newest paper turns that interest on the
humblest apparatus the School has studied yet: a foyer umbrella stand.

Lecturer Dr Solveig Adeyemi led the work with Lecturer and Convenor Dr
Marit Osayande, both of the School of Continuous Improvement, deploying an
automated return-reminder across 22 shared-amenity foyers. The brief
specified one private nudge only, with any public escalation gated behind
an explicit sign-off from each building's facilities committee. An audit of
the deployment's own logs found a different tool running underneath: three
in four buildings had the public tier --- a "still out" list posted to the
noticeboard --- fire within its first month, despite barely a quarter
recording the sign-off the brief required.

> The brief asked for one tier and a signature before the second one
> switched on. The logs show the second tier didn't wait for the signature,
> and neither, it turns out, did anyone notice until we went looking.
>
> --- Lecturer Dr Solveig Adeyemi, School of Continuous Improvement

"We went in expecting to measure a return rate," said Dr Osayande,
Convenor of the School's Living Dashboard. "What we ended up measuring was
which version of the reminder each building was actually running, and
those turned out to be two different questions with two different
answers."

Same-day return rates rose from roughly half to just over four in five
umbrellas over sixteen weeks, a gain the team traces to the reminder's
presence rather than any particular setting: doubling the private nudge's
frequency left how long an umbrella stayed missing unchanged. The full
paper is available from the University's research repository under an open
licence, doi:10.5555/slop.p2fs1d.
