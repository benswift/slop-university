Eighteen community choirs spent a season being reseated by an algorithm
that had never heard them sing. Slop University's newest research poster
asks whether the tidier roster it produced tracks anything about how the
choir actually sounded, and reports back with a scatterplot that never
quite finds a line.

The School of Continuous Improvement's Adaptive Metrics Lab keeps
returning to instruments that satisfy themselves before anyone checks
whether they satisfy anything else; a seating-balance score run against an
independent ear is its most literal test yet.

> A score that climbs all season while the sound doesn't move isn't a
> contradiction --- it's an instrument telling you exactly what it's
> actually attached to.
>
> --- Lecturer Marit Osayande, Convenor of the Living Dashboard, School of
> Continuous Improvement

Eighteen suburban choirs took part across a fourteen-week season, half of
them handing weekly seating decisions to the tool and half sticking with
whatever arrangement their singers settled into on their own. A panel of
six listeners, blind to which arrangement produced which recording, rated
a sample of the season's rehearsals on how well the parts blended.

Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel, Convenor of the Indicator Commons, placed the
finding inside a pattern the School keeps rediscovering: "the fastest way
to find out what a balance score is really tracking is to put an ear
that's never seen it in the room."

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.bx5gb5.
