A bin-night roster only works if someone is still reading it. A new paper
from the School of Emergent Priorities finds that as a street's laminated
schedule ages, fewer residents are.

Dr Iben Chikere and Dr Anouk Mensah tracked twenty-four residential streets
whose alternating recycling-and-general rosters ranged from freshly
reprinted to well past a year old, walking each street on the eve of its
next collection to log which bin every household actually rolled to the
kerb. Eleven of the streets also received a small QR-code stamp linking to
a live version of the same schedule, added a month before the audit closed.

> A roster is a kind of handover note to a street's future self. This one
> just turned out to have a shelf life nobody had measured before.
>
> --- Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The match between what a roster said and what a street did fell steadily as
the roster aged, and the gap it left behind was filled less by guesswork
than by copying: on the oldest rosters, one in five households matched a
neighbour's bin rather than the schedule. Co-author Dr Anouk Mensah, who
usually tracks how a plan's own phrasing outlives the plan behind it, called
the roster "an unusually literal version of the same question --- the sign
was still up, the words hadn't changed, and it had stopped being the reason
anyone chose a bin."

A QR-code stamp meant to refresh the roster's authority without reprinting
it made no measurable difference, a result that sits squarely inside the
School's wider brief: tracking what a written instruction is still doing
once habit has quietly taken its place.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.4jycca.
