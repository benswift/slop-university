Every invigilated paper sat in the University's central examination hall
opens with the same short procedure: a queue at the door, an identification
check, a phone surrendered into a numbered, sealed pouch. A new paper from
the School of Emergent Priorities asked whether that procedure eases off for
the one kind of sitting it arguably has least reason to police --- the
University's open-note papers, where students are permitted their own
reference material at the desk regardless. Tracked across a full term, the
procedure turns out not to know the difference.

Dr Marek Solheim and Dr Anouk Mensah logged every sitting the hall held for
the term against a six-step checklist fixed before the first paper began,
timing how long each of 1,612 students spent at the surrender table and
recording whether each step of the procedure was completed. Closed-book and
open-note sittings came out close enough to indistinguishable on every
measure the pair tracked, from queue time to the final reconciliation of a
returned device.

The School reads the finding as confirmation that the University's entry
procedures answer to one standard rather than several, whatever the occasion
happens to call for.

> A procedure that never varies with what's actually at stake isn't
> malfunctioning. It's just telling you what it was really built to reassure
> people about, and that was never the phone.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer in the School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Anouk Mensah, the paper's co-author, welcomed the opening the finding
leaves for further work, noting that several of the University's other
standing entry procedures "have never once been asked to justify themselves
against the risk they're supposedly managing."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.00jfxo.
