A recommendation stops being a recommendation the moment nobody is still
willing to say no to it. The School of Emergent Priorities spent a season
watching that willingness fade at forty-two junior-sport ovals, one
Saturday-morning storm call at a time.

Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran, Professor Verity Marris, Director of the
Trajectory Analytics Group, and Dr Osei Vandermeer, Convenor of the Review
Cadence Observatory, logged what a storm-radar app recommended, what the
ground curator on duty actually called, and what the weather went on to
do, fixture by fixture, across 512 decisions over a fourteen-week season.

Curators overrode the app's recommended call in fewer than one decision in
ten by the season's final fortnight, down from more than half at the
start, even as their own calls and the app's matched the morning's
verified weather about equally often throughout. Replacing the app's
numeric confidence score with a plain yes-or-no flag on a subset of ovals
left the override rate exactly where it already was.

> The override was never going to disappear because the app got something
> wrong. It disappeared because nobody could point to the Saturday it
> stopped mattering.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran, School of Emergent Priorities

"We'd like to follow the same curators past this season and see whether
the sky-read comes back once the number stops feeling new," said Dr Osei
Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor of the Review Cadence Observatory.

The University treats a volunteer's changing confidence in a tool as
exactly the kind of quiet handover its research programme exists to catch
before it's finished.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.1dz0cg.
