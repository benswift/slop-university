A charity shop's donation bins empty at whatever pace its roster sets; its
rails fill with whatever the sorting behind them let through. A new research
poster from the School of Emergent Priorities puts a number on the gap
between the two.

> Nobody signs up to sort slowly. The roster has to ask for it directly, or
> the tally will keep answering the only question anyone's checking.
>
> --- Lecturer Lindiwe Achterberg, School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Lindiwe Achterberg and Dr Mirela Hanke piloted an extended-shift,
lower-quota roster at three charity shops over nine months, running it
alongside each shop's standard fast-throughput schedule and auditing forty
items a week per shop against the sorting log. The piloted roster's own
tally fell 34 percent behind the standard schedule's. The shop-floor fault
rate it left behind told the opposite story, dropping from 11.4 percent to
6.2 percent across the same nine months.

The gap wasn't even across what came through the door. Electricals carried
the steepest fault difference of the four categories audited; books, barely
sorted at all under either roster, carried the shallowest.

"A quota tells a volunteer how fast to go, never how carefully," said Dr
Mirela Hanke. "This is the first time we've had a number for what the
second half of that instruction was costing."

None of the three shops has adjusted its rostered quota since the trial
closed. The School takes that less as a verdict on the finding than as a
demonstration of how firmly a single number can anchor a roster once it's
the only one being watched.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.an33nw.
