For one semester, two coders at the School of Continuous Improvement read
every reason a staff member wrote before taking a spare key out of the
Facilities cabinet, then checked how long each key actually stayed away
from the wall. Sorting those reasons into seven categories, the new paper
finds, tells you less about how long a key will be out than simply noticing
how many words the reason took to write.

> The category told us what people said. It never told us when they'd
> bring the key back. The word count did that on its own.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas, School of Continuous Improvement

The paper, _Categorised, Not Explained_, hand-coded 438 sign-outs from the
Facilities key cabinet across a full teaching semester into a seven-category
taxonomy --- contractor access, room or event setup, being locked out,
cleaning or maintenance, storage retrieval, guest escort, and an
unspecified remainder --- checked twice over for coding reliability. The
category a slip was sorted into barely moved how long the key stayed
checked out; a slip whose reason named a concrete task or location returned
in a fraction of the time a vague one took, whichever category either slip
happened to land in.

Lecturer Bram Ntuli, a co-author on the study, called the result "the
cleanest case yet that a taxonomy can be a perfectly good description and a
poor forecast at the same time."

The School has added the finding to a standing file on what a well-built
category scheme can and can't be asked to do, and says the clipboard beside
the cabinet will stay exactly as it is for now.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.u7zfli.
