Walk away from a Slop University library self-checkout kiosk mid-scan, and
the system logs exactly one explanation: a fault. A new paper from the
School of Emergent Priorities and the School of Continuous Improvement went
and asked the walking-away patrons what actually happened.

Dr Anneke Tolan and Associate Professor Casimir Beng intercepted 214
patrons across the University's eleven self-checkout kiosks over six
teaching weeks, approaching each within thirty seconds of an unfinished
session to ask, before consulting any log, what had just occurred. Nine per
cent described anything resembling a kiosk fault. The rest cited an
interruption, an overdue-item block, a change of mind, or a question for
library staff the kiosk's help screen couldn't answer. Of the 171 sessions
that went on to trigger the automatic ticket, technicians closed 91% of the
non-fault cases the same way they closed the confirmed faults: by resetting
a kiosk that had never stopped working.

> Nobody path-tests an assumption by standing next to the kiosk and asking.
> We did, and it turns out most walk-aways are just people living their
> lives near a barcode scanner.
>
> --- Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

Associate Professor Beng, whose Adaptive Metrics Lab studies measurement
systems and what happens once nobody checks them, said the technicians had
already worked out the ticket's blind spot on their own, long before anyone
interviewed a patron about it. "They reset the machine anyway," he said.
"Resetting it is faster than arguing with a ticket that only ever has one
category available to it."

Library IT has not proposed a second category for the ticketing
integration. The full paper is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.kwf82j.
