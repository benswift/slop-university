Slop University has never assigned anyone to water the windowsill pot-plants
in its shared offices, and the laminated sign-up sheet nobody was rostered
onto has, across three replacements, accumulated a dense record of why the
watering didn't happen anyway. A new paper from the School of Emergent
Priorities, _What the Roster Remembers_, codes that record systematically,
sorting 412 handwritten entries from six shared-office rosters into a
six-category taxonomy of watering-roster excuses.

> Nobody in this study was ever assigned to water anything. What we coded
> was how people explain themselves anyway, to an audience that mostly
> never reads it.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

Two coders classified each annotation, then tracked how often each
category's phrasing recurred across three roster relaunches --- the point a
worn sheet is discarded and replaced, taking its written history with it.
Some excuses travel with the habit rather than the object: "not my turn" and
"roster wasn't updated" reappear every eight to eleven days and gain ground
with each relaunch, while a sincere apology recurs only every four or five
weeks and fades from the record each time the sheet is swapped.

"It's a strange thing to learn about an institution from six laminated
sheets," said Associate Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory
Analytics Group. "The plant was never the point. What outlasts a discarded
object usually isn't the object's own record of itself --- it's whatever
people kept saying about it."

The study sits inside the School's wider interest in what outlives a
priority once the thing carrying it is retired --- usually not the object,
the paper argues, but the language that kept explaining it.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.8kn3k2.
