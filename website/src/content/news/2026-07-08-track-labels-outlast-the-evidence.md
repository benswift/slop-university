The day-one diagnostic that sorts every Master of Priority Studies cohort
into a Foundation or Fast-Track stream carries one condition on the label it
assigns: it is advisory, and can be revisited at any time. The School of
Emergent Priorities has now asked how many students still believe that.

Researchers surveyed 214 students across the 2025 and 2026 intakes, six
months after each cohort's diagnostic, asking first whether they could name
their track and then, separately, whether they could name any evidence that
would move them off it. Nearly nine in ten answered the first question
without prompting. Fewer than one in twelve could answer the second, and the
gap between the two answers held steady across both intakes surveyed.

For the School of Emergent Priorities, the gap sits alongside a
longer-running question about how a label outlives the reasoning that
assigned it, once advising conversations settle into treating the one as
shorthand for the other.

"You can hear which one the label is in the first thirty seconds of an
advising conversation," said Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow in the
School of Emergent Priorities. "You can go a whole semester and never hear
the criteria mentioned at all."

"A day-one instrument that's never revisited isn't advisory in anything but
name," said Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics
Group in the School of Emergent Priorities. "We built in a review pathway.
This is the first look at whether anyone uses it."
