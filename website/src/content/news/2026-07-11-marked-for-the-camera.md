> Staff work out very quickly what a camera is for. Ours apparently worked
> out what our camera was for faster than we did.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

That is roughly what a new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities and
the School of Continuous Improvement found when it asked 138 staff why they
mark up a meeting-room whiteboard. Since Facilities introduced its Room
Engagement Score --- a single end-of-slot photograph of the board, scored
"engaged" if there's ink on it and "engagement not evidenced" if there isn't,
feeding straight into the following term's room-booking priority --- staff in
high-demand rooms have learned exactly what the lens is looking for. Sixty-one
per cent now report marking the board "usually" or "always," against nineteen
per cent in rooms nobody is fighting to keep.

Dr Achterberg and Dr Mirela Hanke, the paper's second author, matched the
survey against the Score's own audit log and an independently coded record of
whether each meeting actually reached a decision. Deliberate marking
predicted a strong "engaged" score; it barely predicted anything else. "The
ink and the outcome," said Dr Hanke, "turned out to be two different
questions, and the room-booking system has only ever been asking one of
them."

Facilities has not indicated any change to the Score ahead of next term's
allocation review.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.yjud2l.
