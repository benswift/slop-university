Slop University researchers began this study by interviewing eighteen people
about a feeling they had never had to put into words before: the point at
which a community toy library's promise of unlimited borrowing stops feeling
unlimited. The result, published this week, is a six-item questionnaire the
University is calling the Perceived Lending Ceiling Scale.

Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the School of Continuous
Improvement's Indicator Commons, led the work with Dr Dagny Okafor and Dr
Ingeborg Nwachukwu. The scale was tested across nine independently-run toy
libraries, none of which took part in writing its questions, and checked
against something no toy library had previously agreed to hand over:
volunteers' own private record of who they had quietly asked to ease off.

> A written policy can say whatever it likes about limits. We wanted to know
> what a member actually feels standing at the counter, and it turns out that
> feeling arrives quite reliably, whether or not anyone ever writes it down.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Renke Sabel, Indicator Commons, School
> of Continuous Improvement

"The six questions took eighteen conversations and three toy libraries to get
right," said Dr Okafor, who convenes the School's Evaluation of Evaluation
program. "What surprised us wasn't that the scale worked. It's that it worked
exactly as well for someone who joined last month as for someone who has
belonged for years."

The finding extends the School's evaluation-ecosystem agenda into a question
it had not asked of any lending scheme before: not what a policy promises,
but what a member has already worked out for themselves. The University says
any toy library is welcome to adapt the instrument for its own use.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.qycm4w.
