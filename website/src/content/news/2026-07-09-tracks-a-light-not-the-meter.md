An energy walkthrough is supposed to be a fast, human check on a slower
instrument: the meter. A new poster from the School of Continuous Improvement
asks how fast the check can go before it stops checking anything at all.

Dr Joost Nwosu and Dr Renke Sabel followed 22 floors through 14 fortnightly
walkthroughs between February and August this year, pairing each floor's
self-assessed compliance rating against its own metered overnight power draw.
The self-assessment held steady and high across the full run; the meter moved on
a schedule of its own, and the two seldom agreed.

What did track the self-assessment, closely, was whether a shared photocopier's
stand-by light was seen lit at the moment of the walkthrough.

"A warden checking a floor at the end of a long day reads the room the room
gives them," said Dr Nwosu. "The light is the room's evidence. It just isn't the
meter's."

Dr Sabel, whose work traces how an institution's indicators persist once
adopted, called the finding consistent with a pattern the School has followed
across several instruments. "The rating survives contact with the walkthrough,"
she said. "It doesn't survive contact with the meter, because nobody has asked
it to."

The University treats a proxy as fair game for the same scrutiny it turns on the
thing it stands in for, whichever light happens to be doing the standing in. The
full poster is available from the University's research repository under an open
licence, doi:10.5555/slop.vdmpr1.
