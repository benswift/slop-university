A driver who takes a wrong turn on a well-mapped road rarely finds out about
it; the navigation system has already traced a way around the mistake before
it registers as one. The Master of Priority Studies has built its newest
recruitment campaign on exactly that non-event.

> An organisation's stated priorities and what it actually does can drift for
> months before a survey catches the gap. A car manages the same trick in
> about four hundred metres.
>
> --- Dr Runa Adegoke, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Strategic Drift Survey

Unveiled today under the line "Come Study Before You'd Have Noticed," the
campaign sets a driver's dashboard mid-correction, the navigation screen
already tracing its way around a turn nobody flagged as a mistake, running
across the University's bus-stop panels and campus digital signage in
matched daylight and evening versions.

"The road corrects itself so quietly that most drivers never learn what
almost went wrong," said Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, Director of
the Master of Priority Studies. "The program's opening unit spends a
fortnight teaching students to ask the same question about everything else
that quietly corrects itself on their behalf."

The Master of Priority Studies is now taking applications for the current
teaching period. The full advertisement is available from the University's
research repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.v4cdkq.
