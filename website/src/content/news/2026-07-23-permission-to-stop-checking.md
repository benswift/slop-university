Slop University researchers spent fourteen months watching share-house
group chats to see which ones knew when to stop.

Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Runa Adegoke led the work with Lecturer Dr
Ingrid Vasseur and Lecturer Dr Marit Osayande, all of the School of Emergent
Priorities. The team sorted each chat by whether its founder had also typed
a sentence naming the job and an end date, then audited every chat for the
point its message traffic stopped for good.

> A chat doesn't stop because the job is finished. It stops because someone
> gave everyone permission to stop checking, and a lot of chats never get
> that permission.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Runa Adegoke, School of Emergent
> Priorities

Chats with a self-written end date went quiet within a median of two and a
half months; chats without one kept some traffic moving for closer to ten.
The gap wasn't only speed --- chats without a stated end date also
accumulated roughly four times as many house-rule decisions unrelated to
the reason the chat existed: shoes in the hallway, quiet hours, and who was
rostered for the bins, litigated long after the bond clean or working bee
that started the thread.

"The chat doesn't need a funeral," said Dr Vasseur, who convenes the
School's Futures in Committee. "It just needs someone willing to be the one
who stops replying first."

The University recruited chats through three capital-city share-house
cooperatives, coding each week's messages as on-purpose, off-purpose, or
social on a scheme fixed in advance. Within the School, the finding adds to
a larger interest: how long a stated priority survives contact with
whoever is meant to act on it.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.273hrj.
