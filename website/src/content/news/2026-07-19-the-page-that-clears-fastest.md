> A response-time column can't tell the difference between a carer who
> checked the room in twenty seconds and one who didn't go at all. We built
> the second measurement because the first one was never going to notice on
> its own.
>
> --- Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi, School of Continuous Improvement

Slop University has published a new research poster auditing a night-shift
practice that isn't unique to any one facility: grading how fast a carer
answers an in-room fall-alert page. Dr Solveig Adeyemi and Senior Lecturer
Runa Adegoke followed twenty-six night shifts across three residential
aged-care facilities, setting each page's response time against an
independent record of whether anyone actually walked the room afterwards.

Working from the facilities' own alert logs and door-sensor entry data, the
pair modelled response time against how many other pages were already
queued when a page arrived, holding shift hour and carer tenure constant.
Confirmed falls, which trigger their own emergency pathway, sat outside the
audit's scope.

The University treats an internal scorecard as fair game for scrutiny
whether it belongs to a partner facility or to one of its own indicators,
and reports what turns up regardless of which.

"The gap between what a metric is graded on and what it was built to
protect is exactly the distance the Strategic Drift Survey exists to
measure," said Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke. "This is the smallest room
we've found it in yet."

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.8gyufz.
