> A ticket doesn't end an argument. It just gives it somewhere else to
> happen.
>
> --- Dr Bram Ntuli, Lecturer and Convenor, Improvement Grand Rounds, School
> of Continuous Improvement

That is the header finding of a new research poster from the School of
Continuous Improvement, which spent ten weeks logging queue-jumping
disputes across thirty-four neighbourhood delis and butcher counters, each
running one of three arrangements: a paper-ticket dispenser, an app-based
virtual queue, or no formal system at all.

Dr Ntuli and co-author Dr Thandiwe Solberg coded 612 disputes drawn from
more than 8,400 logged visits by where each one actually happened — at the
counter itself, or one step removed, at the dispenser or inside the app.
Read one way, the formal systems worked: at-counter confrontations fell to
a little over a quarter of their prior share under paper tickets, and to
roughly one in seven under the app. Read the other way, they simply moved:
total dispute incidence held within a percentage point of the same figure
regardless of which system a counter ran.

"A counter that looks calmer isn't necessarily calmer," said Dr Thandiwe
Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Demo Quarter, School of Continuous
Improvement. "It's just moved the argument somewhere the person behind the
till doesn't have to watch."

Dr Ntuli said the team is already extending its location codebook to
post-office and pharmacy queues, on the expectation that the same
instruments, and the same relocation, are turning up wherever a counter
tries to formalise its line.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.n8c0ot.
