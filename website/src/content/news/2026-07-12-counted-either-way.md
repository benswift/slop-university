The School of Emergent Priorities spends most of its research effort on
noticing an ending before the ending is official. Its newest poster turns
that habit on the print-credit top-up subscription tucked beside every
library photocopier.

Digital Services logs every cancellation of the auto-top-up service the same
way it logs a successful one: as a "self-service resolution." Dr Marek
Solheim, Dr Anouk Mensah, and Professor Verity Marris set eighteen months of
that cancellation figure against the final eighteen months of four campus
subscription-style services the University has already retired --- a locker
rental, a laundry-credit top-up, a bike-share membership, and a meal-plan
auto-renewal.

Cumulative cancellations on the print-credit service reached 34% of active
subscribers by June, inside the 30-35% band at which three of the four
comparator services were formally discontinued. Across both datasets, no
cancellation was logged any differently from a completed top-up.

> A cancel click and a top-up click are, as far as the dashboard's concerned,
> the same click going in different directions. We'd like the difference to
> show up somewhere before the service does.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Professor Marris, whose own work follows how priorities outlive the
conditions that justified them, said the trajectory "reads less like a
warning than like an anniversary nobody's marked yet." The University holds
the comparison as an early instance of its anticipatory-capability agenda
meeting a service most people assumed too small to anticipate anything.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.gz0g6t.
