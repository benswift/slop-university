> We came into this expecting a labelling problem. What we found looks more
> like a small, well-mannered turf war.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas, School of Continuous Improvement

Nine shared postdoctoral offices in the School of Continuous Improvement's
building run on a weekly hot-desk booking calendar, with each desk's
occupant announced locally by a sliding-tab nameplate beside the door. A new
paper from the School checked whether the two records agree, and audited
every misdirected letter, parcel, and visitor that turned up when they
didn't.

Dr Okwuosa and Dr Bram Ntuli tracked all 36 desks across the nine offices
every Monday morning for a full teaching semester, cross-checking each tab
against that week's booking and logging the front office's tally of
redirected mail and confused drop-ins. The tab matched the booking calendar
in 71 per cent of the 504 desk-weeks audited; the remaining stale
desk-weeks, despite being under a third of the sample, accounted for close
to three-quarters of the semester's misdirected deliveries and visitors.
Occupants with a senior title left their name on the door for a working
week or more after handing back the desk, against a single day for
postdoctoral fellows --- and a laminated reminder card posted for the
semester's second half changed almost nothing.

"A booking calendar assumes the desk agrees with it," said Dr Petra
Umbile, who was not involved in the study. "It's a reasonable assumption
right up until someone doesn't feel finished with a desk yet."

The School says the finding will feed directly into its indicator-currency
work, with an automated, calendar-linked tab now under discussion as a
successor to the laminated card.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.8dleq5.
