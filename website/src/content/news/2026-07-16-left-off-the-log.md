The Student Union pantry's charter asks visitors to take what they need and
leave what they can, and for as long as the shelf has stood there volunteer
moderators have cleared it against a shared notebook rather than any form the
charter itself supplies. A new research poster from the School of Emergent
Priorities sets what visitors say guided their last visit against what those
moderators actually wrote down.

The comparison sits inside the School's wider interest in how an
institution's own language holds up once it meets the institution's own
paperwork.

Researchers intercepted 214 pantry visitors over one term with an anonymous
survey asking which of the charter's own reasons — a need, a spare, an
expiry, a duplicate — had prompted their visit, then hand-coded 361 of the
shelf's own removal-log entries against the same five-category taxonomy,
holding the category set fixed throughout.

> A shelf like this keeps two records of itself, and they barely recognise
> each other.
>
> — Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

A need- or spare-based reason — the charter's own wording — turns up in 41%
of matched survey responses but only 6% of coded log entries; "no reason
given" covers 64% of the log against 18% of self-reports. "It says as much
about what a log is for as it does about what a charter asks for," said Dr
Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor of Futures in Committee.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.o2d7x7.
