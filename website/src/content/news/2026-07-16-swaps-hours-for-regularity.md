A desk that logs barely any hours can still be the one likely to fail
Facilities' Evening Tidiness Sweep --- so long as it keeps the hours it does
log at roughly the same time every day. A new paper from the School of
Emergent Priorities finds a desk's arrival-time regularity forecasts a failed
tidiness check better than the total hours a desk racks up, the very number
Facilities' own risk score has ranked desks by since it was introduced.

The Adaptive Metrics Lab's Desk Utilisation Risk Score has, since it was
introduced, flagged the University's flexible-tenancy hot-desks most likely to
fail the nightly sweep by how many hours each was logged occupied that week.
Researchers from the School's Trajectory Analytics Group set that score
against an alternative signal adapted from circadian rest-activity-rhythm
research, more usually applied to sleep and health outcomes, scoring instead
how consistently a desk's occupant arrives at the same time of day.

> A desk that turns up at the same time every day has time to develop a
> routine around tidying it. One that turns up whenever still gets its hours
> in, just never in a shape a habit can settle into.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

The comparison ran across a full teaching term on two of the University's
hot-desk floors, tracking each desk's badge-swipe pattern and its outcome at
the nightly sweep week over week. The regularity-based forecast outperformed
the hours-based score on both floors, and combining the two added nothing
regularity alone did not already predict --- a result the researchers say
leaves the existing score answering a question the sweep was never really
asking.

"It's an unusually clean case of an institution already holding the better
signal and ranking by the worse one instead," said Associate Professor Kwame
Lindqvist.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.h4dvr2.
