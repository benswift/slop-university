Of the 684 slips collected from Slop University's suggestion boxes this
term, roughly one in three concerned something Facilities could fix by
morning. The rest, a new paper from the School of Continuous Improvement
finds, mostly ended their journey as a single line on the Living Dashboard.

Dr Mirela Hanke and Dr Renke Sabel coded fourteen weeks of submissions from
the University's eleven suggestion boxes into five categories, then checked
each one against the six standing bodies the University keeps for
evaluation and review. Only facilities and maintenance requests, it turns
out, reach an individual response at scale, not because any of the six
bodies has a remit over them, but because Facilities already had a
work-order system running before the box network existed. Everything else,
from catering complaints to compliments, is absorbed into the Engagement
Pulse tile's single monthly count.

> A count tells you the box got used. It doesn't tell you what for, and we
> would rather the University knew both.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Sabel, whose work has traced how few indicators are ever formally retired
once adopted, reads the Engagement Pulse tile as a case worth watching
rather than reworking outright. "An indicator earns its keep by shaping a
decision, not by being logged," he said, adding that the finding folds
neatly into the School's broader appetite for knowing exactly what its own
instruments are made of. The researchers say a wider cross-walk, covering
suggestion channels beyond the box network, is a natural next step.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.tlmg6a.
