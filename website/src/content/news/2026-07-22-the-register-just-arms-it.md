Somewhere in most share houses that share a robot vacuum, someone eventually
starts writing down what it does wrong --- the sock it ate, the rug it
bunched against the door, the cat it startled mid-nap. A new Slop University
paper set out to check what that habit is actually doing to the arguments
it's meant to settle.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu and Lecturer Dr Marit Osayande,
both of the School of Continuous Improvement, tracked 34 share houses across
a single term, comparing 23 that kept a written incident register against 11
that never did. Weekly check-ins logged both the vacuum's mishaps and the
arguments that followed them, and coded each argument for what it actually
invoked.

> Nobody sets out to build an archive of who's to blame. It just accumulates
> while you're trying to settle the thing in front of you.
>
> --- Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, School of Continuous
> Improvement

The two groups argued about their vacuum at statistically indistinguishable
rates across the fourteen-week window. What differed was what an argument
reached for: register-keeping houses' disputes cited a specific, named prior
incident more than four times as often as houses with no register to cite,
while non-register houses fell back more often on the incident directly in
front of them.

The result extends the School of Continuous Improvement's continuing
interest in what a documentation habit actually does once it's adopted,
rather than what it was designed to do, joining earlier work on a share
house's energy-budget power-board and a household chore wheel's own
record-keeping habits. The full paper is available from the University's
research repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.llj9ll.
