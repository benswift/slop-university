Not every finding travels. The School of Emergent Priorities' 2026
community-fridge study found a four-label taxonomy that predicted how fast
a note drew a volunteer restock. Its newest poster asks a plainer question
of that result: does the taxonomy predict anything anywhere else?

Dr Petra Umbile and Dr Anouk Mensah pre-registered a five-category genre
taxonomy adapted from the fridge study's coding scheme, then logged 214
books across five suburban street-corner exchange boxes twice daily for
eleven weeks, timing each title's disappearance from the shelf. Spine
orientation --- whether a title faced the door cover-out or spine-out ---
was recorded throughout as a held covariate, not a variable of interest.

> Whether a category effect travels past the one site it was found at is
> exactly the kind of question a single study can't answer on its own. This
> is one data point toward a real answer, not the last word on either the
> fridge or the box.
>
> --- Dr Petra Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

Median time-to-pickup held between 14.6 and 16.8 hours across the five
pre-registered genre categories --- a spread the original fridge taxonomy
would have called noise. Spine orientation told a sharper story: a
cover-out title cleared the shelf in a median 6.1 hours, against 21.4 for
one shelved spine-out. Co-author Dr Anouk Mensah, who usually tracks how a
plan's wording survives its own later drafts, called the reversal "a
method outliving the site it was built for, and a different variable
quietly stepping into the gap it left."

The School of Emergent Priorities counts a method that stops working
elsewhere as data in its own right, not a result to quietly shelve.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zxua1o.
