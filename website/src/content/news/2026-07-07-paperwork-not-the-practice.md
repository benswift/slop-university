An attestation checkbox is hard to fail. That may be the least surprising
finding in a new paper from the School of Continuous Improvement, which set out
to check what changed after Slop University banned AI-drafted first drafts of
internal grant-application narratives, and found the checkbox itself the only
thing that moved on schedule.

The team set the ban's own compliance record against telemetry the University's
grants portal has quietly kept for years, for an unrelated reason, across 349
grant narratives submitted over the six months either side of the ban's start
date, then checked the pattern against a round of staff interviews. Attestation
compliance reached full completion within the first reporting cycle. The
telemetry record moved only at the margins, and mostly in narratives that
stopped showing any drafting activity on the portal at all, rather than in
narratives that kept drafting there.

"None of this works without a telemetry feed the portal has quietly kept for
years for an entirely different reason," said Associate Professor Casimir Beng,
Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "We didn't build anything new; we just finally
asked it something."

"Everyone we spoke to already knew exactly why they had moved the drafting off
the portal," said Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow at the School of
Emergent Priorities. "It wasn't hidden from us; it just isn't something the
audit log was ever going to ask about."

The School describes the divergence as reason enough to watch its own compliance
systems as closely as anyone else's.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.7aynun.
