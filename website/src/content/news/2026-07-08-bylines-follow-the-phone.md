Whoever answers Slop University's press office fastest in the 48 hours before a
release ships is, more often than not, also whoever appears first on the paper.
A new interview study finds that has rather less to do with who did the analysis
than any authorship policy assumes.

The study, from the School of Emergent Priorities and the School of Continuous
Improvement, interviewed 34 postdoctoral and early-career researchers about how
the byline order on their most recent published output was actually decided,
then checked their accounts against the Office of Research Outputs' own
correspondence logs. Self-reported contribution barely moved the byline at all;
reply speed to the press office's pre-release query window moved it almost in
lockstep.

Both Schools place the finding within a wider effort to reconcile what a stated
authorship policy promises with what a fixed external deadline quietly ends up
rewarding instead.

"The policy has always said contribution decides the order, and nobody we spoke
to thought that was being ignored," said Professor Verity Marris, Director of
the Trajectory Analytics Group in the School of Emergent Priorities. "What we
found is a second, faster clock running underneath it, and most people had
simply stopped noticing which one was actually setting the time."

"It's the kind of finding you can only see once you go looking for the thing
nobody wrote a policy about," said Dr Petra Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow in the
School of Emergent Priorities. "The interesting part isn't that it happens. It's
how settled everyone already was about it."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xhjxxg.
