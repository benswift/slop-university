Reserve a slot on a courtyard barbecue's new sign-up board, and the courtyard
will still serve whoever got there first. That is the headline result of a
School of Continuous Improvement poster tracking sixteen apartment-complex
courtyards through a twelve-week deployment of a QR-coded booking system for
the shared grill.

Associate Professor Casimir Beng and Dr Mirela Hanke logged 812 grill turns
across the study, fitting eight courtyards with a sign-up board at the
study's halfway point and holding the remaining eight as walk-up-only
controls throughout. Grill-turn order tracked arrival order almost
identically in both arms, before and after the board went live, and median
wait time held near twelve minutes regardless of which courtyard a resident
lived in. Only around a third of the slots residents actually booked were
claimed in the order they'd reserved them.

> The board gave residents something to point to. It didn't give them a
> different queue.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab

Dr Hanke, whose own work follows what happens once a measurement instrument
is mistaken for the thing it measures, called the booking board "a proxy for
order that never got the chance to become the order itself." For a school
built on the gap between what a system records and what a system changes,
the courtyard grill turned out to run the same test the university keeps
running on itself.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.39kpsc.
