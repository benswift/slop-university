An annual number arrives once, says its piece, and leaves the University to live
with it for the next twelve months. Slop University's own instruments do not
observe that schedule.

A new brochure, published this week by the Office of Research Outputs, makes the
case for judging the University by the record it keeps of itself: the School of
Continuous Improvement's Indicator Commons, Impact Pathway Atlas, and Living
Dashboard, each running on its own schedule and each open, on the same page as
its own method, to anyone who wants to check it.

The brochure surveys four recently published studies as evidence the instruments
are already doing the work asked of them, setting each beside the routine
comparison it was built to replace, from a queue-length index that tracks the
University's ranking trajectory to an audit of what actually earns a colleague's
trust in an internal tool.

For an institution that has spent the year turning its instruments inward, the
brochure is a rarer document: written for a reader outside the buildings, making
the case that the University's own account of itself should be trusted over
anyone else's.

"A ranking tells you where you landed. It doesn't say who's allowed to argue
with the number," said Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer in the School of
Continuous Improvement. "The Commons is built so that anyone who wants to argue
with one of ours can find exactly where to start."

"Every one of these instruments started as something we built to check our own
work," said Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab.
"Handing the same instruments to a prospective reader is a newer idea for us
than people probably assume."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.t73jw8.
