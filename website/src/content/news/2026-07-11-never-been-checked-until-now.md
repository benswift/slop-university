Next term's bike-rack capacity expansion is decided corral by corral, and
the number deciding it comes from a sensor nobody had checked against a
person standing there counting. A new paper from the School of Emergent
Priorities does the counting.

Dr Lindiwe Achterberg and Associate Professor Casimir Beng set the
University's share-scooter corral occupancy sensor against an unannounced
manual headcount across nine weeks and 15 of the University's 21 registered
corrals, timing 312 physical counts against the sensor's own reading for
the same minute. The two agreed only moderately, and the gap wasn't random:
corrals where scooters tend to sit longest read persistently busier to the
sensor than a headcount would justify, while corrals with fast turnover
read quieter than the queue at their kerb would suggest.

> A sensor that has never been wrong yet isn't the same as a sensor that
> has been checked. Ours hadn't been, until now.
>
> --- Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, whose Adaptive Metrics Lab studies
measurement systems and what happens when they stop tracking what they
were built to track, called the dwell-time pattern "the more interesting
number to have found," noting that an instrument built to flag whether a
bay is occupied at all will always have an opinion about how long, whether
or not the capacity review asked it one. The School reads the finding as
consistent with its wider interest in how a number gets trusted well past
the point anyone last checked it.

Facilities has not revised the capacity-review process. The pair say a
headcount-weighted supplement, even an occasional one, is what they would
like to see trialled ahead of the network's next allocation round.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.gdacz4.
