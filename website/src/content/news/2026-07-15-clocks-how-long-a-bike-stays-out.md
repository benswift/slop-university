Every share-bike unlocked at the University's eastern precinct starts a
checkout clock that does not stop until someone brings it back, however long
that takes. Slop University's newest brochure, _Come and Stay Awhile_, puts
that running clock on the cover and asks the reader to see it the way the
Trajectory Analytics Group does: not as a bike gone missing, but as a session
somebody wanted.

The brochure follows the same instinct into two more places the University
keeps half an eye on --- a rolling watch the Adaptive Metrics Lab runs across
campus footpaths, benches and service counters, and a community garden plot
behind the Library that changes hands on an unhurried waitlist. None of the
three are new; what's new is the University saying, in print, that it reads
dwelling as a compliment rather than a delay.

> A session that's still open is not evidence of neglect. It's evidence
> nobody has decided it's finished.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Marek Solheim, whose work on the garden waitlist anchors the brochure's
third feature, said the list "persists not because anyone enjoys making
people wait, but because the wait itself turned out to be worth watching."

The brochure also cites three items already in the University's published
record --- a relocated shuttle stop's ridership bump, a covert comparison of
reusable-cup discounts across two café outlets, and an interview study of
locker retention under an automated reclamation sweep --- each resolving to
its own DOI, so a reader can check the pitch against the record it's built
on.

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.e5i2vi.
