Move a shuttle stop forty metres and the boardings go up. A new poster from
the School of Emergent Priorities asks who is doing the extra boarding.

Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist and Dr Lindiwe Achterberg counted every
morning boarding at the East Precinct stop for fourteen weeks, spanning the
mid-term relocation that brought it nearer the new engineering building, and
counted the unmoved Western Avenue stop alongside it. Boardings at the
relocated stop climbed from a pre-move average in the low forties to a
post-move average near sixty. The control stop did not move at all. The gain
arrived inside three service weeks and then stopped, which is not the shape a
growing precinct produces.

The pair had set aside the first ten service days as a novelty window, on the
assumption that a settling-in effect would need discarding first.

> We built the exclusion window to protect the estimate from a transient, and
> the window turned out to contain the entire effect. What we learned is
> mostly about the instrument we brought, not about the shuttle.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

The relocation was funded against a forecast of standing new demand. The
finding does not dispute that the stop is busier; it locates the extra
passengers among people already walking the same line.

Dr Achterberg, whose work follows initiatives that stay provisional for years
at a time, said the design should travel: two further stops were relocated
under the same business case, "and nobody has yet counted what happened at
either of them."

The University takes some institutional pride in a School willing to audit the
arithmetic behind a decision the University has already paid for.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.d7z13b.
