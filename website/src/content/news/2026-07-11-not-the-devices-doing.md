Slop University's central courtyard holds an automated bioacoustic deterrent
responsible for a data point Facilities is proud of: a steady fall in pigeon
sightings since the device went into service three terms ago. A new paper
from the School of Continuous Improvement asked the staff who work in and
around that courtyard what else changed over the same period.

Dr Thandiwe Solberg and Dr Sten Okwuosa interviewed eleven grounds and
facilities staff who maintain the courtyard, then set what those staff
described against the deterrent's own monthly compliance figure across the
same eleven-week window. A scheduled nine-day outage, when the device fell
silent for planned maintenance, gave the pair an unplanned natural
comparison.

Staff described routines the compliance report doesn't reference: windows
closed earlier than rostered, bin collection retimed around the courtyard's
quieter hours, and a shared understanding not to escalate a fault
mid-exam-period. Those routines, the pair found, track step for step with
the improvement the device's own report claims outright, and continued
unbroken through the outage, when sightings held steady with the device off.

> A compliance figure only ever tells you what it was built to look for.
> Ours was never built to look for a bin roster.
>
> --- Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter

Dr Sten Okwuosa, whose own work traces how a research finding travels after
publication, welcomed what the courtyard case adds to that record: the
credit for an outcome and the labour behind it "can travel to entirely
different places, and usually nobody is deciding that on purpose."

Facilities has not revised the courtyard's monthly report, but says the
pairing of interview and log data is the kind of check it would like
applied more widely across the estate.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.gj64uh.
