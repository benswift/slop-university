The School of Emergent Priorities has spent the past two semesters asking a
question its own community radio station had never formally put to the
people it governs: whether the handwritten sign-up sheet pinned to the
studio door actually decides who gets a segment, or merely records that
someone once wanted one. A new paper answers it by asking 28 presenters
directly, in their own words, how they believe they got on air.

The finding held the School's attention less as a verdict on the station
than as a demonstration of how much an ordinary allocation object can be
asked to carry. Under a quarter of debuts, in either semester studied,
followed the sheet's own written order; the largest single group instead
described a slot held for them by an existing presenter before they had
written a name down themselves. A mid-year switch from paper to a shared
digital form, introduced specifically to close that gap, left the pattern
unchanged.

> A sheet only records the moment someone decided to write their name down.
> Everything that happened before that moment is where the allocation was
> actually happening, and nobody had thought to ask a presenter to describe
> it.
>
> --- Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

Dr Ingrid Vasseur, the paper's second author, said the station's move to a
digital form was "a genuine attempt at transparency that the practice
itself simply migrated past," and that a natural next question was what a
favour extended at the studio door is actually weighing when a producer or
a presenter decides to extend one.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.sah9b9.
