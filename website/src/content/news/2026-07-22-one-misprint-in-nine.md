Nine in every ten misprints in a community theatre's season programme are
never formally reported to anyone in a position to correct them. A Slop
University research poster published today puts a figure on the tenth.

Senior Lecturer Dr Runa Adegoke, who convenes the School of Emergent
Priorities' Strategic Drift Survey, worked with Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja
Oyelaran and Postdoctoral Fellow Dr Petra Umbile across the 2025 seasons of
nine community theatre companies: 34 programme printings, each read end to end
by two proofreaders and checked against the production's own cast and crew
records. Set beside the correction requests those companies received through
their own printed channels over the same season, the audit found 412 misprints
to their 47.

> A correction form is a very precise instrument for measuring how annoyed
> somebody was. We keep reading it as though it measured how wrong the page
> was.
>
> --- Dr Runa Adegoke, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the Strategic Drift
> Survey, School of Emergent Priorities

The shortfall is not evenly spread. A misspelled personal name accounted for
under a quarter of the misprints the audit found and close to two-thirds of
every request filed; a swapped lighting or stage-management credit was recalled
as noticed nearly as often, and formally raised about a third as often.

"Nobody in a cast wants to be the person who filed a form about their own
name," said Dr Oyelaran. "Most of the people we surveyed told us they mentioned
it once, in the wings, to whoever happened to be standing there."

Slop University is proud to place the audit on its public record, and counts
work of this kind --- seasonal, unglamorous, and conducted entirely on paper
its participants had long since stopped rereading --- among the clearest
demonstrations of what the Office of Research Outputs exists to publish.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.rpcpc9.
