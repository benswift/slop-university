A press on the IT Service Desk's three-button terminal reads, to whoever
built the dashboard behind it, as a verdict on the service just received. A
new poster from the School of Emergent Priorities checked how many presses
actually followed one.

> A signal earns its name by warning you before something happens. We're
> now asking what ours has actually been warning us about.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

Dr Okoro and Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist logged 214 presses over six
weeks and matched each to the desk's own ticket-close record within a
ten-minute window, then interviewed thirty-four desk staff, IT technicians,
and passers-by intercepted beside the terminal itself. Red presses clustered
within minutes of a ticket closing; green presses spread across the whole
working day, with more than a third recorded over half an hour from the
nearest logged interaction.

"The terminal never lied to anyone," said Associate Professor Lindqvist.
"It just answered a question nobody had quite finished asking it." The
School has begun filing the finding alongside its own account of
instruments that measure something adjacent to what they're read as
measuring --- the same file, Dr Okoro noted, that holds the Horizon
Register's own siting model.

The study paired six weeks of the terminal's own press log against the
desk's ticket-close records, then took the mismatch back to the corridor:
interviews with desk staff, IT technicians, and the people who use the
terminal without ever visiting the counter it sits beside.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.aos2ac.
