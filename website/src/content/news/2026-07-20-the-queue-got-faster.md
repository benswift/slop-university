A live reliability score can tell a waiting tennis player exactly how likely
the court ahead is to sit empty. What it cannot do, an eight-week Slop
University trial has found, is change whether it actually does. The finding
lands as part of the School of Continuous Improvement's ongoing account of
what a displayed number changes and what it only rearranges.

Researchers fitted seven community courts with the booking platform's
existing reliability score, left seven others unchanged, and tracked eight
weeks of queue behaviour across both arms. The score's own arithmetic never
moved; only whether waiting players could see it did.

No-show rates held steady across both arms for the full eight weeks. What
shifted was how quickly a freed slot found its next occupant: at the
score-displayed courts, the median wait to reclaim an empty lane fell by
two-thirds, while the score-hidden courts saw no equivalent change.

> A displayed number is very good at recruiting an audience for itself.
> Whether it changes the thing underneath is a separate question, and
> usually a slower one.
>
> --- Lecturer Marit Osayande, Convenor of the Living Dashboard

"What we've mainly learned is how quickly a queue will do a dashboard's job
for it, if you let it," said Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi. "The next question is
what that costs the people doing the filling in."

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.x95pog.
