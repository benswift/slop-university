Facilities decides which campus bike racks get more room and which get
pulled out using a single photograph, taken once a day, of every slot with a
bike in it. A new research poster from the School of Continuous Improvement
asked how much that one frame is really telling anyone.

Fourteen racks were logged every four hours for fourteen weeks, with each
occupied slot classified by how long its bike had actually been there: gone
within a day, back inside a week, or untouched longer than that. Long-stay
bikes made up under a quarter of everything tagged at the outset, but by the
study's final weeks they accounted for most of the slots the daily photo was
still recording as occupied; three of the four racks currently marked for
expansion carried a long-stay share above the study's own midpoint.

> A photo taken once a day can't ask a bike why it's still there. Ours is
> the closest we've come to asking anyway.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Renke Sabel, the poster's co-author, said the walkthrough's own count
"isn't wrong, exactly, it's just answering a question nobody meant to ask
it," and that pairing the photo with a movement threshold was "the obvious
next step before anyone spends money on more racks."

The School counts the finding less as a verdict on the racks than as what
it has learned about its own habit of trusting a single frame: precise,
tidy, and blind to everything that happened between photographs.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.2dta56.
