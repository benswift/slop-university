Members of a suburban gym chain now book classes earlier and cancel later
than they did before its app introduced a reliability score, and turn up to
class almost exactly as often as they always did. A new paper from the
School of Continuous Improvement traces the difference to a six-minute
window: the stretch of time just outside the score's two-hour cancellation
cutoff, where members increasingly wait before giving up their spot.

The University points to the study as a reminder that its measurement
scholars audit an instrument just as readily when it belongs to somebody
else's business as when it belongs to the University's own indicator
estate.

> A promotion algorithm only has to notice when someone books and cancels.
> It was never going to notice why, and the six minutes we found are what
> happens once nobody has to answer for it.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab

Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi said the finding sits inside the School's wider
interest in what a well-behaved dashboard can hide about the routine
sitting underneath it, and that the team plans to follow the same six
minutes into other booking systems next.

The team matched twenty weeks of the app's own booking and cancellation
logs against door-scanner attendance counts across 22 sites, isolating the
score's effect from an unplanned natural experiment: a backend migration
left four sites displaying the score without its waitlist-promotion
mechanism actually running, letting the researchers separate what the
number's presence changed from what its machinery did.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ln1awt.
