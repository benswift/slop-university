> A bin's decline is easy to read as progress until you ask what it left
> out. We would rather publish the harder number than the flattering one.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas, School of Continuous Improvement

Facilities consolidated the campus's scattered outdoor smoking points into a
single covered shelter last year, and the shelter's own monthly tally of
collected cigarette butts has fallen steadily ever since. A new research
poster from the School of Continuous Improvement set out to check what that
tally was actually tracking.

The study set the shelter's own collection record against an independently
run litter count, kept to the same schedule but answerable to no dashboard,
tracing collection figures across a full academic year and the term either
side of it.

Over fourteen months, the shelter's own log fell from 340 butts a month to
132, a decline of 61%. The independent sweep, covering the shelter and the
six outdoor points it replaced, recorded a fall of only 9% over the same
period, from 402 to 366.

"The interesting question isn't whether the count fell --- it did --- but
whose count we'd have believed if we hadn't run the second one," said Dr
Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter, the poster's
co-author.

The University treats the gap between the two counts as exactly the kind of
finding its evaluation-ecosystem agenda exists to surface, whichever count
it happens to flatter.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.9a6na6.
