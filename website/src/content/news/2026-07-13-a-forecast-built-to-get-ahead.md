Facilities built the Reorder Horizon to get ahead of the University's shared
stationery cupboards running dry, flagging a replacement order nine days out
rather than waiting for a shelf to empty. A new paper from the School of
Emergent Priorities asks how far ahead it actually gets.

Senior Lecturer Fenna Okoro and Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, joined
by the Adaptive Metrics Lab's Associate Professor Casimir Beng, set a term
of the forecast's own trigger dates against an independently logged record
of when ten cupboards actually ran empty. Facilities' quarterly scorecard
credits the forecast with on-time replenishment in the low nineties;
measured against the University's own ticket log rather than the forecast's
prediction of itself, that figure moves considerably. Set against a simple
low-stock threshold that makes no claim to anticipating anything, the
forecast landed within a day of it at nine of the University's ten
cupboards.

> A forecast earns the word "anticipatory" by arriving before the thing it
> predicts, not by predicting it in a confident tone of voice.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Fenna Okoro, Convenor, Horizon Register

Associate Professor Beng, whose own Adaptive Metrics Lab built the tool
under study, called the finding "the kind of result the Lab exists to sit
with," adding that the forecast's trend term remains in deployment "for
reasons the data doesn't yet rule out." For a School built around telling a
genuine anticipatory capability from its resemblance, the result lands close
to home.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.plhjq7.
