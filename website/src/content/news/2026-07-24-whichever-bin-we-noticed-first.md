A bin rolled level with whichever one the neighbours already put out,
rather than the laminated roster nailed to the verge post, is the whole
argument behind the School of Emergent Priorities' newest bus-stop
campaign.

Unveiled today under the line "Whichever Bin, We Noticed First," the
artwork sets a wheelie bin mid-roll on a quiet street, drawn level with
next door's kerbed pair instead of the sign meant to say which week is
which. It runs across the University's bus-stop panels and campus digital
screens, with a paired night rendering for after dark.

> Twenty-four streets and several collection cycles taught us how a roster
> loses its audience. Thirty-odd words on a bus shelter is a faster way to
> lose one.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow

The finding underneath, published with Dr Iben Chikere as "Whichever Bin
the Neighbours Already Rolled," audited two dozen residential streets whose
laminated bin-night rosters vary naturally in age, tracking how far kerb
placement drifts from the posted schedule as a sign gets older, and how
much of that drift is absorbed by residents simply matching a neighbour's
bin instead. An embedded test found that swapping the laminated sign for a
QR code linked to a live digital roster did nothing to close the gap.

"A roster earns obedience for exactly as long as somebody's still reading
it," said Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities. "After
that, the street just watches itself."

The School treats the campaign as a cheerfully literal case of its own
research question --- how a stated arrangement survives contact with the
people meant to keep following it. The full advertisement is available from
the University's research repository under an open licence,
doi:10.5555/slop.ye3nou.
