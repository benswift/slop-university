A suburban council's business case for extending a dog park's off-lead
hours rested on a single instrument: the automatic counter bolted to the
park's own gate. A new research poster from the School of Continuous
Improvement asks whether that counter can be trusted at the hours the
decision actually needed it.

> A counter earns the trust of everyone who never has to stand next to it
> and check.
>
> --- Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Indicator Commons,
> School of Continuous Improvement

Across a single off-lead park and twenty-four weeks, Dr Sabel and Dr
Thandiwe Solberg set ninety-six manual headcounts, taken in thirty-minute
blocks spanning dawn to dusk, against the gate counter's own running
tally, holding the sensor's mounting and firmware constant throughout.

Agreement between the two counts holds above nine in ten sessions at
midday, then slides to a little over half at the park's dawn opening,
precisely the block the council's expansion business case drew its
dawn-hours modelling from.

"What we'd like to know next is how many other hours-of-operation
decisions in this city are leaning on a sensor nobody has stood beside,"
said Dr Solberg.

The School of Continuous Improvement folds the result into a wider ledger
it has kept for years: which of the University's borrowed instruments earn
the least scrutiny precisely where they're leaned on hardest.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.z1gj28.
