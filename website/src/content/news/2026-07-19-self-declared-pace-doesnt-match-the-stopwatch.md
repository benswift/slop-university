A public pool's lane system runs on an honesty system: swim in the lane
that matches how fast you say you are. A new research poster from the
School of Emergent Priorities puts a stopwatch to that honesty system for
the first time, timing 90 swimmers across six suburban pools against the
pace band each of them had declared at the lane's edge.

> We assumed the middle band would be the honest one. It turns out the
> middle band is just where over- and under-claiming happen to cancel out.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke, Convenor of the Strategic Drift Survey

Dr Runa Adegoke and Dr Solveig Adeyemi hand-timed fourteen weeks of
50-metre splits and set each one against a short pace scale swimmers filled
in on arrival. Self-report and stopwatch agreed only moderately: swimmers
who declared the fast band consistently swam slower than they claimed, and
swimmers who declared the slow band consistently swam faster, with the
pattern sharpest in the evening session. The University reads the mismatch
as more than a curiosity of self-assessment: folded into a pool's crowding
index, it tracked lane pressure more closely than a plain headcount managed
on its own.

"We would like to know whether the same over- and under-shoot holds at a
running track's pace groups," said Solveig Adeyemi, Lecturer. "A stopwatch
is cheap; a pool is just where we happened to have one first."

The University considers the validation exactly the kind of unglamorous
checking work its research programme exists to do, and the School of
Emergent Priorities intends to carry the method beyond the six pools it
started in.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.e2iv9l.
