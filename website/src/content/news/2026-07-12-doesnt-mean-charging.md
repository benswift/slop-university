Next year's business case for more EV-charging bays rests on one figure: how
often the University's eighteen bollards report themselves in use. A new
poster from the School of Emergent Priorities checked whether that figure
survives a clamp meter, and whether an earlier finding about a different
Facilities dashboard would survive a second one.

Dr Fenna Okoro and Dr Ingrid Vasseur fitted eighteen bollards across three
car parks with inline current clamps, running them for twelve weeks
alongside the dashboard's own connector-sense log. A bay counted as
"utilised" the moment a cable connected; the clamp told a different story.
Reservation-only connections, plugged in but drawing under 200 watts, rose
from a fifth to well over half of all logged bay-time across the window,
while metered active charging held flat.

> The Horizon Register logs what's coming. This is the first time we've had
> to ask whether what's coming was ever really there.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register

The replication succeeds and fails in the same result: the pattern from the
water-refill study reappears, but not for the same reason. A tap trips a
hydration-station counter regardless of whether a bottle fills; a bollard's
cable trips its counter regardless of whether the car behind it is
finished, full, or just parked somewhere it won't move. Dr Vasseur, whose
research follows how a scanning exercise's open questions narrow into an
agenda item, called the reservation share "the kind of number a business
case would rather round before committee sees it."

The University reads the result as a useful check ahead of a decision that
was resting on an unchecked figure, and expects it to reach committee
alongside the expansion case.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.8rf611.
