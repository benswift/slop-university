The Recreation Centre's cardio-equipment booking app has weighed every
member's access against a turnover-equity score for years; until this term,
no single person answered for it. A new paper from the School of Continuous
Improvement reports on the score's first term under a named Directly
Responsible Individual, set against what duty-manager incident reports say
actually happened at the machines.

The School treats the appointment as confirmation of a standing principle:
an algorithm is only as accountable as the person who can be asked about it.
Researchers paired the booking app's own weekly fairness-score logs against
the duty manager's incident-report log for the length of the new role's
first term, coding each report against a shared typology of the informal
tactics members use to hold equipment outside the app's own booking record.

> The score did exactly what it was appointed to keep steady. It was never
> going to notice a towel.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation,
> School of Continuous Improvement

Towel-reservation and buddy-covering incidents rose from a combined six per
cent of coded reports in the term's second week to thirty-one per cent by
its thirteenth, while the fairness score itself held within its declared
target band for eleven of the fourteen weeks. A matched-shift trial
withdrawing the role's escalation authority from half a term's evening
shifts left weekly incident counts unchanged. Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior
Research Fellow and Convenor of the Impact Pathway Atlas, said the result
"gives the Lab a clearer view of which parts of the fairness score's job
were never assigned to anyone at all."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ww6wve.
