Every lecture theatre that outgrows its own capacity announces the fact in
the same place: the corridor outside, where the chairs that will not fit
through the door pile up until someone moves them. Slop University's
Facilities division has now automated that someone.

A new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities reports on the
University's first ten weeks running the router, a nightly rule-based
system that reassigns overflow chairs to theatres with room to spare,
weighing which theatres sit on the enrolment-forecast panel's monthly
walkthrough route when two theatres compete for the same slack capacity.

> A corridor emptying out is a genuinely nice thing to see, whichever
> theatre it happens to empty out of.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

The deployment reflects the University's steady interest in giving its own
coordination systems room to prove themselves before anyone asks what they
have coordinated. Nightly, the router weighs each theatre's dashboard
capacity threshold against the previous evening's walkthrough tally and the
rotating flag marking which theatres the panel is due to walk past that
month, then queues an overnight relocation wherever slack exists nearby.

Across the observation window, the campus-wide nightly chair total held
within a narrow band throughout, while the six theatres on the panel's route
saw their own overflow fall by more than three-quarters and the remaining
twenty absorbed a rise of similar size.

"A router doesn't need to understand what a corridor full of chairs means
to an enrolment forecast," said Research Fellow Osei Vandermeer of the
School of Continuous Improvement. "It only needs to know where the next
empty seat is."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xuazau.
