The gap between a permission and its expiry is basically all Professor
Verity Marris researches, and her school's newest recruitment campaign puts
the smallest possible version of it on a bus shelter: a microchip pet door
that never quite caught up with a lapsed subscription.

Running across the University's bus-stop panels and campus screens under the
line "The Door Still Remembers You," the campaign features Professor Marris
crouched at a suburban doorstep, clipboard in hand, inspecting a flap that
has been quietly admitting an unauthorised guest for months. The School of
Emergent Priorities doesn't treat the casting as a stretch. An
authorisation, at any scale, tends to outlast the arrangement that granted
it, and a household's smallest access point is as good a place as any to
start counting.

> The arrangement ends quietly. The access rarely does.
>
> --- Professor Verity Marris, Director, Trajectory Analytics Group

The Trajectory Analytics Group built its whole research agenda on that lag
between decision and consequence, and treats a domestic pet door as a small,
low-stakes proof of concept for a pattern it usually finds at institutional
scale.

"A subscription and a strategic priority end the same way," said Associate
Professor Kwame Lindqvist, Director of the Master of Priority Studies.
"Somebody stops paying, and it takes the mechanism a surprisingly long time
to notice."
