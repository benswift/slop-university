> A formula that names honesty as an input and then barely uses it isn't
> lying, exactly. It's just telling you where the honesty term actually sits
> in the design.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

Facilities' entrance-level Recovery Score decides which of the University's
fourteen umbrella-loan bins get priority restocking each term, and its own
documentation credits the decision to two things: how easy a bin is to reach,
and how honestly its borrowers have behaved. A new paper from the School of
Emergent Priorities set out to find how much work that second factor is
actually doing.

Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist and Dr Marek Solheim surveyed 163
registered borrowers across all fourteen sites on their return habits, paced
the walking distance from each bin to its nearest entrance, and logged a week
of peak-hour foot traffic. Modelling the published Score against the three
measures found distance responsible for the substantial majority of the
pattern, foot traffic a distant second, and self-reported honesty barely
registering. Dropping the honesty term from the model and recalculating which
bins would be restocked first left the University's priority order almost
exactly where it started.

The School reads the result as a case for checking what a formula's own named
inputs are contributing, not assuming the naming settled the question.

"We didn't set out to catch anyone in a technicality," said Dr Solheim. "The
Score isn't wrong about which bins need attention. It's just quieter than its
own paperwork about why."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.cocm20.
