Ninety staff share one microwave in a ground-floor break room, and for three
months running, "kitchen-amenity fairness" topped the service log's complaint
categories. Facilities' response was the Reheat Slot Roster: a wall board
where staff write their name against a ten-minute slot, one in four reserved
as a Quick-Reheat Lane. A new paper from the School of Continuous Improvement
checked what the roster actually changed.

Dr Sten Okwuosa and Dr Thandiwe Solberg tracked complaint volume for two
quarters after deployment, alongside a stopwatched field measurement of
wait-to-reheat time across matched lunch-hour samples before and after the
roster went up. Complaints fell by four-fifths. The measured wait did not
move. A further quarter spent trialling the roster without its Quick-Reheat
Lane changed neither figure.

> A complaint is one of the few signals a shared kitchen appliance sends
> anyone at all, so it's an easy one to build a whole success story on before
> you've checked what else moved.
>
> --- Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway
> Atlas

The school treats the gap as exactly the kind of case its evaluation-ecosystem
agenda exists to catch: a legible, low-cost fix that retires the complaint a
problem is filed under without retiring the problem the complaint was
supposed to index. "The wait never filed a ticket," said Dr Solberg, "so
checking it took a stopwatch instead of a service log." Facilities'
continuation report credits the roster with resolving the issue; the paper
does not dispute that the complaints stopped, only what stopping them proves.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.yvfijy.
