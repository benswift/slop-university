Fourteen confidential-waste bins beside the University's busiest
photocopiers have spent three years reporting exactly how full they are,
and three years watching a fortnightly collection run that has never once
used that information, according to a new research poster from the School
of Emergent Priorities.

> The bins were never the interesting part. What's interesting is how
> comfortably a schedule can sit beside data that's contradicted it for
> three years, and still call itself under review.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The pilot fill-level sensor was fitted to the bins to test
demand-responsive scheduling, one of several instruments the School has set
loose on the University's more provisional arrangements. Across thirty-two
sampled collection cycles, its readings tracked the preceding week's
photocopier print volume closely --- during assessment periods, several
monitored bins reached most of their capacity almost a week before anyone
arrived to empty them. The collection interval held at fourteen days
throughout.

"Every capital-works cycle, the same fourteen bins get costed as though
they arrived last month," said Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist. "The
pilot line has been renewed so often it's practically tenured."

The pattern fits a School whose research keeps turning up the parts of the
institution nobody has been assigned to finish, and the finding extends its
long-running interest in arrangements that outlast the review meant to
settle them.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.uvrtkn.
