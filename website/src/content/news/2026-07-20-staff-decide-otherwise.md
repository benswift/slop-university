A booking app's no-show fee is designed to remove judgement from the desk: a
client fails to show, the system flags it, the charge follows. A new Slop
University research poster from the School of Emergent Priorities and the
School of Continuous Improvement finds the judgement never actually left.

Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran and Dr Anneke Tolan audited nine months of
booking-app fee logs across 46 salons and interviewed twenty-four
receptionists and managers about how a flagged no-show is actually
resolved. Staff waived the charge in roughly two cases out of five every
quarter, a share the app's escalating reminder cadence left untouched, and
the app's own dispute-resolution log accounted for only a small fraction of
those waivers.

> What a receptionist decides to overlook has never shown up on an org
> chart, and this project is the closest the School has come to drawing
> one.
>
> --- Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior Research Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

"The next round adds a field for the stated reason," said Senior Lecturer
Ronja Oyelaran, "mostly to find out whether staff use it, or write around
it the way they write around everything else."

The finding sits inside the School of Emergent Priorities' broader
interest in the distance between a stated policy and what actually happens
at the counter, and complements the School of Continuous Improvement's
long-running work on instruments that assume their own compliance.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.v55q91.
