A hired graduation gown changes hands more than the hire desk's paperwork
admits. Numbered fibre tags, stitched into the collar lining of 214 gowns
across the University's Autumn and Spring 2025 conferrals, let a new poster
from the School of Emergent Priorities check the desk's damage-fee ledger
against the ceremony's own photographs. The tags found that more than a
third of tracked gowns were worn, at the moment the shutter clicked, by
someone other than the name on the hire docket. The desk's damage fees
followed the docket name regardless --- a gap the School of Emergent
Priorities has been quietly cataloguing wherever a record and the thing it
records stop lining up.

> A docket says who signed. A photograph says who was standing there. We
> just wanted to know which one the fee actually listens to.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

A second phase of the study put the same tag-to-photograph mismatch report
in front of hire-desk staff before they billed a fresh batch of cases, to
see whether the paperwork would follow the evidence once the evidence was
sitting on the counter. "Handing someone a better record and watching what
they do with it is its own kind of instrument," said Dr Dagny Okafor, who
convenes the School of Continuous Improvement's Evaluation of Evaluation
program. "This one just happened to be a gown."

The method, in short: covert fibre tags gave each gown an identity
independent of its hire docket, letting the ceremony's own proof-sheet
photographs stand as a second, unrelated record of who actually wore it.
Damage-fee attribution was then coded against both records across a before
and an after batch.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.fe4usp.
