At gas-bottle swap cages run by a hardware chain, a photo-based
condition-check app now looks at every returned cylinder before a staff
member does. A new Slop University research poster from the School of
Emergent Priorities and the School of Continuous Improvement asks what the
second look catches that the first one didn't, tracking both gradings
against an independent inspector's blind reassessment across a full
season.

Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran and Lecturer Marit Osayande followed 214
cylinders through six swap cages over fourteen weeks, having each one
scored three separate times, by counter staff, by the app, and by an
inspector working blind to both, with the scoring order rotated week to
week so neither check could learn the other's habits.

> A second check only earns its keep if it catches something the first
> one missed; ours mostly just confirmed it.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran, School of Emergent Priorities

App-graded and staff-graded calls agreed with each other on 96% of
cylinders, and matched the inspector's independent read at closely
comparable rates across the season. "The app didn't need to be wrong to
be expensive," said Lecturer Marit Osayande, Convenor of the Living
Dashboard. "A season of added counter time is the kind of cost that
rarely shows up on the same ledger as the safety case that justified it."

For the School of Emergent Priorities, a second check that mostly agrees
with the first is its own kind of finding, and the swap cages gave the
School an unusually clean one to sit with this season.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.b7j0ib.
