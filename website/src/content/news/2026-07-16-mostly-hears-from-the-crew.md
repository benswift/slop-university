Every restroom exit kiosk on campus puts the same four-button question to
whoever walks past it, and until this term nobody had checked who was
actually answering. A new research poster from the School of Continuous
Improvement, _Nobody's Unhappy at 6 AM_, matches two buildings' worth of
kiosk button-press timestamps against the cleaning contractor's own
service-scan log across a full semester, and finds one rating answering back
suspiciously fast.

> A four-button kiosk can only ever ask the person standing in front of it,
> and lately that person is increasingly on the clock.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

The team paired every logged press against the nearest preceding badge-tap
service scan across six kiosks, then compared the timing against what an
even spread of presses across each cleaning round would produce. Four in ten
of the kiosk's top rating landed inside ninety seconds of a scan; every other
rating scattered across the whole gap between rounds, matching what the even
spread would predict.

"The score was never asked to say who pressed it, only what got pressed,"
said Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter,
School of Continuous Improvement. Facilities has confirmed the kiosk's
monthly figure will get a second look before it's next cited in the
contractor's service review --- the operational use the Office of Research
Outputs points to as the finding's most immediate consequence.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.1rx612.
