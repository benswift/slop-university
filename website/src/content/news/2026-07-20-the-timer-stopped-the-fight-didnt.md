A household timeout timer's countdown reliably ends a session. Whether it
ends the argument is a separate question, and a new Slop University paper
finds the two rarely finish at the same moment.

For a School built around the gap between a measurement and the thing it
measures, a kitchen countdown turned out to be an unusually literal example
to study.

> Every family that let us sit down with a week of their own timeout log, in
> the middle of the school holidays, gave us something no dataset alone
> could: the difference between what a session closed as and what it
> actually felt like to be in the room for.
>
> --- Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Over the July school holidays, Dr Chikere and Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi sat
down with a parent from each of 34 households already using a shared
household timeout-timer app, working back through a week of the app's own
logged sessions and asking, one at a time, when the argument had actually
stopped, independent of what the countdown showed.

Across 71 sessions, the app's own closing record matched a parent's account
in well under a third of cases; a further, stricter test, requiring an
explicit apology before counting an argument as settled, moved that figure
only slightly.

"What we didn't ask, and probably should, is what the app owes a household
once it turns out that closing a session and settling an argument aren't the
same event," said Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.b8wknf.
