Twelve golf buggies work the Grounds depot's maintenance runs. Until this
term, taking one meant writing a name and a departure time on a laminated
sheet, with no note of which buggy still had charge in it. A new paper from
the School of Continuous Improvement built the fix: a phone panel showing
each buggy's live charge beside a one-tap request button. Ten weeks of
before-and-after records suggest the fix mostly changed how long the fix
took to use.

> We gave people better information and watched what they did with it.
> Mostly, they read it and took the buggy nearest the door anyway.
>
> --- Dr Osei Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor, Review Cadence
> Observatory

Across 34 staff and twenty weeks of requests, the share choosing the
nearest buggy regardless of its charge barely moved: 87% before the tool,
85% after, and charge at departure told the same story. What did move was
the clock: median decision-to-departure time rose from nine seconds to
nearly a minute once the tool launched. A four-week trial hiding the charge
figure left selection unchanged, tracing most of the added time to
consulting a screen rather than to anything it showed.

"A shorter walk beat a better number every time we looked," said Associate
Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "The tool earns
its keep on the maintenance side; it just isn't doing the persuading we
built it to do."

Ten weeks of paper sign-out logs were measured against ten weeks of
tool-mediated requests from the same staff and fleet, isolating the
display's content from its mere presence. The null result belongs in the
record, the School says, rather than a drawer.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.jac264.
