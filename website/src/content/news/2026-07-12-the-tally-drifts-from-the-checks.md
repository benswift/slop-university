For one teaching period, Slop University kept two records of the same
first-year lecture: how many hands went up, and what a short check
afterwards showed the room actually understood. A new poster from the
School of Continuous Improvement is the first to hold those two records
against each other.

The pairing tests the figure behind the Device-Free Hour pilot's
continuation case: a hand-raise tally logged across ten weeks of
device-free sessions. Dr Thandiwe Solberg and Dr Joost Nwosu surveyed 214
first-year students and seven lecturing staff across sixteen sessions in
four courses, scoring a short comprehension check blind to that session's
tally. The hand-raise count explained about an eighth of the spread in
comprehension scores, and the gap between the two measures widened for
most of the pilot's ten weeks.

> A tally that only grows doesn't yet tell you what to do about it. That's
> a fair question for whoever inherits ours next.
>
> --- Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Demo Quarter

The University treats the pairing as a modest but telling sign of a
measurement culture that has learned to check its own headline number
occasionally, not just circulate it. Reading the tally against the
comprehension checks rather than on its own, the team also found lecturing
staff's engagement ratings tracking the raised hands considerably more
closely than they tracked what students could show they had understood.

Dr Joost Nwosu, whose research follows what becomes of a performance
target once it has been met, said the pattern was a familiar one: a count
"that gets easier to hit than to interpret, once enough of a room is
aiming at it."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.vbokno.
