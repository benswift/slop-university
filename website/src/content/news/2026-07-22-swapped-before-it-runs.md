> Give four coworkers a fair algorithm and a group chat, and you find out
> fast which one they actually trust with the roster.
>
> --- Senior Research Fellow Dr Anneke Tolan, School of Emergent Priorities

A workplace carpool group has never needed much to decide whose turn it is
to drive: a running joke, a text the night before, an unspoken exception for
whoever has an early meeting. Last year the School of Emergent Priorities
offered six such groups, across two suburban workplaces, a tidier answer
anyway --- Fair Turn, a small tool that computes a weekly driver assignment
designed to even out the driving load, and posts it straight into the
group's existing chat.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingrid Vasseur and Senior Research Fellow Dr Anneke
Tolan tracked all six groups for fourteen weeks, comparing the tool's
assigned driver each week against who actually drove. About two in five
assigned turns were swapped before the week was out, at both workplaces, at
a rate that held steady whether or not the swap form asked members to say
why. Most of those swaps, the pair found, were arranged first in the
group's own chat and only entered into the tool afterwards, if at all.

"An algorithm can compute a fair turn all it likes," said Dr Vasseur.
"Whether it's the turn that happens is a second, separate question, and
this one mostly answered itself before the roster ever loaded."

For the School of Emergent Priorities, the deployment sits alongside a
wider question about where an anticipated arrangement and a lived one
quietly part ways --- a question the School expects to keep asking long
after this particular roster stops loading.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.k74ebc.
