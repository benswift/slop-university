Ninety-seven percent of the alerts a strata building's video intercom sends
out are marked "acknowledged" within five minutes of arriving. Whether
anyone read them is a separate question, and a new paper from the School of
Continuous Improvement spent sixty-four interviews finding out.

The University reads the finding as one more entry in the School's account
of audits that keep their own numbers clean well past the point those
numbers stop describing anything.

> A five-minute window was built to ask whether someone checked the door.
> It was never built to notice that the fastest possible tap and the least
> attentive one have become the same gesture.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab

Across nine strata schemes, residents of three years or more were more
likely to dismiss the notification unread than to check who had buzzed in,
a practice barely present among residents under a year in their unit. The
building's own dashboard read the change as an improvement: the same
longer-tenured residents logged a faster time-to-acknowledge than anyone
else in the sample.

Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi said the finding fits a pattern she has noticed in
her own work on incident reports that close themselves out long before
anyone reads what is in them, and that the team is glad of a second setting
to test it in.

The team coded each interview into one of four ways of handling the
notification, checked it against each building's own compliance export, then
added a "please confirm you saw this" prompt to three of the nine buildings
for six weeks. The practice mix afterwards was indistinguishable from the
six buildings that received no prompt.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.22tpz2.
