A path worn into the Main Quad's lawn will keep wearing back in, a new Slop
University paper finds, no matter which route the campus wayfinding app
recommends instead.

The University's wayfinding application began surfacing a paved alternative
to the library-courtyard shortcut in week ten of the study term, a change
intended to fold two under-used paved segments into one corridor worth
maintaining. The paper checks what happened to the lawn itself once the
recommendation went live.

> A register is only as good as its capacity to notice when its own forecast
> doesn't land. This one didn't, and it will carry that lesson into whatever
> we forecast next.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

The study paired a weekly photographic record of the lawn, taken across a
full term either side of the app's routing launch, with the application's own
anonymised usage logs, using each measure to check the other.

The original desire path's worn area held steady before and after the
launch, while a second track matching the app's suggested line began
appearing within three weeks and continued to widen for the rest of the
term. Students told the app they would take the new route far more often
than photographs of the corner showed them actually taking it, a gap that
held about as wide for first-year students as for everyone else.

Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics Group, said
the finding belongs less to wayfinding than to anticipation: "A system that
tells us what students will do next is only useful once we've checked
whether they did."

The University treats the gap between what an app logs and what a lawn shows
as exactly the kind of evidence its research mission exists to surface.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.p1f1y9.
