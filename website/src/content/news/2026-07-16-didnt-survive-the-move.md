The School of Emergent Priorities has made a habit lately of asking whether a
working measurement can be trusted anywhere else it's pointed. Its newest
research poster turns that question on an auto-reorder rule the University
quietly ported from a confidential-waste-bin sensor pilot to the paper trays
feeding its lecture-hall printers, and finds the correlation the rule assumed
does not survive the move.

> A bin fills the way a bin fills. A printer tray empties the way an
> assignment is due, and nobody wrote that difference into the rule.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The retest fitted twelve lecture-theatre printer stations with the same
tray-level sensor the original bin pilot used, ported that pilot's reorder
threshold without adjustment, and ran a parallel log of every printroom
override across a full teaching term, uninstructed on the rule the log was
quietly checking.

Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, a co-author on both the original pilot and its
retest, called the override log "the instrument nobody thought to budget for,
and the one that ended up carrying the study" — the University counts that
kind of self-correction among the discipline its research culture is
supposed to practise on itself, not just recommend to others.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.yepz3p.
