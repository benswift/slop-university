Improvement Grand Rounds took attendance by card tap for three years, until
an internal review last year raised doubts about what the taps were actually
recording. The School of Continuous Improvement's fortnightly case-review
seminar switched back to a paper sign-in sheet, on the understanding that a
signature is harder to fake than a borrowed card. A new paper puts that
understanding to a first test.

> A clipboard reads as more trustworthy than a card reader mostly because
> nobody has checked. This is us checking.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation

Lecturer Dagny Okafor, Lecturer Bram Ntuli, Postdoctoral Fellow Mirela
Hanke, and Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel built a lightweight tool that scores
each semester signature against that signer's own earliest mark, flagging
any that drifts unusually far from it. About one in nine signature
instances was flagged. The team then checked those flagged instances
against the old card reader, left switched on beside the door even after
it stopped being the official record, and found flagged signatures
somewhat more likely to be missing a matching tap --- a gap the paper is
careful not to oversell.

"We convene Grand Rounds to look hard at everybody else's review formats,"
said Dr Bram Ntuli, a co-author and the series' convenor. "It was only
fair to turn the same treatment on our own sign-in sheet."

The paper sits inside the School's growing habit of measuring its own
measuring, rather than assuming an instrument means what its name
promises. The School has no plans to change how Grand Rounds records
attendance for now, and will revisit the question once a full year of
sign-in data is available.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.k7y0kk.
