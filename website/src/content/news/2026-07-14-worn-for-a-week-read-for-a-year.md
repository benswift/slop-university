Nine weeks after orientation, most commencing students can still say which
colour lanyard they wore --- and for one of the two colours, that memory
comes with a cost.

The lanyard scheme was built for a single Monday: colour by admission
pathway, so tour leaders could route ATAR-entry and alternative-entry groups
to pathway-tailored campus tours. A new poster from the School of Emergent
Priorities finds the colour outlived its Monday. Surveyed at week nine, more
than half of 612 commencing students could still name their own lanyard
colour, and students who wore the alternative-entry grey reported a small
but steadily widening sense of being treated differently by tutors and
peers --- a gap gold-lanyard students did not describe.

The School reads the finding as confirmation of what its
anticipatory-capability agenda has long argued: a system built to answer one
question rarely retires just because the question is settled.

> A lanyard is supposed to come off at the end of the day. Ours apparently
> doesn't.
>
> --- Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

"Nothing about this scheme was designed to last past Monday morning," said
Dr Marek Solheim. "We're interested in what a system keeps doing once
nobody's watching it anymore."

The method, in brief: a week-nine survey paired self-reported
lanyard-colour recall with a five-item scale of perceived treatment, timed
and ordered at random so no student could anticipate what the questionnaire
was really asking, with tutors kept unaware of the study's subject
throughout.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.5ylchv.
