By the fourth week of term, RackWatch's live map had all but stopped getting
visitors. The Estates Committee's minutes did not seem to notice.

RackWatch layers a crowd-sourced QR-code flag over Facilities' long-running
Tuesday headcount of the University's forty-two bike-rack clusters, letting
the occupancy map update between counts rather than only once a week. A new
paper from the School of Emergent Priorities set a full twelve-week term of
the map's own server logs against the committee minutes that cite it.
Visits fell from 812 in the first week to single figures by the fourth and
stayed there. Citations of "RackWatch data" held between one and three a
week throughout, and 87.5% of the term's rack-removal decisions cited the
tool in both of its two removal rounds — before and after Facilities added a
visible "last updated" disclaimer to the map's own header, a change that
moved neither figure.

> A citation and a visit are supposed to be the same habit wearing two
> names. Ours came apart within a month.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

"It's the same page, technically," said Lecturer Marek Solheim, a co-author
on the paper. "It just hasn't been the same page for weeks by the time
anyone reads it into the record."

The School of Emergent Priorities says it would rather field the question
about RackWatch now than field it from the Estates Committee later, and
reads the paper as one instance of a wider habit worth watching across the
University's smaller, less-funded operational tools.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.sd7fi5.
