"The number staff most wanted was never in the survey," said Dr Osei
Vandermeer, Research Fellow and Convenor of the Review Cadence Observatory in
the School of Continuous Improvement. "They wanted to know whether being
paused meant anything at all."

New findings from the School of Continuous Improvement compare eighteen
University initiatives --- nine placed in Improvement-Holding Status, a
practice that withdraws an initiative's indicators from the Living Dashboard
while it receives additional support, and nine matched initiatives that
stayed visible throughout. Surveyed staff reported near-identical
improvement in both groups by the time each initiative reached its next
Review Cadence Observatory review.

The release marks the first time the University's own governance practices,
rather than its teaching or service systems, have supplied the subject of an
internal study.

Staff were far from unified on what the pause is actually for. General staff
mostly read it as a way of keeping a struggling initiative out of the
reporting cycle; initiative leads mostly read the identical practice as
protective.

"Both readings can be true of the same event, which is what makes it worth
sitting with rather than resolving in either direction," said Dr Petra
Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Emergent Priorities.

The School frames the finding as continuous with its broader work on what a
measurement instrument comes to mean for the people it measures once its
readings stop appearing in public.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.q39tzj.
