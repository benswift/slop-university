A percentage on a park noticeboard is supposed to describe what already
happened underneath it. A new research poster from the School of Continuous
Improvement asks what happens once the number starts describing what happens
next instead.

Dr Solveig Adeyemi and Dr Bram Ntuli tracked forty-six site-weeks across
seven council dog parks fitted with a live off-leash compliance sign, setting
its overnight reading against the leash-law citations rangers issued in the
same week, and against when each patrol actually rolled out the gate.
Citations agreed with the sign only loosely across the panel. Patrol timing
didn't: on weeks the sign read high overnight, the next patrol arrived
markedly earlier than usual, whatever the complaint line had logged.

> A sign like this one isn't just reporting on the park any more. It's
> started telling the roster what to expect before anyone's phoned anything
> in.
>
> --- Dr Solveig Adeyemi, Lecturer, School of Continuous Improvement

Co-author Dr Bram Ntuli, Lecturer and Convenor of Improvement Grand Rounds,
said the pattern was a familiar shape seen from a different angle: "we spend
our Grand Rounds asking what a standing review leaves off the record. Here
it's the roster doing the leaving, and the record is a number nobody rang in
to begin with."

The poster sits alongside a run of School of Continuous Improvement work
asking the same question of the University's own registers, this time
turning the instrument on a system well outside campus altogether.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.n00a4y.
