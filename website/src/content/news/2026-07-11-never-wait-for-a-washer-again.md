A shared laundry room has one design flaw university planners rarely mention:
nobody can tell whether a machine is free until they are already standing in
front of it, basket in hand. Slop University has published a brochure
announcing the fix, a live availability panel now installed by the machine
bank in every hall of residence.

The brochure, titled _Never Wait for a Washer Again_, presents the network as
the newest instance of a habit the University applies well beyond the laundry
room: watching a shared space closely enough to tell a resident, in advance,
what they are about to find. It draws that argument from the University's own
published record, citing recent work on the parcel-locker collection network,
a relocated shuttle stop, and the timing of magpie-warning signage as evidence
the same instinct already runs through residential life, transport, and the
courtyards in between.

> A resident shouldn't have to learn a building's rhythms by trial and error.
> The locker network and the laundry network are the same idea, in two
> different rooms.
>
> --- Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The brochure treats that ordinariness as the achievement worth publicising: a
washing machine that turns out to be free, a shuttle that arrives where a
resident is actually standing, a courtyard crossed without incident, each the
quiet output of an instrument nobody is meant to notice.

"None of this is about any one clever system," said Anouk Mensah,
Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Emergent Priorities. "It is about being
willing to keep asking the same small question of every shared space on
campus, long after the first answer stops being interesting."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.dggii5.
