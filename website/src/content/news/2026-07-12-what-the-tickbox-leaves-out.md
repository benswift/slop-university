The School of Continuous Improvement has spent the better part of a year
teaching its own instruments to explain themselves. Its newest paper turns
that instinct on a fixture already wired for it: the traffic-light sensor
mounted above every silent-study floor in the University Library.

The sensor reports three states, and Facilities keeps one record of the red
ones: a "noise complaint" tickbox, ticked whenever the light turns red, with
no field for what turned it. The University reads the new result as
confirmation that its instruments have more to say than the record
currently asks of them.

Working from six weeks of sensor logs across three floors, two researchers
built and tested a six-category scheme for what actually turns the light
red, then checked how much of that detail survives into the single line
Facilities keeps for each event. Agreement between the two coders came back
strong; the survival rate into the existing record did not.

> Six seconds of plant noise and ninety seconds of conversation get exactly
> the same word in our records. We wanted to know how much detail a return
> to reading the sensor properly might buy back.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation

"It's a small scheme, and it says nothing about what the University should
do with it," said Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo
Quarter. "That's a separate question, and a fair one for someone else to
ask."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.bmm3u3.
