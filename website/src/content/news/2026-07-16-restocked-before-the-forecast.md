The sunscreen dispensers bolted to the Oval's fence line have refilled on
the same signal since they went in: someone notices the pump running dry
and files a Facilities report, usually a day or two after the sunniest
stretch has already passed. The School of Emergent Priorities has been
awarded an Anticipatory Capability Fund grant to replace that signal with
one that arrives first.

The funded protocol pairs a next-day heat forecast with a restocking
roster, moving the refill trigger ahead of the empty-bottle report by
however many days the forecast allows, and logging the interval between
forecast and refill as a readiness measure in its own right — the Fund's
first attempt to anticipate something nobody has gotten around to
complaining about yet.

> A forecast is only as useful as the first plan we're willing to move
> because of it. Sunscreen felt like the safest possible thing to test that
> on.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

Co-grantee Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics
Group, said the project would settle a narrower question within a season:
"whether a forecast can out-run a complaint, on something small enough that
getting it wrong costs nobody very much."

The grant carries $132,916 and a dashboard that will, for the first time,
put a number on the distance between a forecast and a refill.
