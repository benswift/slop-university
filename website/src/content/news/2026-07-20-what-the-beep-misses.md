Slop University's marketing output doesn't usually explain itself first.
What the Beep Misses, a new brochure from the University, opens by naming
the very thing its research keeps circling back to: the ordinary checks ---
a scanner, a printed code, a permit --- that decide something matters
before anyone official has looked at it.

The brochure draws together three of this year's published findings: a
hardware chain's gas-bottle safety scanner, a real-estate shopfront's
disclosure code, and a school pickup lane's paid priority permit. Each
study started the same way, tracing what a fast check actually confirms
once it has been running long enough for someone to notice, or not. Read
side by side, the studies read less like three unrelated errands and more
like evidence of a settled institutional habit.

> Putting a swap-cage scanner and a shopfront's disclosure code in the same
> brochure sounds like a stretch, until you notice they're answering the
> same question: what does the check confirm, once someone stops assuming
> it works.
>
> --- Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Indicator Commons,
> School of Continuous Improvement

The release also introduces two researchers to the University's
public-facing programme of lectures and open data walks: Dr Fenna Okoro,
who convenes the Horizon Register, and Dr Bram Ntuli, who convenes
Improvement Grand Rounds. "A brochure has to justify itself faster than a
paper does," said Dr Runa Adegoke, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of the
Strategic Drift Survey. "Setting three studies side by side did that
quickly, in a way none of them argued for on its own."

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ouaw4j.
