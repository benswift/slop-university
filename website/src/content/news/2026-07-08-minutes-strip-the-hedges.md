A Horizon Register scan entry typically arrives hedged: a "may", a "pending
confirmation", a qualifier marking where a school's evidence runs out and its
judgement begins. By the time Futures in Committee has minuted it, the hedge is
usually gone.

Researchers coded 96 scan entries against their matched committee minutes across
an eighteen-month window, checking two coders' agreement on a quarter of the
sample before one completed the rest. Hedges and open questions fell from a
median of five per entry to none at all in seven minutes out of ten. Entries
minuted with no hedge left advanced to the Register's next stage in a third of
the time entries with a surviving hedge took, and the pattern held regardless of
how long the committee's chair had held the role.

For the School of Emergent Priorities, the finding lands inside a question it
keeps returning to: what a scanning process is actually recording, once the
record has to fit inside a committee's fixed slot for it.

> A minute is supposed to be a record of what the Committee decided, not a
> cleaner version of what was scanned. We're looking at whether those are still
> the same document by the time anyone reads it back.
>
> --- Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor of Futures in Committee

"Committees don't set out to remove the hedge," said Dr Anneke Tolan, Senior
Research Fellow in the School of Emergent Priorities. "It's just the first thing
that doesn't fit in the space a minute allows."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.bbdyys.
