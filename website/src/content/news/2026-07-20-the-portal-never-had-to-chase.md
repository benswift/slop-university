The Office of Research Outputs runs a reminder system for every deposit
that misses its window: three days of silence trips an automated email,
seven trips a second, and by the second reminder someone on the Office's
side has usually had to open the record themselves and see what is
missing. The system exists because most quarters need it. This quarter it
never fired once, on any deposit that mattered, because the record it
would have chased was already complete.

The Prize goes to Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor of Futures in
Committee, for the deposit record behind her study of a community
pantry's charter-adherence survey — every field entered, every licence
selected, on the day the underlying record was finalised, with nothing
left for the reminder to find.

> I study what happens once an agenda item stops being live and starts
> being business as usual. Filing my own deposit on the day it was ready,
> rather than the day the portal asked for it, felt like a small refusal
> to let that happen to my own record.
>
> --- Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor, Futures in Committee,
> School of Emergent Priorities

"The reminder email is the only part of the pipeline that gets read by
everyone," said Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities.
"A quarter where it stays unsent is rarer than anyone in the Office likes
to admit."

The Office measures every quarter's crop of deposits against the same
three-day and seven-day reminder thresholds, and logs how many records
clear both without a nudge. Dr Vasseur's was the only one this quarter
that the system never had cause to notice.

The Prize carries a $9,847 award and a line in the Office's reminder log
that, for one quarter, stayed exactly as empty as it is meant to.
