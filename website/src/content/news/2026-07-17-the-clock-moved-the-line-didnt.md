When a severe-weather app moves its storm alert an hour earlier, the extra
warning has to go somewhere. A new paper from the School of Emergent
Priorities finds most of it evaporates before it reaches the clothesline.

Dr Kwame Lindqvist and Dr Petra Umbile fitted forty households' outdoor
clotheslines with a sensor recording, to the minute, when the washing was
brought in, then tracked each household through a vendor's staggered
rollout of an upgraded storm-alert model across fourteen weeks. Alert lead
time rose from a mean forty-two minutes before rain to ninety-seven; the
households' own clearance margin, the gap between line-clear and rain,
moved five minutes, a shift the team's own tests could not distinguish from
no change at all.

> An alert can move a household's clock without moving the household. This
> is the first time we've watched the two clocks side by side and seen how
> little they agree.
>
> --- Dr Kwame Lindqvist, Associate Professor, School of Emergent Priorities

The team's clearer predictor turned out to have nothing to do with the
alert's own timing. A household's history of false alarms tracked its
clearance margin more closely than lead time did: the most-wrongly-alerted
fifth of the sample cleared the line, on average, after the rain had
already started. "The app knows exactly how much earlier it's warning
someone," said Dr Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow. "It has no idea how many
times it's cried wolf on that same line before."

The School reads the result as one entry in a longer account of what an
earlier warning is actually for, an account that so far favours a
household's own memory of being wrong over anything the app can
timestamp.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.dhx4xh.
