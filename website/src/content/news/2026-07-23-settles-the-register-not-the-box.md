A "no junk mail" sticker enters a council's compliance register the day it
goes up, and from that point the register has one job: report whether the
address is still sorted. A new Slop University paper puts that single figure
alongside the people who actually decide what lands in the box, interviewing
48 admail distributors and parcel couriers across six suburbs and setting
their accounts against ten months of the council's own audit rounds. The two
rarely move together.

The findings sit inside the School of Continuous Improvement's wider work on
what a stable indicator can and cannot be taken to show --- an agenda the
School treats as unfinished by design.

> A register that hasn't moved in ten months isn't proof that nothing changed
> underneath it. It's a sign we were never measuring the part that does.
>
> --- Lecturer and Convenor Dr Marit Osayande, School of Continuous
> Improvement

The team spoke with distributors and couriers over a fourteen-week window,
then set what each group said against the register's own rounds. What one
group described as a judgment call about a faded sticker, the other described
as mail that was never addressed to them in the first place --- two different
reasons behind the same tick in the same box on the same register.

"The register calls this settled," said Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and
Convenor of Capability Sprints. "What we didn't expect was how differently
two groups of workers explain the very same tick in the box --- that's the
question we'd like the next audit to sit with."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.m634sj.
