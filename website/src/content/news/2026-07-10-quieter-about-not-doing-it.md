Facilities' Living Dashboard, the School of Continuous Improvement's
always-on self-measurement wall, credited a new lift-dispatch controller in
the University's tallest building with a 13.4% cut to the building's weekly
lift electricity draw. Rather than take the figure as read, the School
fitted an independent meter to each of the building's four lift cars and
checked the number against the machinery that actually produced it. The
audit sits inside the School's wider habit of testing an indicator against
the unit it was built to describe, rather than the unit a meter happened to
already exist at.

> A building tells you what it saved, never who saved it for. Averaging
> four lifts into one bill was never dishonest, only incomplete, and we
> wanted the incomplete part written down properly.
>
> --- Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter

The study logged each car's own energy draw across a nine-week window that
happened to catch the controller's staged rollout across the building, then
set the car-level totals against the aggregate the Dashboard had always
reported. A short trial switching off just the lobby screen that nudges
waiting passengers to share a car left the measured saving unchanged. "The
two lower cars did exactly what the controller promised," said Dr Joost
Nwosu, Postdoctoral Fellow. "The other two just got quieter about not doing
it."

The finding leaves the Dashboard's headline figure standing --- the School
is not proposing Facilities revise how it reads the switchboard --- while
adding a record of where inside the building that figure actually comes
from.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.np9tl0.
