For eleven months, a School of Continuous Improvement team treated an
apartment lobby's noticeboard as a research site in its own right ---
photographed weekly, entry by entry, rather than sampled only when a
dispute got loud enough to notice.

Lecturer and Convenor Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, Lecturer and Convenor Dr Dagny
Okafor, and Postdoctoral Fellow Dr Mirela Hanke coded 487 entries across
four blocks into six recurring types, then timed how long each type took
to read as settled. The six-type split held independently across every
block. Entries the team coded as a Ruling closed within days; entries
coded as a Complaint took roughly a week longer, on average, to reach the
same quiet end.

> Nobody on that noticeboard has ever been given the authority to decide
> anything. What we coded was how convincingly an entry could borrow it
> anyway.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation,
> School of Continuous Improvement

"The taxonomy travelled between blocks that had never compared notes,
which is the part I didn't expect going in," said Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu,
Convenor of Capability Sprints. The project extends the School's
continuing attention to which of an indicator's decisions were ever really
its own to make.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.19p288.
