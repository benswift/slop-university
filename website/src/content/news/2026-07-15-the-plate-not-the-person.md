A small brass-effect plate now sits beside eleven of the campus's water
bubblers, each engraved with a single name: the Facilities staff member the
University holds responsible if that bubbler runs dry or breaks down. A new
paper from the School of Continuous Improvement set out to test whether the
plate does anything besides look official.

Comparing 68 bubblers assigned a named owner against 78 left on the
University's ordinary, unassigned fault queue, the study found named-owner
bubblers repaired in a median 4.1 days against 7.0 days for the rest, a gap
present from the pilot's first month and unchanged by its last. A second
test complicated the obvious explanation: repair times were no different in
the weeks the named owner was rostered on than in the weeks they were on
leave.

> We evaluate accountability arrangements for a living, which makes it a
> little uncomfortable to find one of our own working for a reason we did
> not design in. We are publishing it anyway.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation,
> School of Continuous Improvement

Dr Osei Vandermeer, the paper's co-author, said the Review Cadence
Observatory would be watching whether the effect holds if the Named Asset
Ownership pilot expands beyond its three metering zones, "since an effect
that scales is still worth having, even if it isn't the effect anyone
originally asked for."

The School counts the finding as a win for its habit of auditing its own
initiatives as closely as anyone else's, whatever the audit turns up.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.74mgdl.
