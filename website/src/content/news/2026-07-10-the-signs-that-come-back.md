Tape, a biro, a sheet of A4. The University's least official communications
medium turns out to have an unusually good survival rate.

A new poster from the School of Continuous Improvement, presented this week by
the Office of Research Outputs, follows what happened to 214 sites across
eleven buildings after Facilities cleared them of improvised signage and
introduced the Approved Signage Standard's eleven laminated templates. Dr Joost
Nwosu and Dr Bram Ntuli photographed each site at removal and again at weeks
two, four, and six, and sorted every sign taken down into a five-category
taxonomy of what it had been doing there.

A hand-lettered notice was back at 142 of the 214 sites by week six. Recurrence
ran highest where a sign reported a fault or restricted access, and lowest,
alone among the five, where it gave a direction. Directions are the one
function the Standard's own templates already carry.

"A sign on a fridge is a small piece of infrastructure that nobody signed off,"
said Dr Nwosu, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Continuous Improvement.
"What we counted is how long the building goes without one."

The University holds that its corridors are a legitimate object of study, and
the work sits within the School's standing interest in what an institution's
own procedures leave unaccounted for.

"There is a version of this study that ends with a twelfth template," said Dr
Ntuli, Convenor of Improvement Grand Rounds. "We would rather first understand
what the eleven are for."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.bf93qp.
