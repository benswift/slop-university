The campus apiary's six smart hives feed the same number into every
Sustainability update: a Pollinator Index built from how much heavier the
hives get each week. A new poster from the School of Continuous Improvement
went looking for what else moves with it.

> A number that only goes up starts to feel like proof by itself. We wanted
> to know what it was proof of.
>
> --- Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Demo Quarter, School
> of Continuous Improvement

Dr Solberg and Dr Osei Vandermeer paired three garden beds within fifty
metres of a hive against three beds further than a hundred and fifty,
running fortnightly ten-minute transects across a fourteen-week season,
counting every non-honeybee visitor an observer blind to the week's
hive-weight reading could identify. The Index climbed thirty-eight per cent
over the season. Richness near the hives held at a median of three species
throughout, against six further away, a gap already present before the
Index had moved at all.

"Sensing without a comparison is just an announcement," said Dr Vandermeer.
"The far beds gave the Index something to be measured against."

The School has folded the result into its running case that a measurement
is only as good as what it has been checked against, and is recommending
the floral-resource survey the poster proposes before the Index anchors
this autumn's sustainability update.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.so11nu.
