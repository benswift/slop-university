For twelve weeks, researchers stood along the fence line of six primary
school gates, saying nothing, logging only the order families arrived in ---
work that began before the dismissal system it was watching had gone
anywhere near live.

Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Fenna Okoro, who convenes the School of
Emergent Priorities' Horizon Register, and Lecturer Dr Marek Solheim tracked
an informal early-arrival arrangement across the gates' queues from four
weeks before a tap-on dismissal system was announced through three weeks
after it switched on. The arrangement swelled through the announcement
window and receded within weeks of the system's actual start, landing close
to where it had begun --- a pattern the School reads as part of its wider
interest in what an institution's anticipation of change does before the
change itself arrives.

> The Horizon Register exists because change rarely waits for its own
> paperwork to catch up --- this is just the clearest picture we've had of
> that yet.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer and Convenor Dr Fenna Okoro, Horizon Register

The study paired a discreet, blind observation protocol with a fixed
three-phase window --- baseline, announcement, and rollout --- translating
each family's position in the queue into a single occupancy measure the
University could track without touching the dismissal system's own records.

"It's a reminder that a queue keeps its own memory of what's coming, whether
or not anyone asked it to," said Dr Marek Solheim, who contributes to the
School's anticipatory-capability agenda.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
