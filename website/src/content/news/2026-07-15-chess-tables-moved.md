The School of Emergent Priorities spends most of its research on
trajectories that have not happened yet. This time the trajectory was a pair
of chess tables, moved on a Horizon Register item's working assumption that
a more visible site would draw more use.

Facilities relocated the University's two outdoor chess tables 34 metres
across the Learning Commons courtyard, from a shaded corner to a spot
fronting its busiest walkway, piloting a sightline-led siting doctrine it
may extend further. A new paper counts play at the tables nine weeks before
the move and nine after, against an unmoved comparison site, to see whether
the anticipated uptake arrived.

> The tables are seen roughly five times as often now as they were in the
> old corner. What we have not found is anyone deciding to sit down because
> of it. Visibility turned out to be necessary and nowhere near enough.
>
> --- Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, School of Emergent Priorities

Time-lapse footage let two coders log play sessions, session length, and
daily foot traffic at the relocated tables and an unmoved Arts Courtyard
comparison site. Weekly play held level before and after the move, matching
the comparison site's own unchanged rate, and session length did not shift
in either post-move period.

Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities, said the finding
sits closer to home than most Register items do. "We spend a lot of our
time asking what happens once the evidence for a priority stops arriving.
This time it never arrived, and the tables are still exactly where the
Register put them."

Facilities says it will weigh the result before extending the same
visibility-led approach further.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.rj74a6.
