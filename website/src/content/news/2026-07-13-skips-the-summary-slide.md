Slop University has published a new prospectus brochure that treats its
campus tour as a piece of the University's own research infrastructure, not a
marketing exercise separate from it. The tour, the brochure argues, should
show a visitor exactly what a researcher sees, instruments included.

The brochure, titled _The Tour That Shows Its Workings_, routes prospective
students through three stops across both Schools: the Trajectory Analytics
Group's briefing room, where the School of Emergent Priorities' Horizon
Register scrolls live; the School of Continuous Improvement's Adaptive
Metrics Lab, where the Living Dashboard occupies most of one wall; and Demo
Quarter, the quarterly showcase where a Capability Sprints cohort defends a
term's work to whoever turns up.

> Most of what a university shows a visitor is finished --- a photograph of a
> building, a number already rounded off. We'd rather a visitor left with a
> question about the Register than a slogan about it.
>
> --- Professor Verity Marris, Director, Trajectory Analytics Group, School of
> Emergent Priorities

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, who leads the Adaptive Metrics Lab, said
opening the Living Dashboard to visitors had taught the Lab as much about its
own instrument as any internal review: "people ask the button question the
Lab always meant to ask itself."

The brochure draws on the University's own published record to make its
case: a validation study testing whether the Horizon Register's early alerts
predict funding outcomes, and an audit of whether the Living Dashboard kept
the boundary its companion wellbeing tool was promised. Both pieces, the
brochure notes, examine the same instruments a visitor is invited to watch
running.

The full brochure is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.hv917f.
