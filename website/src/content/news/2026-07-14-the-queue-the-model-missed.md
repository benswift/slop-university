The intercampus shuttle's arrival-forecasting model has one blunt power:
when its sensors log a stop as empty, it tells the driver to keep driving. A
new research poster from the School of Emergent Priorities finds drivers are
increasingly declining the instruction.

> A model can be right about a sensor reading and still be wrong about a
> stop. We wanted to know how often a driver's own glance out the
> windscreen was catching the difference.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register, School
> of Emergent Priorities

Dr Okoro and Dr Ingrid Vasseur reviewed eight weeks of dispatch logs across
both shuttle loops, checking every scheduled skip call against what actually
happened at the kerb, and followed up with structured post-shift interviews
across the driver roster to establish what each override was actually
responding to. Drivers overrode the model on 78 of 210 reviewed stops, most
often citing a queue the model's own sensors had already logged as empty.

"The reasons didn't scatter the way we expected," said Dr Vasseur. "They
sorted themselves by the timetable, not by who was driving."

The Horizon Register is filing the result alongside its wider watch on
where a forecast's confidence and what's actually waiting at the kerb part
ways.

The full research poster is available from the University's research
repository under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.in4uxg.
