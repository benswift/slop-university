A poster's QR code has told the Societies Space and Funding Panel how many
people are coming to a student-society event since last semester, and the
figure now carries weight beyond the poster it's printed on: it feeds
directly into which societies keep, gain, or lose a room-allocation and
funding tier. A new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities asks how
closely that scan count tracks who actually walked through the door.

> We were not trying to catch anyone out. We wanted to know whether the
> number the Panel already trusts was worth trusting, and the only way to
> find out was to ask the people the poster went up for.
>
> --- Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor, Futures in Committee, School
> of Emergent Priorities

Researchers interviewed 28 event organisers across 41 events run by 19
societies over one semester, then checked scan logs against organisers' own
door tallies for the 34 events that kept one. The scan count ran a median
2.4 times higher than the door tally, and the gap was widest among
organisers who said they knew the figure was tied to their society's
standing.

"Once organisers understand what the number decides, the number stops being
a record of the event and starts becoming part of how the event is run,"
said Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities,
the paper's co-author.

The Panel says it will weigh the finding alongside its existing formula
when societies next come up for a tier review, rather than treat one
semester's audit as reason to redesign the formula outright.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.b0njfs.
