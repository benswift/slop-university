The pedestrian-crossing button outside the Learning Commons gets pressed
several thousand times a year, and the University's Traffic Advisory
Committee treats every one of those presses as a measure of unmet demand ---
the leading figure in its case for a second crossing further down the same
road.

Ingrid Vasseur and Renke Sabel installed an independent recorder inside the
signal controller's housing and logged fourteen weeks of button presses
against the controller's own phase-change transitions, matching each press
to any signal change inside a ±90-second window. A kerb-mounted infrared
counter held pedestrian volume constant as a covariate across the same
period.

> A committee that wants a number to mean demand will read a button press as
> demand, whatever the button is actually connected to.
>
> --- Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor, Futures in Committee

Only 5.8 per cent of the 6,214 logged presses preceded a phase change inside
the matching window, a rate consistent with the intersection's own cycle
length rather than any response to the press; weekly press volume and weekly
phase-change counts showed no meaningful association across the full term (r
= 0.06).

"The tally was never lying about how many times the button gets pressed,"
said Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel, a co-author. "It just never had anything
to do with the signal, and nobody had matched the two records before."

The University places the finding alongside a run of work from across its
schools examining what a routinely cited figure actually tracks once it is
checked against the apparatus it is assumed to describe. The Traffic
Advisory Committee's business case has not been revised.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.15vtzx.
