A jumper draped over a picnic-table bench outside the library looks, to
Facilities' quarterly walkthrough, exactly like an empty table --- until
whoever left it there comes back. A new research poster from the School of
Continuous Improvement spent a term testing how often that return happens,
and how rarely the walkthrough is there to see it.

> A tally that only counts a body in a seat will always read a claimed table
> as free capacity. We wanted a count that noticed the jumper too.
>
> --- Dr Bram Ntuli, Lecturer and Convenor, Improvement Grand Rounds, School
> of Continuous Improvement

The poster tracks fourteen picnic-table clusters outside the library and
refectory across a full term, coding each table's informal claim signals
against continuous time-lapse footage and against the timing of Facilities'
own walkthrough. A draped jumper or a laptop left mid-charge predicted the
claimant's return within ten minutes in the large majority of sightings the
footage caught; the walkthrough's tally logged the same tables as "in use"
only rarely.

"The tally was never asked to notice a jumper," said Dr Thandiwe Solberg,
Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter, the poster's lead author. "It
was only ever asked to notice a body, and a body is the part of the claim
that's hardest to find at the exact minute someone walks past."

Facilities has not said whether a future utilisation review will fold
informal claim signals into its count; the University reads the finding as
a reminder that a measure built for one purpose rarely notices what a
purpose-adjacent glance would catch.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zkfjv5.
