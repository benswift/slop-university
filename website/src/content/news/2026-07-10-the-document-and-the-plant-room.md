The Shared Comfort Charter is unambiguous on the point. Each shared-office zone
holds a local setpoint, and the people who work in that zone hold the right to
adjust it. A new poster from the School of Continuous Improvement went to find
out what that right amounts to once the building is running.

Dr Thandiwe Solberg and Associate Professor Casimir Beng surveyed 186 staff
across the University's six shared-office zones, asking each respondent to
estimate how much of the working day their zone's own thermostat setting
actually governs. They then obtained six months of override events from
Facilities' central energy panel, matched zone by zone to the same occupied
hours, and coded the Charter's control language clause by clause against both
records.

Estimates clustered high and tight, between two-thirds and three-quarters of
occupied hours. The panel's log, over the same zones and the same period, put
the figure an order of magnitude lower. Not one respondent reported having been
told an override had occurred.

> Nobody in this study was misinformed. They read the document that governs
> their zone and they answered accordingly. The gap we found sits between that
> document and the plant room, and every person we surveyed was standing on the
> document's side of it.
>
> --- Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter

The University regards an entitlement it grants itself as no less answerable to
the evidence than one it grants anyone else.

"The panel is doing precisely what it was commissioned to do, and doing it
well," said Associate Professor Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "The
question the poster leaves with us is which of these two records a zone should
be able to consult about its own afternoon. I don't think we have put it in
those terms before."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.x20kf7.
