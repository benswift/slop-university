Every wall-mounted defibrillator on campus carries a small QR tag, and once
a month someone from Facilities walks past with a handheld reader and scans
it. That scan is most of what stands behind the number in the University's
annual safety report: AED Fleet Readiness, currently 96.2 per cent. A new
paper from the School of Continuous Improvement asks what else, if
anything, the figure is counting.

> Ninety-six per cent is a good headline number precisely because it
> doesn't move. That's not nothing, but it's not the same thing as knowing
> every cabinet would work.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel, Convenor, Indicator Commons

The paper, co-authored with Associate Professor Casimir Beng and Research
Fellow Osei Vandermeer, continues the School's practice of turning its own
instruments back on themselves. The team matched a semester of the
ledger's monthly scan records against the separate procurement table that
tracks each cabinet's pad-expiry status, then recalculated the published
readiness figure under two alternative formulas: one that drops the
pad-expiry term altogether, and one that doubles its weight. Both
alternatives landed within a percentage point of the figure the University
currently reports.

Associate Professor Beng said the finding "sits alongside a run of similar
results from across the School's instrumentation work, and the consistency
itself is starting to become the more interesting pattern." Facilities has
indicated it has no plans to revisit the current weighting, noting the
figure has held steady across several reporting cycles.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.wfyo68.
