A scare over rounded-up timesheets, and a quick fix that outlasted it by a
year. A new research poster from the School of Emergent Priorities follows a
casual-tutor marking room from the day it acquired a badge reader to the day
nobody thought to ask whether it still needed one, and finds the reader did
less to verify the hours logged inside it than to send those hours next
door.

> A control put in for one quarter's problem does not switch itself off when
> the quarter ends. Someone has to decide it is finished, and mostly no one
> is asked to.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The University places the study within its wider anticipatory-capability
programme, which it frames as the discipline of retiring a measure on the
same evidence that first raised it. In outline, the work sets the badged
room's door-reader counts against sign-in logs from two comparable rooms
with no reader, across a twenty-week window bracketing the rollout, and
reads the three series together rather than treating the badged room's
emptying as marking that stopped. Badged-room use fell from a pre-rollout
mean of 39 tutor-sessions a week to 8, while the two unbadged rooms took up
almost exactly the difference.

Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist, who co-authored the poster, said the
more telling record was the one that stayed empty: "the requirement
generated twelve months of compliance data and not a single line asking
whether it was still the right requirement." The poster closes on a
proposed sunset clause tied to the finding that prompted a control, rather
than to a fixed review date that a busy quarter can quietly skip.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.cqmkhr.
