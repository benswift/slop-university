A car-share return checklist asks five questions every time a vehicle changes
hands: fuel, exterior condition, cleanliness, the child seat, the toll tag. A
new paper from the School of Emergent Priorities and the School of Continuous
Improvement set out to learn which of those five questions renters actually
looked at when answering, and which they answered from memory. Working from a
fleet's own screen-interaction logs rather than from renters' say-so, the
researchers built a four-level taxonomy of verification --- measured,
glanced-at, remembered, or skipped --- and matched it against 1,842 returns.

The findings sit easily inside the University's growing catalogue of
instruments that keep a perfect record of something other than the thing they
were built to record. Fuel level, easy to check and costly to miss, gets
confirmed against the gauge in most returns. The toll transponder, whose
absence turns up on a bill weeks later if it turns up at all, is mostly
ticked from memory --- and more so the closer a renter gets to the fee that's
trying to hurry them along.

> A checklist field marked complete has always meant two different things,
> and a return deadline is a very efficient way of finding out which one
> you're looking at.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke, Convenor of the Strategic Drift Survey

A photo-capture step the fleet introduced for the transponder field bore that
out: it lifted verification comfortably outside the fee window and did almost
nothing inside it. "The step still gets performed," said Associate Professor
Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "What changes is whether
anyone's looking at what the photo shows."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.qyze4p.
