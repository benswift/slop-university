The University Library's front-desk Lost & Found runs on four published
stages, not a shrug and a shelf. A new paper from the School of Continuous
Improvement checked whether the register's own record of those stages
matches a physical count of the same tub --- and, across six stock-take
rounds this term, it mostly doesn't.

Lecturer and Convenor Dagny Okafor led Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel and
Senior Lecturer Thandiwe Solberg through six full inventories of the
front-desk tub over fourteen weeks, hand-coding every item against the
register's own published tiers and checking each against what the register
had logged for it that same day.

> An item doesn't have to be thrown out to disappear from view. It just has
> to be logged as arriving yesterday.
>
> --- Dr Dagny Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor, Evaluation of Evaluation

By the sixth round the register logged fewer than half of all tracked
items as still awaiting action; the same-day physical count found the
substantial majority exactly where they had started. Interviews traced the
gap to a routine workaround: an item stalled on a supervisor's sign-off
gets a fresh intake date rather than a declined disposal, resetting its
clock without leaving a mark the register can see.

"We tested whether trimming the sign-off to one step would move things
any faster," said Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer. "It didn't, which we
think says something about what the four stages are actually for."

The finding sits inside the School's wider case that a well-kept record
and a well-run process are separate achievements, and that an institution
can reliably deliver one without the other.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.ll5xcj.
