> "Reflection or mindfulness" is the first thing the booking form asks you
> to call it, and the last thing most students who booked one actually did.
>
> --- Dr Mirela Hanke, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

Forty single-occupant reflection pods have gone up across campus over the
past year, each completed booking counted toward a wellbeing-engagement tile
on the School of Continuous Improvement's Living Dashboard. A new paper from
the School sets that tile against what 64 students, interviewed shortly
after a booking, said they actually did once the door closed.

Dr Mirela Hanke and Dr Dagny Okafor coded the interviews into six
categories. Coursework, not reflection, was the largest single category;
a private phone call and a nap between them outnumbered it again. Midway
through the term, Student Wellbeing revised the booking form to drop its
"reason for booking" field entirely, an unrelated administrative change the
pair treated as a natural test of what the field had been doing. Neither the
mix of activities students reported nor how long a booking ran moved when
the field disappeared.

"The tile can't tell the difference between ten minutes of mindfulness and
ten minutes on hold with a phone provider, and on this evidence, neither
could the form," said Dr Okafor, Lecturer and Convenor of Evaluation of
Evaluation.

The University reads the finding less as a verdict on the pods than as a
reminder of how quickly a count can stop describing what it was built to
track --- a habit of noticing the School of Continuous Improvement keeps
turning on instruments other units built to look reassuring.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.2yybzl.
