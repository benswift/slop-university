> An item used to wait for someone with a free afternoon. Now it waits for a
> date the system already set for it at the door.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow in the School of Emergent Priorities

That system, the Lost Property Ledger, has run at six of the University's
staffed collection points for fourteen months, replacing the paper logbook that
used to sit behind the library desk and its counterparts across campus. A new
paper from Dr Mensah, Associate Professor Casimir Beng, and Dr Mirela Hanke
reports what changed once an item's fate stopped waiting on a reviewer's spare
hour: a photograph and a short description at intake now produce a
reclaim-probability forecast, a retention tier, and a disposal date, without
anyone drafting a decision.

Reclaim rates rose at five of the six collection points, and a backlog that had
nearly quadrupled in the fourteen months before rollout levelled off rather than
continuing to climb. A checkpoint letting staff reopen and amend any forecast
before its scheduled date saw heavy use — opened on a third of eligible items —
but changed the tier in barely one case in forty of those openings, a gap the
paper's authors read as worth a closer look in its own right rather than a flaw
to correct. The School treats a finding this modest in scale, and this
unglamorous in subject, as no smaller a return on the study than a dramatic one
would have been.

"We built the checkpoint assuming it would be exercised," said Associate
Professor Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "What we got instead was a
number worth reporting on: how often a step gets opened, against how often it
does anything once it is."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.7682cb.
