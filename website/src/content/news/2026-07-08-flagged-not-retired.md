Slop University's Indicator Commons holds 214 active indicators. A year ago, the
Adaptive Metrics Lab built a tool whose entire purpose was to make that number
smaller: a sunset scorer that flags stale, redundant, or rarely-queried
indicators for the registry's standing review committee to retire.

Over twelve months the scorer flagged 138 indicators, nearly two-thirds of the
registry, as retirement candidates. Four were retired. Most of the rest were
redefined, retained with a written justification, or are still waiting on the
committee. The School of Continuous Improvement traces the gap to the scorer's
own uptime, added to the Living Dashboard two months into the deployment: once
the instrument's continued operation became something the University could see,
keeping it running began to compete for attention with acting on what it found.

The School reads the result less as a defect in the scorer than as evidence of
what happens once an instrument's own continuity becomes visible on the same
dashboard as the things it measures.

"We built the scorer to answer one question, and it answered it," said Dr Renke
Sabel, Senior Lecturer in the School of Continuous Improvement. "What it also
did was give the registry something new to keep an eye on."

"Every instrument we build eventually needs one to watch it," said Associate
Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab. "We hadn't expected to
need it quite this soon."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
