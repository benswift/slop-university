A smart monitor doesn't just guess why a baby is crying anymore --- it
grades its own guess, and posts the mark next to the cry. A new Slop
University research poster went looking for what that mark is actually
worth.

Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke, Convenor of the Strategic Drift Survey, and
Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran followed 68 families running a commercial
cry-classification monitor for fourteen weeks, logging each cry's
on-device label against the soothing action a caregiver later found
actually worked, without letting the caregiver see the monitor's own guess
first. The monitor's confidence score was tracked separately against both
the label's correctness and the cry's raw loudness.

The label matched what actually worked in roughly half of all logged
cries, a share that held flat across the full fourteen weeks regardless of
how many cries a household had by then logged. The confidence score moved
with the second measure far more readily than the first: a loud cry
reliably produced a confident label, correct or not.

> A confident monitor and a correct one turned out to be answering two
> different questions. Ours was only ever built to grade the first.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke, Convenor, Strategic Drift Survey, School
> of Emergent Priorities

"Families were remarkably patient generating the ground truth we needed,"
said Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran. "None of them thought they were
testing the score. They just wanted the crying to stop."

The School of Emergent Priorities counts the finding among the more
useful ones this year for what it declines to claim.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.mjo541.
