Every residence hall's laundry room already runs on the same booking app,
which until this term did one job: show which machine was free. This term,
four halls gave it a second job — a two-bookings-per-resident weekly cap,
introduced on the same date without a single machine added.

A new research poster from the School of Continuous Improvement, _Freed by
the Cap, Refilled by the Cohort_, sets the cap's own booking-app timestamps
against five weeks either side of its introduction, split by a detail the
cap's dashboard doesn't track: two of the four halls spent the term
absorbing residents displaced by a delayed dorm closure elsewhere.

> A cap is easy to read as a fix once you stop asking who else showed up
> this term.
>
> --- Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor of Demo Quarter,
> School of Continuous Improvement

In the two halls whose population held steady, the median wait for a free
washer nearly halved once the cap took effect. In the two halls carrying
this year's overflow, the cap produced barely a tenth of that improvement,
a gap the researchers trace not to the cap failing but to the freed slot
reaching a longer queue before anyone got to feel it.

"The number the cap gets judged against is the wait time averaged across
all four halls," said Dr Bram Ntuli, Lecturer and Convenor of Improvement
Grand Rounds. "That number improved. It improved for one campus's worth of
residents and barely moved for another's."

For the School of Continuous Improvement, the finding fits a broader
pattern this term: an instrument's headline number is rarely wrong so much
as silent about which population it was averaged across.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.6k7xyr.
