Nineteen warehouses, one shared piece of kit, and a school with a standing
interest in what a proxy measure quietly comes to stand for: Slop
University's newest research poster puts a number on how well a hi-vis vest
tier --- introduced so sensor-guided machinery could tell a trainee from a
time-served picker --- tracks anything a worker actually feels.

Researchers surveyed 236 rostered floor staff exiting their shift across the
nineteen sites over fourteen weeks, asking each to rate how safe the floor
felt that day, then set the answers against the worker's assigned vest tier
and their rostered tenure.

> A tier built to help a robot find you sits a lot closer to a badge than an
> instrument was ever the goal.
>
> --- Lecturer Marit Osayande, Convenor of the Living Dashboard

Self-reported safety tracked how long a person had been on the floor far
more reliably than which vest they were wearing. Tenure alone predicted a
worker's safety rating about as well as tier and tenure combined, and vest
tier alone barely predicted anything at all.

"The machinery got exactly what it needed," said Dr Sten Okwuosa, Senior
Research Fellow. "What's less clear is what everyone standing next to it got
out of the same three colours."

The School of Continuous Improvement reads the result as one more entry in
its account of instruments that start out measuring one thing and end up
worn as another. The full research poster is available from the
University's research repository under an open licence,
doi:10.5555/slop.ghl47s.
