Somewhere on campus, a numbered stall sits reserved for a rider who hasn't
scanned a tag against it in months. A new research poster from the School of
Continuous Improvement set out to ask Facilities staff, and the riders
themselves, why the reservation was never handed back. The project extends
the School's running interest in the permissions an institution forgets to
retire, this time applied to a stall rather than a dashboard entry.

Researchers interviewed thirty-four people --- twenty-two riders with no
recorded scan in the past four months, and twelve Facilities and Security
staff --- and sorted their accounts into four recurring, overlapping
categories: inertia built into the reservation's own design, a lingering
attachment to the claim, a gap in how departures are processed, and a tag
quietly passed on without ever being re-registered. Matched against the full
eighteen-month stall-tag register, the same ordering held: tags attributed to
design inertia cleared in a median of forty-eight days once flagged, while
those passed on informally took a median of 335 days to surface at all.

> A tag doesn't need anyone to keep believing in it. It only needs nobody to
> take it back.
>
> --- Dr Renke Sabel, Senior Lecturer, School of Continuous Improvement

"The four categories aren't a ranking of who's at fault," said Dr Bram Ntuli,
Lecturer and Convenor of Improvement Grand Rounds. "They're mostly about what
the reservation was ever asked to do once the riding stopped."

The poster proposes trialling a release prompt tied to ID-card deactivation,
with a sixty-day holding class before a stall returns to the pool. The full
poster is available from the University's research repository under an open
licence, doi:10.5555/slop.etwfmk.
