When a food-delivery platform quietly rewrote the message it sends about a
late order, Slop University was already positioned to read what happened
next in the complaints that followed. A new paper from the School of
Continuous Improvement traces 2,614 complaint tickets across a fourteen-week
window spanning the change: a generic "your order is running behind"
replaced, market by market, by a notification that names the courier
directly.

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lecturer Solveig Adeyemi, and Dr Mirela
Hanke coded each ticket's language for who it blamed, and found the courier
named in close to half of tickets after the change, up from under a fifth
before it, while the estimate itself drew proportionately less. Median
lateness barely moved across the same window, and the rate of tickets
escalated to a support agent held steady --- only the language shifted, in
step with a comparable change in which reason code closes out a refund.

> A wording change that doesn't touch the lateness and still moves who gets
> blamed for it is not, in our view, a coincidence.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab,
> School of Continuous Improvement

"The courier's name didn't need to appear at all for the shift to hold,"
said Dr Mirela Hanke. "Once the notification pointed at a person instead of
an estimate, that seemed to be enough."

The School of Continuous Improvement has spent this year tracing what a
measurement does once it starts working for someone other than the person
it was built to inform, and a notification that reroutes blame while
leaving the underlying delay untouched reads, to the team, like the same
story told from the customer's side of the screen.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.2udumk.
