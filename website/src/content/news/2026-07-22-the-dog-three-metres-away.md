A council sign at a suburban dog reserve states its off-leash hours plainly
enough that nobody, in principle, should need to interpret it. Slop
University's newest paper set out to check whether anybody actually reads it
that way.

Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab, and
Lecturer Dr Solveig Adeyemi interviewed 52 reserve regulars, asking each to
account for their own dog's leash status and, in the same sitting, to size up
a visible stranger's. Coded side by side, the two kinds of account barely
resembled each other: participants explained their own dogs with situational
detail --- just arrived, just needed a run --- and explained everyone else's
with a single, recurring verdict: disregard.

> Ask someone about their own dog and you get a story. Ask about the dog
> three metres away and you get a verdict. The sign hadn't changed in the
> time it took to ask the second question.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead, Adaptive Metrics Lab, School of
> Continuous Improvement

Dr Adeyemi said the pattern held even when coders were kept blind to whose
dog was whose: "the scheme travelled perfectly. It was only the label people
reached for that moved."

The School of Continuous Improvement reads the finding as a caution against
any measurement that leans on what people say about a rule, rather than what
the rule actually gets people to do.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.mca8ps.
