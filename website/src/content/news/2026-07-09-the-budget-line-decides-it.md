Every cohort of the Master of Priority Studies ends the same way: a capstone
pitch, a five-tier priority rank from the sponsoring panel, and a
recommendation released into the University to make of what it will. A new
paper from the School of Emergent Priorities asked alumni what became of
that rank once the room emptied.

Dr Ingrid Vasseur and Professor Verity Marris surveyed 151 alumni from six
capstone cohorts, a 67 percent response among those the program could still
reach, and set each recommendation's panel tier against whether the
sponsoring unit already held a matching budget line at the time of the
pitch. Tier carried no detectable relationship to whether a recommendation
was later implemented; a pre-existing budget line did, closely, with
implementation running to roughly three in four recommendations pitched
against an existing line and about one in four without.

The School counts its own capstone panel among the instruments it studies,
and holds its ranking to the same account it asks of every horizon-scan
recommendation the School puts its name to.

"A panel ranking gives a room something to agree on before it disperses,"
said Dr Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor of Futures in Committee. "What our
alumni keep telling us is that the agreement in the room was never the thing
that moved afterwards."

"The rank was never wrong, exactly," said Professor Marris, Director of the
Trajectory Analytics Group. "It just wasn't answering the question everyone
assumed it was."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.aaxt6q.
