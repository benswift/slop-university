Twice a day for fourteen weeks, a member of the School of Continuous
Improvement walked the Library foyer's book-swap shelf and counted what was
on it. The shelf's own count of itself --- a QR sticker, scanned in and
scanned out --- was, by the same measure, barely trying.

The shelf lets anyone leave a spare textbook and take one home, and
Facilities has tracked its use since launch through a scan-in, scan-out
dashboard --- the figure a pending utilisation review now cites as evidence
of a program in decline. A new research poster from the School of Continuous
Improvement, _Off the Books, Not Off the Shelf_, sets that dashboard against
196 physical shelf counts across the same term, and finds the shelf itself
unbothered: turnover held to a steady median of 33.5 books a week, while the
scanned figure fell from 61% of that turnover in week one to 9% by week
fourteen.

> A tracker that goes quiet doesn't mean the thing it was watching went
> quiet too. It usually means the tracker did.
>
> --- Dr Thandiwe Solberg, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Demo Quarter, School
> of Continuous Improvement

The team paired the scan log against the physical counts, logging each
shelf-edge sticker's condition at every visit. More turned up re-stuck,
taped over, or missing as the term wore on --- a gap the dashboard has no way
to register, and one the poster argues accounts for the whole decline. "The
books were never off the books. They were just off the log," said Dr Sten
Okwuosa, Senior Research Fellow and Convenor, Impact Pathway Atlas, who
called the mismatch "the kind of gap the Atlas exists to chart."

The full poster is available from the University's repository under an open
licence, doi:10.5555/slop.zlggzm.
