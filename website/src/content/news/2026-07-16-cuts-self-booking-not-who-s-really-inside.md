> A cooldown only slows down the card. It doesn't slow down the friend
> standing next to its owner, holding a key of their own.
>
> --- Dr Joost Nwosu, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Continuous Improvement

The University's six shared piano-practice rooms have run on the same
terminal since a scheduling review three years ago: tap a student card, hold
the room for up to ninety minutes. This term the terminal gained a rule of
its own — a forty-five-minute cooldown before the same card can book again —
after Facilities logged students holding a room past their slot by
rebooking themselves the moment their session ended.

A new research poster from the School of Continuous Improvement, _Passed to
a Friend, Not Freed_, audits what the cooldown actually changed. Researchers
logged eight weeks of terminal bookings across all six rooms, tracking every
blocked rebooking attempt and whatever booking followed it in the minutes
after.

Same-card rebooking fell from 41% of slots to 6% within three weeks of the
cooldown's introduction — by that measure alone, a clean fix. But the share
of slots going to a genuinely new booker barely moved, from 58% to 61%
across the full eight weeks. Eighty-eight per cent of blocked rebooking
attempts were followed within minutes by a booking from the same student's
declared usual practice partner.

"The room changed hands on the terminal's own ledger," said Dr Bram Ntuli,
Lecturer and Convenor of Improvement Grand Rounds. "Whether it changed hands
in the room is still the Grand Rounds' next agenda item."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.bc4ruu.
