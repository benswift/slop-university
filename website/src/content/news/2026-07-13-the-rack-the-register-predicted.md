When the School of Emergent Priorities' Horizon Register flagged rising
bike-commute sign-ups in the West Wing precinct, Facilities read the trend as
a cue: install covered parking before the demand arrives. A new paper checks
whether it did.

> The Register's job was to protect a bank of parking against a rising
> count. Where cyclists actually park turns out to say more about the
> building they're headed to than about the count that put the parking
> there.
>
> --- Dr Fenna Okoro, Senior Lecturer and Convenor, Horizon Register

Fenna Okoro and Anneke Tolan tracked occupancy at the new, forecast-sited
West Wing bank and an older, unforecast bank outside the Library across a
full teaching term, and interviewed 42 cyclists parking at either about why
they had chosen that spot. The forecast-sited bank held a fraction of its
capacity throughout; the older bank ran over capacity most weeks. Coded
against the interviews, a simple measure the Register's model never
consults --- walking distance to a cyclist's actual destination --- tracked
occupancy far more closely than the precinct forecast that sited the bank
in the first place.

"Cyclists were never confused about why they park where they do," said Dr
Tolan. "The interesting question was always why the instrument built to
anticipate them didn't ask."

The University reads the gap as exactly the kind of finding its own
anticipatory instruments exist to surface, and says the Register's siting
method will be reviewed alongside the paper's recommendations.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.2e6ud1.
