For nine months, researchers from the School of Emergent Priorities
photographed handwritten notes left inside neighbourhood street-library
boxes, coding hundreds of exchanges between strangers who never meet, to
ask a single question: how does one of these correspondences actually end?

A new paper answers with a four-category taxonomy, built from 341 threads
across 74 boxes: a note that signs itself off, one that simply stops, one
still addressed to a visitor who has quietly stopped answering, and one
taken from the box before any ending is legible in place. Dr Marek
Solheim, Dr Anneke Tolan, and Dr Anouk Mensah found the second category,
the quiet stop, dominates the corpus overall, though it swaps places with
a proper sign-off once a box sits on a quiet residential street rather
than a busy corner.

> Not every correspondence needs an ending assigned to it by somebody with
> a taxonomy. We're comfortable if this one raises more of those questions
> than it answers.
>
> --- Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

The team went a step further, training a model to guess how a thread will
end from nothing but its opening note. It manages a modest improvement
over always guessing the most common outcome, an improvement Dr Mensah
said lands almost entirely on the threads addressed to someone who has
stopped answering. "An opening line can tell you a correspondent expects
to be waited on," she said. "It has much less to say about whether anyone
actually says goodbye."

The project is the latest entry in a School-wide effort to work out which
endings an institution can see coming, and which it only recognises once
they are already over.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.50tmyv.
