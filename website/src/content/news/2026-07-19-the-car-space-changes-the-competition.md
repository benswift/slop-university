Attach a single reserved car space to an office tipping competition's
leaderboard, and the leaderboard stops being just a leaderboard. A new
paper from the School of Continuous Improvement followed nine workplace
footy-tipping competitions through a season to find out what changes once
a real reward sits behind the ranking.

> This sits alongside everything else the Adaptive Metrics Lab has traced
> about a measure and the behaviour that builds up around it. The car
> space is one of the smallest stakes we have found so far.
>
> --- Associate Professor Casimir Beng, Lead of the Adaptive Metrics Lab

Dr Beng, Dr Renke Sabel and Dr Marit Osayande compared five competitions
that tie their confidence-weighted leaderboard to a reserved car space
against four that publish the same leaderboard with nothing attached. Two
coders read every submission's timing, confidence choices and, where a
competition ran one, its internal chat thread, sorting what they found
into five recurring workarounds, from padding a sure thing's confidence
score to colleagues quietly splitting their picks to cover the perk
between them. The pattern held all season and grew more pronounced as the
fixture list narrowed toward the finals.

"The obvious next question is what happens once the car space is taken
away again," said Marit Osayande, Lecturer and Convenor of the Living
Dashboard. "We don't yet have a competition where we've watched that
happen."

A retrospective check, recomputing each round's leader under a simple
win-count instead of the platform's confidence-weighted formula, changed
almost nothing about how often the five workarounds turned up. The
University reads that as the more useful half of the finding: the
leaderboard's arithmetic is doing far less work than the reward behind it.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.cm2xn5.
