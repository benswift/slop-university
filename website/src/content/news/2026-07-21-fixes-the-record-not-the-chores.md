Nine weeks after a household adds a formal appeals process to its chore
wheel, the wheel finally agrees with what actually happened around seven
times in eight. Whether more chores got done in that stretch turns out to
be a separate question, and a new Slop University paper answers it
separately too.

Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke, Convenor of the Strategic Drift Survey,
together with Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran and Associate Professor Kwame
Lindqvist, tracked 76 households already running an active chore wheel
across eighteen weeks, logging who the wheel named for each week's dishes,
bins, floors, and pet-care turn against who an independent household record
showed actually did it, before and after the households added a slip
letting anyone formally contest a disputed turn.

Agreement between the wheel and the household's own record climbed from
three turns in five before the appeals process to roughly seven in eight
after it, while the share of chores actually completed each week barely
moved. Most of the appeals households filed, once they had the option,
named the same one chore.

> A wheel that gets edited to match what happened is not the same as a
> wheel that predicted it. We spent eighteen weeks making sure we knew
> which one we were looking at.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Runa Adegoke, Convenor, Strategic Drift Survey, School
> of Emergent Priorities

"Households don't usually get an appeals process of their own, so when one
shows up, it tends to get used for whatever's most annoying that week, not
whatever's most unfair," said Senior Lecturer Ronja Oyelaran.

The University counts the deployment among a growing number of studies
willing to measure an instrument's own accuracy before crediting it with
anything else.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.d7bnmg.
