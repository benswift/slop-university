Ask a farmers market queue who is behaving fairly, and the answer changes
depending on whether a rostered marshal is standing at the back of the line, a
new Slop University paper reports --- even though how the queue actually
behaves barely changes at all.

Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran and Senior Lecturer Dr Runa Adegoke, both of
the School of Emergent Priorities, set out to test whether an asymmetry the
University first identified at a suburban dog park travels to a different
queue entirely. Across twelve Saturday markets, patrons rated their own most
recent turn in line and a nearby stranger's on the same five-point scale used
in the original study, while independent field observers quietly logged who
actually queued in order. At markets without a marshal, self-ratings ran well
ahead of the ratings given to everyone else. At markets with one, the gap
closed almost completely.

> A marshal doesn't make anyone queue any better. It makes everyone a little
> more generous about how the person in front of them is queuing.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran, School of Emergent Priorities

The audit trail behind the finding is what makes it hold. Field observers
coded actual queue order at every stall, blind to what respondents had told
the survey, and their tally moved only slightly between marshalled and
unmarshalled markets --- nowhere near enough to explain why the judgement gap
all but vanished on its own.

Dr Adegoke, who convenes the School's Strategic Drift Survey, said the result
"gives the University one more reason to check what a self-report instrument
is actually measuring before it retires the observer who could check it
directly."

The School of Emergent Priorities treats the result as a data point in its own
ongoing account of when a self-report instrument can be trusted to stand in
for direct observation, and when it can't.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.4heaxe.
