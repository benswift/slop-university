A missing-dog flyer does not know when to come down. A new Slop University
research poster puts a number on how long after it should.

Working across fourteen suburb Facebook groups and two councils'
microchip-scan intake records over eleven months, researchers from the
School of Continuous Improvement tracked what happened to a "still at
large" alert once the animal it named had already been scanned home. The
poster extends the School's running interest in what a record keeps saying
once the thing it recorded has moved on.

> An alert that reunites a family is doing exactly what it was built for.
> The interesting part is what it keeps doing after that.
>
> --- Lecturer and Convenor Dr Marit Osayande, School of Continuous
> Improvement

Alerts in unmoderated groups held their "still at large" tag for a median
of eleven days past the pound's own return record; a mandatory resolved-tag
rule cut that to under six, without closing the gap entirely. The effect
tracked engagement more closely than group size --- a widely shared post
outlived a quiet one by close to double, regardless of which rule its group
enforced.

"However a group moderates itself, the alerts people actually forward are
the ones that stay wrong longest," said Dr Ingeborg Nwachukwu, welcoming
the pattern as a question for the University's wider work on informal
record-keeping.

The findings sit alongside the School's ongoing audits of formal registers
that outlast the conditions they were built to track, and add a
community-run instrument to that record.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.1z3731.
