Every visitor to a network of regional swimming pools already passes
through a turnstile that logs the exact minute they arrive. For years, most
of the same visitors also signed a paper ledger at reception on the way
past --- a second record of the same fact, kept in case the first one
wasn't enough. A new Slop University paper from the School of Continuous
Improvement followed what happened once individual pool managers, on their
own timetables, let that second record go.

> An indicator earns its keep by being consulted, not by being kept; ours
> had stopped being consulted long before anyone thought to ask.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel, Convenor, Indicator Commons, School of
> Continuous Improvement

Lecturer Ingeborg Nwachukwu and Senior Lecturer Renke Sabel compared eleven
pool centres that kept the ledger against thirteen that dropped it over a
fourteen-month window, tracking how completely incident reports were filed
and how quickly a disputed record was resolved. Neither measure moved.
What did move was how confident reception and duty staff felt that an
incident could still be reconstructed from the centre's own records ---
confidence that held where the ledger stayed and slipped where it didn't,
even after a printed summary of the same electronic log went up on the
staff noticeboard in its place.

"The confidence dip doesn't surprise me," said Lecturer Ingeborg Nwachukwu.
"It's the same thing every cohort we follow teaches us: a habit outlasts
the reason for it, right up until something removes the last visible sign
it mattered."

The School of Continuous Improvement counts the finding among the clearer
instances this year of a record mattering more to the people who keep it
than to the process it was meant to protect.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.a1hnm0.
