Slop University doesn't often ask whether one of its own findings holds up
somewhere else. A new paper reports what happened when it did: a mechanism
that fixed a household's chore-wheel paperwork without touching the chore
itself was rebuilt for an apartment block's working bee, and this time it
left the paperwork alone.

Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran and Associate Professor Kwame Lindqvist,
both of the School of Emergent Priorities, working with Lecturer Dr Marit
Osayande of the School of Continuous Improvement, fitted 24 apartment
buildings' working bees with a public check-off board, standing in for the
appeals process an earlier household study had credited with tightening its
own record. Eighteen matched buildings kept circulating their task list
privately, by committee email, as before.

> We built the board expecting to see the same thing twice. Instead we
> learned what a household's finding was quietly borrowing from the people
> living in it.
>
> --- Senior Lecturer Dr Ronja Oyelaran, School of Emergent Priorities

Across four working-bee cycles, the board left the gap between a tick and
an independent facilities audit unchanged in both arms. What moved instead
was the audited work itself: buildings running the public board finished
more of it, a margin that widened as the deployment went on.

"A household already has somewhere to raise a complaint about a chore," said
Dr Osayande. "A building mostly doesn't. Ours just started supplying an
audience."

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.we5s9m.
