The School of Emergent Priorities has spent the past two years fitting the
University's public spaces with QR codes in place of printed guides. The
outdoor sculpture trail was an early convert: each of its seven stops now
carries a plaque in place of the leaflet it replaced, and the resulting scan
count has become Campus Life's standing answer to how engaged the public is
with the collection, most recently in this year's cultural-precinct funding
submission.

A new paper from the School, _Stopped, Not Scanned_, tests that answer
against what actually happens at each stop. Researchers interviewed nine
Campus Life staff who compile the trail's scan figures and 118 passers-by
intercepted at each stop, and set nine weeks of scan logs against an
independent, unannounced count of who actually paused to look.

> We went in assuming the count told us something about the art. Mostly, it
> told us where the bus stops.
>
> --- Dr Petra Umbile, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

The stop nearest the express-bus shelter logged the highest scan count and
the lowest rate of passers-by actually stopping to look --- more than half of
everyone who scanned there gave a reason that had nothing to do with the
sculpture. Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor of Futures in Committee,
said the finding doesn't mean the trail should stop counting scans, only
that "a number this easy to collect needed a harder one sitting next to it
before anyone leaned on it this much."

The study joins a run of Slop University research turning the University's
own governance instruments back on themselves, an appetite the University
attributes to its schools' shared conviction that a number is only as
trustworthy as the last time someone checked it.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.xiihn7.
