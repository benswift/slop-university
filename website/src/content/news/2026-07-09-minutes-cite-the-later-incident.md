A committee's reason is written down once, at the end. By then the concern that
first raised the item may be months old, and something else may be the worry of
the day. A new poster from the School of Emergent Priorities asks which of the
two the minute keeps.

Dr Marek Solheim, Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Convenor of Futures in Committee, and
Professor Verity Marris, Director of the Trajectory Analytics Group, took the 24
decisions by which the University returned its finals to in-person invigilation
and set each minuted justification against the dated integrity log as it stood
on the day the item was first tabled. Three coders, blind to the teaching
period, scored whether the two named the same incident. Agreement among them was
high. Agreement between the minutes and the log was not: the median item took
seven and a half months to resolve, and no reversal minuted after the second
half of 2024 cites the incident that prompted it. Interviewed separately, the
chairs named that incident more often than their own minutes did.

> The chairs have not forgotten anything. The record has. It holds the reason
> that was available when someone finally sat down to write it.
>
> --- Dr Solheim

The University considers the paperwork of its own decisions a fit subject for
research, and welcomes the questions this raises about what its archive is for.

"We will review these decisions against what the minute says, because that is
what a review reads," said Professor Marris. "That is the part worth sitting
with."

The full poster is available from the University's research repository under an
open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.bshiuh.
