A scorecard exists, in one local government area, to answer a single
question about each of its school crossings: does the crossing keep its
human supervisor, the "lollipop person" who stops traffic by hand, or does
it move to an automated pedestrian-actuated signal? The instrument is
called the Human-Preservation Protocol, and a new Slop University paper
from the School of Emergent Priorities and the School of Continuous
Improvement reports how often, across a year of real crossings scoring
themselves, it actually recommends the human stay.

Dr Iben Chikere and Dr Solveig Adeyemi tracked the protocol's own
self-scored outcomes at 142 crossings, some still staffed, some already
converted, across four quarterly waves of the rollout, without a researcher
present at any single scoring session.

> A protocol meant to protect a role and a protocol that quietly measures
> its own removal read exactly the same from the outside; the only way to
> tell them apart is to count what it actually recommends.
>
> --- Dr Iben Chikere, Lecturer, School of Emergent Priorities

Retention was recommended for roughly one crossing in sixteen, a rate that
barely shifted whether a crossing already had a supervisor or had already
lost one, and held steady across the year regardless of how familiar
respondents grew with the form.

"What surprised us wasn't the low number," said Dr Solveig Adeyemi,
Lecturer, School of Continuous Improvement. "It's how little that number
moved, no matter who was holding the pen."

The School of Emergent Priorities counts the rollout among its more
literal tests yet of what a named instrument turns out to protect once it
has to make its case, crossing by crossing.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.wx7s2x.
