> A number becomes yours the moment somebody else has weighed in on it,
> whether they agreed with you or not.
>
> --- Lecturer Iben Chikere, School of Emergent Priorities

That's the working theory behind a new Slop University research poster,
which surveyed 118 garage-sale sellers across nine street and
apartment-block group chats over one spring selling season, rating how
sure each felt about a posted price just before the chat replied and again
once it had.

Lecturer Iben Chikere and Dr Anouk Mensah tracked that swing against how
tightly each chat's replies actually agreed. It made no difference:
sellers reported high confidence in their price at close to the same rate
whether the chat converged within a few dollars or scattered across a wide
range, and a separate check found no relationship between that confidence
and how close the eventual sale landed to the number posted.

"The next version tests what happens when the chat replies with an actual
counter-offer instead of just an opinion," said Dr Anouk Mensah. "Right
now it doesn't seem to matter what gets said back, only that something
does."

For the School of Emergent Priorities, the result adds a tidy entry to its
running account of how a settled figure picks up company on its way to
becoming final, whether or not the company agreed with it.

The full poster is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.4kt8gy.
