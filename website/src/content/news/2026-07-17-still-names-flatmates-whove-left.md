> A roster that never says why it's wrong just keeps being trusted the way
> it always was.
>
> --- Dr Anouk Mensah, Postdoctoral Fellow, School of Emergent Priorities

That is the finding behind a new paper from the School of Emergent
Priorities, which audited 38 share-house chore-roster whiteboards against
each house's own tenancy records and found barely a fifth of a roster's
named owners still lived in the house by its third change of flatmates —
while the chores themselves, somehow, kept getting done.

Dr Mensah and Dr Marek Solheim, Lecturer, recruited whiteboards through
room-share and lease-transfer noticeboards across the city, cross-checking
each roster's named owner against bond-lodgement and lease-transfer records
at every turnover since the board was first drawn up. Agreement between a
name on the wall and who actually lived there fell from complete, by
definition, at drafting, to a little over one name in five by the third
turnover, with no single chore proving more likely than the others to get
corrected along the way.

"People assume nobody's redrawn it because nobody's noticed," said Dr
Solheim. "What we kept hearing instead was that everyone had noticed, and
none of them felt it was theirs to fix." Interviews across the sample found
even a household's own stated plan to redraw the board made no measurable
difference to whether it ever did.

The finding sits alongside the School's wider interest in the record a
household, or an institution, keeps of itself, and how far that record can
drift before anyone treats the drift as the thing worth fixing.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository
under an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.q4fe2g.
