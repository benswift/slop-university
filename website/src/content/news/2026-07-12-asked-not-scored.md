Slop University's community garden has never lacked demand; what it has
lacked, until now, is anyone checking whether the priority score built to
ration its plots is the thing actually doing the rationing. A new paper from
the School of Emergent Priorities puts that question to the people best
placed to answer it: the households who have already crossed the waitlist
and received a plot.

Dr Ingrid Vasseur and Dr Lindiwe Achterberg interviewed 22 of the 24
households offered a plot over the past two years, asking each, in their own
words, how they believed their turn came. Matched against each household's
position on the published Stewardship Priority Score at the time, the
answers cluster around one channel far more than any other: asking the
rostered convenor directly, in almost identical proportions whether a
household sat above or below the list's median score. A comparison of how
long each group actually waited tells the same story, with wait times the
Score exists, on paper, to keep apart landing squarely on top of each other.

> A score can be published, updated on schedule, and still not be the thing
> doing the ordering. Ours is exactly that, as far as we can tell.
>
> --- Dr Ingrid Vasseur, Lecturer and Convenor, Futures in Committee

Dr Lindiwe Achterberg, Lecturer in the School of Emergent Priorities, called
the waitlist "an arrangement that clears in practice while staying formally
undecided in principle."

The School counts the study among its more candid checks on the machinery it
also keeps running, and notes that the fortnightly sign-off the finding turns
on remains, for now, unchanged.

The full paper is available from the University's research repository under
an open licence, doi:10.5555/slop.qe42zi.
